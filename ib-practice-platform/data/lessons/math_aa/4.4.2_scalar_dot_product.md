## Scalar (Dot) Product

## Overview

This lesson explores the **scalar product** (also known as the **dot product**) of two vectors, its geometric interpretation, and its applications in calculating work, projections, and angles between vectors. We’ll focus on both algebraic and geometric understandings.

## Key Concepts

*   Understanding the definition of the scalar product.
*   Calculating the scalar product of two vectors given their components.
*   Interpreting the scalar product geometrically as the projection of one vector onto another.
*   Applying the scalar product to solve problems involving work, angles, and projections.

### Subtopic 1: Defining the Scalar Product

The **scalar product** of two vectors **a** and **b** is a scalar quantity. It’s defined as:

**a ⋅ b = |a| |b| cos θ**

Where:

*   **a ⋅ b** is the scalar product of **a** and **b**
*   |**a**| is the magnitude (length) of vector **a**
*   |**b**| is the magnitude (length) of vector **b**
*   θ is the angle between the two vectors.

> **Important:** The scalar product is *not* commutative. In general, **a ⋅ b ≠ b ⋅ a**. This is a crucial point to remember.

**Example 1:**
Let **a** = <2, 3> and **b** = <1, -1>.
|**a**| = √(2² + 3²) = √13
|**b**| = √(1² + (-1)²) = √2
θ = arccos((2 * 1 + 3 * -1) / (√13 * √2)) = arccos(-1 / √26) ≈ 1.94 radians

Therefore, **a ⋅ b** = (√13) * (√2) * cos(1.94) ≈ (3.606) * (-0.966) ≈ -3.49

**Example 2:**
Let **p** = <5, 0> and **q** = <0, 6>.
|**p**| = 5
|**q**| = 6
θ = 90° (since one component is 0)

Therefore, **p ⋅ q** = (5) * (6) * cos(90°) = 30 * 0 = 0

**Example 3:**
Consider two vectors **u** = <3,4> and **v** = <1,2>. Calculate their scalar product.
**u ⋅ v** = |u||v|cos(θ)
First, calculate the magnitudes:
|u| = √(3² + 4²) = 5
|v| = √(1² + 2²) = √5
Now, find the cosine of the angle between them:
cos(θ) = ((3 * 1) + (4 * 2)) / (|u||v|) = (3 + 8) / (5√5) = 11 / (5√5)
Finally, calculate the scalar product:
**u ⋅ v** = 5 * √5 * (11 / (5√5)) = 11

### Subtopic 2: Algebraic Calculation of the Scalar Product

The scalar product can also be calculated directly from the components of the vectors using the following formula:

**a ⋅ b = a₁b₁ + a₂b₂ + a₃b₃ + ... + aₙbₙ**

Where **a** = <a₁, a₂, a₃, ..., aₙ> and **b** = <b₁, b₂, b₃, ..., bₙ>.

**Example 4:**
Given **a** = <1, 2, -1> and **b** = <3, -2, 1>, calculate **a ⋅ b**.
**a ⋅ b** = (1 * 3) + (2 * -2) + (-1 * 1) = 3 - 4 - 1 = -2

**Example 5:**
Let **x** = <cos(θ), sin(θ)> and **y** = <sin(θ), cos(θ)> be unit vectors. Find the scalar product **x ⋅ y**.
**x ⋅ y** = (cos(θ) * sin(θ)) + (sin(θ) * cos(θ)) = 2 * cos(θ)sin(θ) = sin(2θ)

**Example 6:**
Calculate the dot product of the vectors **v** = <−2, 5> and **w** = <3, −1>.
**v ⋅ w** = (−2)(3) + (5)(-1) = −6 − 5 = −11

### Subtopic 3: Geometric Interpretation – Projection

The scalar product is intimately linked to the concept of **projection**.  The scalar product allows us to calculate the *projection* of one vector onto another. The projection of **a** onto **b** (denoted as proj~**b**~**a**) is the component of **a** that lies along the direction of **b**.

The formula for the projection is:

proj~**b**~**a** =  ((**a ⋅ b**) / |**b**|²) * **b**

> **Note:** The result of this calculation is a vector. It represents the length of the projection of **a** onto **b**.

**Example 7:**
Find the projection of vector **a** = <1, 1> onto vector **b** = <1, 1>.
**a ⋅ b** = (1 * 1) + (1 * 1) = 2
|**b**|² = 1² + 1² = 2
proj~**b**~**a** = (2 / 2) * <1, 1> = <1, 1>

This shows that the projection of <1,1> onto <1,1> is <1,1>, meaning the vectors are parallel and the angle between them is 0 degrees.

### Subtopic 4: Using the Scalar Product to Find Angles

The scalar product can also be used to calculate the angle (θ) between two vectors:

cos θ = (**a ⋅ b**) / (|**a**||**b**|)

Therefore:

θ = arccos((**a ⋅ b**) / (|**a**||**b**|))

**Example 8:**
Find the angle between **a** = <1, 0> and **b** = <1, 1>.
**a ⋅ b** = (1 * 1) + (0 * 1) = 1
|**a**| = √(1² + 0²) = 1
|**b**| = √(1² + 1²) = √2
cos θ = 1 / (1 * √2) = 1/√2 = √2/2
θ = arccos(√2/2) = 45°

### Subtopic 5: Applications – Work and Projectile Motion

The scalar product has applications in calculating **work** done by a force and in solving problems involving **projectile motion**.

The work done by a constant force **F** in moving an object a distance **d** is given by:

W = **F ⋅ d**

where **F** and **d** are vectors representing the force and displacement, respectively.  This formula assumes the force is constant and acts along the displacement.

> **Exam Tip:** When using the scalar product to calculate work, ensure the force and displacement vectors are aligned.

## Summary

The **scalar product** (or dot product) is a fundamental operation in vector algebra.  It's defined as **a ⋅ b = |a| |b| cos θ**, where θ is the angle between the vectors.  We can calculate it from component values or use it to determine the projection of one vector onto another, calculate angles between vectors, and even compute work done by a force. *It’s crucial to remember that the scalar product is not commutative*. This concept is vital for understanding mechanics and applications in fields like physics and engineering, aligning directly with A.1.1 – Vectors.
