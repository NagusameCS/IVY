## Powers & Roots (De Moivre)

## Overview

This lesson focuses on the properties of powers and roots of complex numbers, culminating in De Moivre's Theorem. Students will explore how to simplify expressions involving roots and powers, and apply De Moivre’s Theorem to solve problems relating to polar coordinates.

## Key Concepts

*   Understanding the properties of **exponents** and their application to complex numbers.
*   Finding **roots** of complex numbers, including complex cube roots and fourth roots.
*   Applying **De Moivre’s Theorem** to solve problems involving polar coordinates and complex numbers.
*   Recognizing the relationship between **rectangular and polar forms** of complex numbers.

### 1. Introduction to Roots of Complex Numbers

> **Important:**  A **root** of a complex number *z* is a complex number *w* such that *w*^{n} = *z* for some integer *n*.  We're primarily concerned with finding *n*th roots.

Let’s consider finding the square roots of a complex number.  We can express a complex number in polar form: *z* = *r*e^{iθ}, where *r* is the magnitude and *θ* is the argument.  To find the *n*th roots of *z*, we can write:

*w*~k~ = ^{n}√*r* [cos((θ + 2πk)/ *n*) + i sin((θ + 2πk)/ *n*)]

where *k* = 0, 1, 2, ..., *n*-1.  This generates *n* distinct roots.

**Example 1:** Find the square roots of 1 + *i*.

1.  First, find the magnitude and argument of 1 + *i*.
    *   *r* = √((1)^{2} + (1)^{2}) = √2
    *   θ = arctan(1/1) = π/4
    *   So, 1 + *i* = √2e^{iπ/4}

2.  Now, find the square roots:
    *   *w~k~* = ^{2}√√2 [cos((π/4 + 2πk)/ 2) + i sin((π/4 + 2πk)/ 2)]
    *   For k = 0: *w~0~* = √2 [cos(π/8) + i sin(π/8)]
    *   For k = 1: *w~1~* = √2 [cos(5π/8) + i sin(5π/8)]

> **Exam Tip:**  IB exams frequently require students to find *n*th roots of complex numbers, so practice is crucial.

### 2. Simplifying Roots with De Moivre’s Theorem

De Moivre’s Theorem provides a direct method for simplifying expressions involving roots and powers of complex numbers.  It states:

(r * e^{iθ})^{n} = r^{n} * e^{i(nθ)}

**Example 2:** Simplify (√2e^{iπ/4})^{3}

1.  Apply De Moivre’s Theorem:
    (√2)^{3} * e^{i(3π/4)} = (2^{1/2})^{3} * e^{i(3π/4)} = 2^{3/2} * e^{i(3π/4)} = 2√2 * e^{i(3π/4)}

2.  Rewrite in rectangular form:
    2√2 * [cos(3π/4) + i sin(3π/4)] = 2√2 * [ -√2/2 + i√2/2 ] = -2 + 2i

### 3. Finding Complex Cube Roots

Let’s find the cube roots of 8.  We want to find *w* such that *w*^{3} = 8.  In polar form, 8 = 8e^{i(0)}.

*w~k~* = ^{3}√8 [cos((0 + 2πk)/ 3) + i sin((0 + 2πk)/ 3)] = 2 [cos(2πk/3) + i sin(2πk/3)]  for k = 0, 1, 2

*   k = 0: *w~0~* = 2 [cos(0) + i sin(0)] = 2
*   k = 1: *w~1~* = 2 [cos(2π/3) + i sin(2π/3)] = 2 * (-1/2 + i√3/2) = -1 + i√3
*   k = 2: *w~2~* = 2 [cos(4π/3) + i sin(4π/3)] = 2 * (-1/2 - i√3/2) = -1 - i√3

### 4. Applying De Moivre’s Theorem to Polar Coordinates

De Moivre’s Theorem is essential for converting between rectangular and polar forms of complex numbers. Let *z* = *r*e^{iθ}.  Then:

z^{2} = r^{2}e^{i(2θ)}
z^{3} = r^{3}e^{i(3θ)}
and so on...

**Example 3:** Given *z* = 4e^{i(π/3)}, find *z*^{2} and *z*^{3}.

1.  *z*^{2} = (4e^{i(π/3)})^{2} = 4^{2}e^{i(2π/3)} = 16e^{i(2π/3)}

2.  *z*^{3} = (4e^{i(π/3)})^{3} = 4^{3}e^{i(3π/3)} = 64e^{i(π)} = 64(cos(π) + i sin(π)) = -64

### 5. Complex Fourth Roots of 1

Find the fourth roots of unity. This means finding *w* such that *w*^{4} = 1.  In polar form, 1 = 1e^{i(0)}.

*w~k~* = ^{4}√1 [cos((0 + 2πk)/ 4) + i sin((0 + 2πk)/ 4)] = cos(πk/2) + i sin(πk/2) for k = 0, 1, 2, 3

*   k = 0: *w~0~* = cos(0) + i sin(0) = 1
*   k = 1: *w~1~* = cos(π/2) + i sin(π/2) = i
*   k = 2: *w~2~* = cos(π) + i sin(π) = -1
*   k = 3: *w~3~* = cos(3π/2) + i sin(3π/2) = -i

These roots are the solutions to the equation *z*^{4} - 1 = 0.

### 6.  Problem Solving with De Moivre’s Theorem

**Example 4:**  A boat travels 10 km on a compass bearing of 315° from point A to point B. It then turns and travels 8 km on a compass bearing of 45°.  Find the distance between the boat's final position and point A.

1.  Convert bearings to angles from the positive x-axis:
    *   315° = -45°
    *   45°

2.  Represent the first leg as a complex number: *z~1~* = 10e^{i(-45°)} = 10(cos(-45°) + i sin(-45°)) = 10(√2/2 - i√2/2) = 5√2 - 5i√2

3.  Represent the second leg as a complex number: *z~2~* = 8e^{i(45°)} = 8(cos(45°) + i sin(45°)) = 8(√2/2 + i√2/2) = 4√2 + 4i√2

4.  The final position is *z~1~* + *z~2~*:
    *z~final~* = (5√2 - 5i√2) + (4√2 + 4i√2) = 9√2 - i√2

5.  Calculate the magnitude of *z~final~*: |*z~final~*| = √((9√2)^{2} + (-√2)^{2}) = √(162 + 2) = √164 = 2√41

> **Note:** This problem utilizes complex numbers to solve a geometry problem.
