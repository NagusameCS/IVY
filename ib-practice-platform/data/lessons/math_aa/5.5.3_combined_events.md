### 1. P(A | B) = P(B|A) * P(A)
**Formula:**
P(A ∩ B) = P(A | B) * P(B)

* P((A ∩ B)) refers to the sample space (all possible outcomes of events A and B).
* The formula calculates the probability of a specific outcome given another, which is important for making predictions.
### 2. P(A | B) = P(A) * P(B|A)
**Calculations:**
The formulas are quite straightforward:
P((A ∩ B)) = (P(A))^n / P(B), where n ranges from 0 to the number of outcomes (in this case, 15 for all possible events). This formula calculates the probability of a single event given another. For example, if we want to know the probability of getting at least one red sock in five different socks without picking them randomly (P(A ∩ B)) = P(B) * P(B|A), the n = 5 value means that each sock has only two possible outcomes: either all blue or all red.
### 3. P((A ∪ B)|C) = P(A) + P(B|C) * P(C)
**Calculations:**
The formulas are straightforward and allow us to add the probabilities of multiple events together (P(AB)). For example:
P([A ∩ B]) = (1/4) * 0.65^2 / 1 - (0.7 * 0.8)^2 / 9 + (0.3 * 0.4)^2 / 9 + (0.2 * 0.3)^2 / 9
Here, P([A ∩ B]) = 0.65^2 / 1 - 0.7 * 0.8^2 / 9 + 0.3 * 0.4^2 / 9 + 0.2 * 0.3^2 / 9
### 4. P(A ∪ B) = P(B|A)P(A) + P(B|C)P(C)
**Interpretation:**
To find the probability of a specific outcome given another event, we can use the intersection of A and B (denoted as B' or AB). We then multiply this probability by its corresponding conditional probability in the first event:
P((A ∩ B)) = P(B') * P(AB) + P(C|A')*P(AC|A), where P(A') is the probability of an intersection of A and B. This formula allows us to find both the probability of a specific outcome given another event (which in this case, is B' or AB) and the overall likelihood for all possible outcomes (including the intersection).
