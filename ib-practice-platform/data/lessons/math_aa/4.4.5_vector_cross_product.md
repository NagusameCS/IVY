## Lesson: Vector (Cross) Product

## Overview

This lesson introduces the concept of the **cross product** of two vectors, exploring its geometric interpretation as a vector perpendicular to both input vectors and its calculation using the determinant method. Students will develop a strong understanding of the magnitude and direction of the resulting vector, crucial for applications in physics and engineering.

## Key Concepts

*   Understanding the geometric interpretation of the cross product.
*   Calculating the cross product using the determinant method.
*   Determining the magnitude and direction of the resulting cross product vector.
*   Applying the cross product to problems involving torque and angular momentum (HL extension).

### 1. Introduction to the Cross Product – Geometric Interpretation

> **Important:** The cross product is a fundamental operation in vector calculus that transforms two vectors into a new vector perpendicular to both of them. It’s not simply multiplication; it has a very specific geometric meaning.

The cross product of vectors **a** and **b** (denoted **a** × **b**) results in a new vector **c** that:

1.  Is **perpendicular** to both **a** and **b**. This means the angle between **a** and **c** is 90 degrees, and similarly for **b** and **c**.
2.  Has a **magnitude** equal to the area of the parallelogram defined by **a** and **b**.
3.  Its **direction** is determined by the **right-hand rule**.

The **right-hand rule** provides a method for determining the direction of the resulting vector. If you point the fingers of your right hand in the direction of **a** and curl them towards **b**, your thumb will point in the direction of **a** × **b**.

**Example 1:**
Let **a** = <1, 0, 0> and **b** = <0, 1, 0>.  The cross product **a** × **b** = <0, 0, 1>. This vector is perpendicular to both **a** and **b** and forms a right-angled triangle with them.  The area of the parallelogram is |1| = 1.

### 2. Calculating the Cross Product with the Determinant Method

> **Note:** The determinant method is the most common and efficient way to calculate the cross product of two vectors in 3D space.

The cross product of vectors **a** = <a~x~, a~y~, a~z~> and **b** = <b~x~, b~y~, b~z~> is calculated using the following determinant:

```
a × b = |  i   j   k  |
        | a~x~  a~y~  a~z~ |
        | b~x~  b~y~  b~z~ |
```

Where **i**, **j**, and **k** are the standard unit vectors along the x, y, and z axes respectively.  Expanding the determinant, we get:

**a × b** = (a~y~b~z~ - a~z~b~y~) **i** - (a~x~b~z~ - a~z~b~x~) **j** + (a~x~b~y~ - a~y~b~x~) **k**

### 3. Worked Example 1: Calculating the Cross Product

**Problem:** Calculate the cross product of the following vectors:
**a** = <2, -1, 3> and **b** = <1, 4, -2>

**Solution:**
Using the determinant method:

```
a × b = |  i   j   k  |
        | 2  -1   3 |
        | 1   4  -2 |
```

Expanding the determinant:

a × b = ((-1)(-2) - (3)(4)) **i** - ((2)(-2) - (3)(1)) **j** + ((2)(4) - (-1)(1)) **k**
a × b = (2 - 12) **i** - (-4 - 3) **j** + (8 + 1) **k**
a × b = -10 **i** + 7 **j** + 9 **k**

Therefore, **a** × **b** = <-10, 7, 9>

### 4. Magnitude and Direction of the Cross Product

> **Exam Tip:** Always calculate the magnitude of the resulting cross product vector to find its area, which is a key component of many problems.

The magnitude of the cross product **a** × **b** is given by:

| **a** × **b** | = | **a** | | **b** | sin θ

where θ is the angle between vectors **a** and **b**.

The direction of **a** × **b** is given by the **right-hand rule**.  The sign of the components of the cross product determines the direction, indicating whether the area formed by the vectors is oriented in the positive or negative sense.

### 5. Worked Example 2: Finding Magnitude and Direction

**Problem:** Calculate the magnitude and direction of the cross product of **a** = <1, 2, 3> and **b** = <4, -1, 2>.

**Solution:**
1.  **Calculate the Cross Product:**
    Using the determinant method (as shown in Example 1) we get **a** × **b** = <-7, 5, -1>.

2.  **Magnitude:**
    |**a** × **b**| = √((-7)^{2} + 5^{2} + (-1)^{2}) = √(49 + 25 + 1) = √75 = 5√3

    Therefore, the magnitude of **a** × **b** is 5√3.

3.  **Direction:**  Applying the right-hand rule: Point your fingers in the direction of **a** and curl them towards **b**. Your thumb points in the direction of **a** × **b**, which is <-7, 5, -1>.

### 6. (HL Extension) Torque and Angular Momentum

> **Note:** The cross product is vital in understanding rotational motion.

**Torque (τ)** is defined as the cross product of the position vector **r** from the axis of rotation to the point of force application and the force **F**:

τ = **r** × **F**

This represents the *tendency* of a force to cause rotation.  The magnitude of torque is equal to the lever arm (the length of the position vector) multiplied by the sine of the angle between **r** and **F**.

Similarly, **angular momentum (L)** is defined as the cross product of the vector of angular momentum and the time derivative of the angular position:

L = **r** × **p**

Where **p** is the linear momentum of the rotating object.

## Summary

The **cross product** is a fundamental operation in vector calculus that allows us to calculate a new vector perpendicular to two given vectors.  It's calculated using the determinant method, providing a clear way to determine the magnitude (equal to the area of the parallelogram formed by the vectors) and direction (using the right-hand rule). This concept is crucial for applications in geometry, physics (calculating torque and angular momentum), and engineering.  Mastering the cross product opens doors to understanding and solving a wide range of problems involving rotational motion and forces.
