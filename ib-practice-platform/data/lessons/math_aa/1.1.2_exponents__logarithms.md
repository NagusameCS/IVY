## Exponents & Logarithms

## Overview

This lesson explores the fundamental concepts of exponents and logarithms, focusing on their relationships, applications, and how they are used to solve algebraic problems. We’ll cover exponential growth and decay, and the inverse relationship between exponents and logarithms.

## Key Concepts

*   Understanding **exponents** and their properties.
*   Exploring **logarithms** as the inverse operation of exponents.
*   Applying exponential and logarithmic equations to model real-world scenarios.
*   Using **logarithmic properties** to simplify expressions and solve equations.
*   Connecting exponential and logarithmic forms of equations.

### 1. Introduction to Exponents

> **Note:** An exponent indicates the number of times a base is multiplied by itself.

Exponents are a shorthand notation for repeated multiplication.  The expression  $b^n$  (read as "b to the power of n") means  $b \cdot b \cdot b \cdots b$  (n times). Let’s explore some basic exponent rules:

*   **Rule 1: Product of Like Bases:** $b^m \cdot b^n = b^{m+n}$
    *   **Example:** $2^3 \cdot 2^2 = 2^{3+2} = 2^5 = 32$
*   **Rule 2: Quotient of Like Bases:** $\frac{b^m}{b^n} = b^{m-n}$
    *   **Example:** $\frac{5^4}{5^2} = 5^{4-2} = 5^2 = 25$
*   **Rule 3: Power of a Power:** $(b^m)^n = b^{m \cdot n}$
    *   **Example:** $(3^2)^3 = 3^{2 \cdot 3} = 3^6 = 729$

**Worked Example 1:**

Simplify the following expression using the exponent rules:  $x^2 \cdot x^5 \cdot x^3$

**Solution:**

1.  Combine like bases: $x^{2+5+3} = x^{10}$

**Worked Example 2:**

Simplify the following expression using the exponent rules: $\frac{y^7}{y^2} \cdot y^4$

**Solution:**

1.  Simplify the fraction: $\frac{y^7}{y^2} = y^{7-2} = y^5$
2.  Multiply: $y^5 \cdot y^4 = y^{5+4} = y^9$

### 2. Introduction to Logarithms

> **Important:** Logarithms are the inverse of exponential functions. They answer the question: “To what power must I raise the base to get a certain number?”

The **logarithm** is the inverse operation of an exponent.  The general form of a logarithmic equation is:

$log_b(x) = y$

This is equivalent to the exponential equation: $b^y = x$

*   $b$ is the **base** of the logarithm.
*   $x$ is the **argument** of the logarithm.
*   $y$ is the **exponent** (or the logarithm itself).

**Example:** $log_2(8) = 3$ because $2^3 = 8$

### 3. Logarithmic Properties

Logarithms have properties that mirror those of exponents, allowing us to simplify expressions and solve equations.

*   **Product Rule:** $log_b(xy) = log_b(x) + log_b(y)$
*   **Quotient Rule:** $log_b(\frac{x}{y}) = log_b(x) - log_b(y)$
*   **Power Rule:** $log_b(x^n) = n \cdot log_b(x)$

> **Example:** Simplify $log_2(16) + log_2(4)$

**Solution:**

1.  Using the Product Rule: $log_2(16 \cdot 4) = log_2(64)$
2.  Since $2^6 = 64$, $log_2(64) = 6$.

### 4. Solving Exponential and Logarithmic Equations

> **Exam Tip:**  Always check your solutions to ensure they are valid, especially when dealing with logarithms (domain restrictions).

Let's look at solving equations.

**Worked Example 3:**

Solve the exponential equation: $3^x = 27$

**Solution:**

1.  Express 27 as a power of 3: $3^x = 3^3$
2.  Therefore, $x = 3$

**Worked Example 4:**

Solve the logarithmic equation: $log_5(x) = 2$

**Solution:**

1.  Convert the logarithmic equation to exponential form: $5^2 = x$
2.  Therefore, $x = 25$

### 5.  Applications: Exponential Growth and Decay

Exponential functions are commonly used to model phenomena involving growth or decay.

*   **Exponential Growth:**  $y = A \cdot b^x$, where:
    *   $y$ is the final amount.
    *   $A$ is the initial amount.
    *   $b$ is the growth factor (b > 1).
    *   $x$ is the time.

*   **Exponential Decay:** $y = A \cdot (1/b)^x$, where:
    *   $y$ is the remaining amount.
    *   $A$ is the initial amount.
    *   $b$ is the decay factor (0 < b < 1).
    *   $x$ is the time.

**Example:** A population of bacteria doubles every hour. Initially, there are 100 bacteria.  What is the population after 5 hours?

**Solution:**

*   $A = 100$
*   $b = 2$
*   $x = 5$
*   $y = 100 \cdot 2^5 = 100 \cdot 32 = 3200$

### 6.  Change of Base Formula

> **Note:** The change of base formula allows us to calculate logarithms with bases other than 10 or *e*.

The **change of base formula** is: $log_b(x) = \frac{log_a(x)}{log_a(b)}$ where *a* can be 10 or *e*.

**Example:** Calculate $log_2(10)$ using the change of base formula with base 10.

**Solution:**

1.  $log_2(10) = \frac{log_{10}(10)}{log_{10}(2)} = \frac{1}{log_{10}(2)} \approx \frac{1}{0.3010} \approx 3.322$
