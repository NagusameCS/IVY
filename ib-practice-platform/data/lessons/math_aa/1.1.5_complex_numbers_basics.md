# Complex Numbers Basics

## Overview

This lesson introduces students to the concept of **complex numbers**, building from real numbers and exploring their representation, operations, and applications. We will cover the definition of a complex number, its notation, and fundamental arithmetic operations.

## Key Concepts

*   Understanding the limitations of real numbers in representing certain quantities.
*   Introducing the imaginary unit *i* and its properties.
*   Performing addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division with complex numbers.
*   Representing complex numbers graphically in the complex plane.

### Introduction to Complex Numbers

> **Important:** Real numbers can only represent quantities with sign changes that are divisible by zero. Complex numbers extend the number system to include quantities involving an *imaginary* part.

Complex numbers are typically represented in the form *a + bi*, where:
*   *a* is the **real part**
*   *b* is the **imaginary part**
*   *i* is the **imaginary unit**, defined as *i* = √(-1). *Note:* This is a fundamental assumption; we're accepting that √(-1) exists for the purposes of this lesson.

**Example 1:**
Let's consider the number 3 + 2*i*. Here, 3 is the real part and 2 is the imaginary part. We can write it as z = 3 + 2i.

### The Imaginary Unit *i***

The **imaginary unit** *i* is a key component of complex numbers. It allows us to represent and work with roots of negative numbers. The defining property of *i* is:

*   *i*^{2} = -1

This simple yet powerful relationship is the foundation for all operations involving *i*.

**Example 2:**
Calculate *i*^{3} and *i*^{4}.
*   *i*^{3} = *i*^{2} * *i* = -1 * *i* = -*i*
*   *i*^{4} = (*i*^{2})^{2} = (-1)^{2} = 1

### Addition and Subtraction of Complex Numbers

Adding or subtracting complex numbers involves combining like terms.  The key is to treat the real and imaginary parts separately.

**Example 3:**
Given z~1~ = 2 + 3*i* and z~2~ = 1 - 4*i*, find z~1~ + z~2~.
z~1~ + z~2~ = (2 + 1) + (3 - 4)*i* = 3 - *i*

**Example 4:**
Given z~1~ = 5 - 2*i* and z~2~ = 2 + 7*i*, find z~1~ - z~2~.
z~1~ - z~2~ = (5 - 2) - (2 - 7)*i* = 3 + 5*i*

### Multiplication of Complex Numbers

Multiplying complex numbers follows the distributive property (FOIL - First, Outer, Inner, Last) similar to multiplying binomials. Remember *i*^{2} = -1.

**Example 5:**
Multiply (1 + *i*) by (2 - *i*).
(1 + *i*)(2 - *i*) = 1(2) + 1(-*i*) + *i*(2) + *i*(-*i*) = 2 - *i* + 2*i* - *i*^{2} = 2 + *i* - (-1) = 2 + *i* + 1 = 3 + *i*

**Example 6:**
Multiply (3 + 4*i*)(3 - 4*i*).
(3 + 4*i*)(3 - 4*i*) = 3(3) + 3(-4*i*) + 4*i*(3) + 4*i*(-4*i*) = 9 - 12*i* + 12*i* - 16*i*^{2} = 9 - 16(-1) = 9 + 16 = 25

### Division of Complex Numbers

Dividing complex numbers involves dividing the real and imaginary parts separately.  Again, *i*^{2} = -1.  It’s helpful to rationalize the denominator, similar to rationalizing denominators with radical expressions.

**Example 7:**
Divide (3 + 2*i*) by (1 - *i*).
First, we multiply the numerator and denominator by the conjugate of the denominator, which is (1 + *i*).
((3 + 2*i*)(1 + *i*))/((1 - *i*)(1 + *i*)) = (3(1) + 3(*i*) + 2*i*(1) + 2*i*(*i*))/(1^{2} - (*i*)^{2}) = (3 + 3*i* + 2*i* + 2*i*^{2})/(1 - (-1)) = (3 + 5*i* + 2(-1))/(2) = (3 + 5*i* - 2)/(2) = (1 + 5*i*)/2 = 1/2 + (5/2)*i*

**Exam Tip:**  Pay close attention to signs when multiplying and dividing. Rationalizing the denominator is a common technique that simplifies complex expressions.

### Complex Plane Representation

> **Note:** The complex plane provides a visual representation of complex numbers.  The horizontal axis represents the **real part** (a), and the vertical axis represents the **imaginary part** (b).

A complex number *a + bi* is plotted as a point (a, b) in the complex plane.  This allows us to easily visualize and analyze complex numbers.

**Example 8:**
Represent 2 + 3*i* and -1 - 2*i* in the complex plane.  Sketch the coordinates on a plane.  (This is best done on paper.)
