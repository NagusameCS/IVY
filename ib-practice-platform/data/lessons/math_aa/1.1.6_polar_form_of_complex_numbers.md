## Key Concepts

This lesson will explore the **polar form** of complex numbers, allowing us to represent them in terms of magnitude and angle. We’ll learn how to convert between Cartesian and polar forms and understand their applications, particularly in representing rotational transformations and analyzing periodic functions.  This builds directly on A.1.1 Number and Algebra – Complex Numbers.

### 1. Introduction to Complex Numbers and Cartesian Form

Complex numbers are numbers of the form *z = a + bi*, where *a* and *b* are real numbers, and *i* is the **imaginary unit** such that *i² = -1*. The real part of the complex number is *a*, and the imaginary part is *b*. 

> **Important:** Complex numbers are essential for representing rotations and transformations in 2D space.

**Example 1:**
Represent the complex number *z = 3 + 4i* in Cartesian form.

*Solution:*
The real part is 3 and the imaginary part is 4.  Therefore, *z = 3 + 4i*.

**Example 2:**
Represent the complex number *z = -2 - i* in Cartesian form.

*Solution:*
The real part is -2 and the imaginary part is -1. Therefore, *z = -2 - i*.

**Example 3:**
Given *z = 5i*, express *z* in Cartesian form.

*Solution:*
The real part is 0 and the imaginary part is 5. Therefore, *z = 0 + 5i*.

### 2. The Polar Form of a Complex Number

The **polar form** of a complex number *z* represents it using its **magnitude (r)** and **argument (θ)**.

*   **Magnitude (r):** The distance of the complex number from the origin (0, 0) on the complex plane. It’s always a non-negative real number.
*   **Argument (θ):** The angle (in degrees or radians) between the positive real axis and the line connecting the origin to the point representing *z*.

A complex number *z* in polar form is given by *z = r(cos θ + i sin θ)*. This is known as **Euler's Formula**.

> **Note:**  The argument, θ, is often referred to as the **phase** of the complex number.

### 3. Converting Between Cartesian and Polar Forms

We can convert between Cartesian and polar forms using trigonometric functions.

**3.1 From Cartesian to Polar:**

To convert *z = a + bi* to polar form, we use the following formulas:

*   *r = √(a² + b²)*
*   *θ = arctan(b/a)* (Ensure you use the correct quadrant for θ based on the signs of *a* and *b*)

**Example 4:**
Convert the complex number *z = 1 + i* to polar form.

*Solution:*
1.  *r = √(1² + 1²) = √2*
2.  *θ = arctan(1/1) = arctan(1) = 45°*  (Since both *a* and *b* are positive, the angle is in the first quadrant).

Therefore, *z = √2(cos 45° + i sin 45°)* or *z = √2 * e^(i * 45°)*.

**Example 5:**
Convert the complex number *z = -1 + i* to polar form.

*Solution:*
1. *r = √((-1)² + 1²) = √2*
2. *θ = arctan(1/-1) = arctan(-1) = -45°* (Since *a* is negative and *b* is positive, the angle is in the second quadrant).

Therefore, *z = √2(cos(-45°) + i sin(-45°))*.

### 4. Magnitude and the Complex Plane

The magnitude *r* of a complex number in polar form is directly related to the distance from the origin.  On the complex plane, the magnitude represents the length of the line segment from the origin to the point representing the complex number. *r* is always a positive value.

> **Exam Tip:**  Be careful when calculating *r* – always take the positive square root.

### 5. Using Polar Form for Multiplication and Division

Multiplying or dividing complex numbers in polar form is often simpler than using Cartesian form, especially when the numbers have different magnitudes or arguments.

**Rule:** *z₁z₂ = r₁r₂ [cos(θ₁ + θ₂) + i sin(θ₁ + θ₂)]*
**Rule:** *z₁/z₂ = r₁/r₂ [cos(θ₁ - θ₂) + i sin(θ₁ - θ₂)]*

**Example 6:**
Multiply the complex numbers *z₁ = √2(cos 45° + i sin 45°)* and *z₂ = √2(cos 60° + i sin 60°)*.

*Solution:*
*z₁z₂ = (√2)(√2) [cos(45° + 60°) + i sin(45° + 60°)] = 2 [cos(105°) + i sin(105°)]*

### 6. Applications: Rotational Transformations

The polar form of complex numbers is incredibly useful in describing **rotational transformations** in the plane. A complex number *z* can be used to represent a point in the plane.  Multiplying by *z* rotates the point by an angle *θ* about the origin.

> **Example:** A rotation of 90° counterclockwise is represented by the complex number *i* (or *e^(i * 90°)*).
