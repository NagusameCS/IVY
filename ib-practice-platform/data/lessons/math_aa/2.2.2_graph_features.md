## Key Concepts (Main Learning Points)

*   Students will be able to accurately identify and interpret key features of various types of functions, including linear, quadratic, and trigonometric functions.
*   Students will understand the relationship between a function’s graph and its algebraic representation, and vice versa.



### Subtopic 1: Linear Functions - Identifying Key Features

Linear functions are characterized by a constant rate of change.  Let’s explore how to identify the key features of these functions.

> **Important:**  A linear function can be represented in the form *f(x) = mx + c*, where *m* is the slope and *c* is the y-intercept.

**1.  Slope (m):** The slope represents the rate of change of the function. It’s calculated as the rise over run between any two points on the graph.

   *   **Formula:** $m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$
   *   **Example 1:** Given the line *f(x) = 2x + 1*, what is the slope?  The slope is 2. This means for every 1 unit increase in *x*, the function *f(x)* increases by 2 units.
   *   **Example 2:**  Find the slope of the line passing through the points (1, 3) and (4, 11).  $m = \frac{11 - 3}{4 - 1} = \frac{8}{3}$

**2.  Y-intercept (c):** The y-intercept is the point where the line crosses the y-axis.  It is the value of *f(0)*.

   *   **Finding the y-intercept:**  Set *x = 0* in the equation of the line *f(x) = mx + c*.  Solving for *c* gives you the y-intercept.
   *   **Example 1:**  For *f(x) = -3x + 5*, the y-intercept is 5. This means the line crosses the y-axis at the point (0, 5).
   *   **Example 2:**  A line has a slope of -1 and passes through the point (2, 4). What is the y-intercept?  Using the point-slope form *y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)*:  *y - 4 = -1(x - 2)*. Solving for y when x = 0: *y - 4 = -1(0 - 2) = 2*, so *y = 6*. The y-intercept is 6.



### Subtopic 2: Quadratic Functions - Key Features

Quadratic functions are defined by a parabola. Their graphs have distinctive features.

> **Note:** Quadratic functions can be represented by the equation *f(x) = ax² + bx + c*, where *a*, *b*, and *c* are constants, and *a ≠ 0*.

**1.  Vertex:** The vertex is the minimum or maximum point of the parabola.  Its coordinates are given by the formula: $x_v = -\frac{b}{2a}$ and $y_v = f(x_v)$.

   *   **Example 1:**  Given *f(x) = x² - 4x + 3*, find the vertex.
      *   $x_v = -\frac{-4}{2(1)} = 2$
      *   $y_v = f(2) = (2)² - 4(2) + 3 = 4 - 8 + 3 = -1$
      *   The vertex is (2, -1).
   *   **Example 2:**  Determine the vertex of the parabola *f(x) = 2x² + 8x - 5*.  $x_v = -\frac{8}{2(2)} = -2$.  $y_v = f(-2) = 2(-2)² + 8(-2) - 5 = 8 - 16 - 5 = -13$. The vertex is (-2, -13).

**2.  Axis of Symmetry:** This is a vertical line that passes through the vertex, dividing the parabola into two symmetrical halves.  Its equation is *x = x_v*.

   *   **Example 1:** Using the vertex (2, -1) of *f(x) = x² - 4x + 3*, the axis of symmetry is *x = 2*.
   *   **Example 2:** Given the parabola *f(x) = -3x² + 6x + 1*, find the axis of symmetry. *x_v = -b/2a = -6 / (2 * -3) = 1*.  The axis of symmetry is *x = 1*.

**3.  X-intercept(s):** These are the points where the parabola crosses the x-axis (where *f(x) = 0*).  Solve the quadratic equation *ax² + bx + c = 0* using the quadratic formula.

   *   **Example 1:** Find the x-intercepts of *f(x) = x² - 5x + 6*.  *x² - 5x + 6 = 0*. Using the quadratic formula: $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} = \frac{5 \pm \sqrt{(-5)^2 - 4(1)(6)}}{2(1)} = \frac{5 \pm \sqrt{25 - 24}}{2} = \frac{5 \pm 1}{2}$. Therefore, *x = 3* or *x = 2*. The x-intercepts are (3, 0) and (2, 0).



### Subtopic 3: Trigonometric Functions – Initial Features

Trigonometric functions (sine, cosine, tangent) relate angles and side lengths of right-angled triangles.  Understanding their graphs is key.

> **Exam Tip:** Trigonometric graphs are periodic, meaning their values repeat after a certain interval.

**1.  Amplitude:** The amplitude is half the distance between the maximum and minimum values of the function.  For sine and cosine, the amplitude is a positive value.

**2.  Period:** The period is the length of one complete cycle of the function. For sine and cosine, the period is *2π*.

**3.  Key Points:** The graphs of sine and cosine are defined by specific key points (e.g., π/2, π, 3π/2, 2π). These points define one complete cycle.

   * **Example 1:**  Consider the graph of *y = sin(x)*. The amplitude is 1 and the period is 2π.



### Subtopic 4: Comparing and Contrasting Functions

Understanding the similarities and differences between different function types is crucial.

*   **Linear vs. Quadratic:** Linear functions have a constant rate of change, while quadratic functions have a curved shape determined by their squared term.
*   **Quadratic vs. Trigonometric:** Both can model periodic phenomena, but quadratic functions are generally defined over a restricted domain (usually real numbers), while trigonometric functions extend infinitely.



### Subtopic 5: Graphical Transformations

Transformations of functions affect their key features.

*   **Horizontal Shifts:**  *f(x - a)* shifts the graph to the right by *a* units.
*   **Horizontal Reflections:** *f(-x)* reflects the graph across the y-axis.
*   **Vertical Shifts:** *f(x) + c* shifts the graph upwards by *c* units.
*   **Vertical Reflections:** *f(-x)* reflects the graph across the y-axis.



### Subtopic 6: Using Function Notation and Equations

> **Example:** *f(x) = x² - 2x + 1*. To find the value of *f(x)* when *x = 3*, substitute *3* for *x* in the equation: *f(3) = (3)² - 2(3) + 1 = 9 - 6 + 1 = 4*.
