## Compound & Double Angles

## Overview

This lesson will explore the fundamental concepts of **compound angles** in trigonometry, focusing on both the geometric and algebraic approaches. Students will learn to derive formulas for sine and cosine of double angles, alongside understanding their applications within problem-solving.  We will cover both SL and HL extensions.

## Key Concepts

*   Understanding the geometric representation of compound angles.
*   Deriving trigonometric identities for double angles.
*   Applying these identities to solve problems involving angles and triangles.
*   Recognizing the importance of these identities in various contexts (e.g., calculus, physics).

### Subtopic 1: Geometric Representation of Compound Angles

> **Important:** A **compound angle** is formed by adding two angles. In trigonometry, we typically refer to the sum of two angles within a triangle. Let's consider a triangle ABC, where angle A is a **compound angle** formed by angles α and β.

*   We can represent the compound angle A as A = α + β.
*   Using the **sine rule**, we can derive trigonometric ratios for this compound angle:

    $$ \frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C} $$

    Where ‘a’ is the side opposite to angle A, ‘b’ is the side opposite to angle B, and ‘c’ is the side opposite to angle C.

*   **Example 1:** Consider triangle ABC where α = 30° and β = 60°. Then A = 30° + 60° = 90°. We can apply the sine rule:

    $$ \frac{a}{\sin 90^\circ} = \frac{b}{\sin 60^\circ} = \frac{c}{\sin 30^\circ} $$

    $$ \frac{a}{1} = \frac{b}{\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}} = \frac{c}{\frac{1}{2}} $$
    This allows us to determine the ratios of the sides, and hence the lengths.

*   **Exam Tip:** *Always* consider the geometric interpretation when dealing with compound angles – it provides a visual aid and a solid foundation for understanding.

### Subtopic 2: Deriving the Double Angle Formulas for Sine

*   We can derive the **sine of a double angle** using the sine rule on a triangle formed by the compound angle.

*   Let’s consider a triangle ABC with angles α and β and side *a* opposite to angle α and side *b* opposite to angle β.  The **compound angle** is A = α + β.

*   Using the sine rule:
    $$ \frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} $$

*   We can rewrite this as:
    $$ a = \frac{b \sin A}{\sin B} $$

*   Now, let's define:

    $$ \sin(2\alpha) = \sin(\alpha + \alpha) $$

*   Substituting:
    $$ \sin(2\alpha) = \sin(\alpha) \cos(\alpha) + \cos(\alpha) \sin(\alpha) = \sin(\alpha)(1 - \sin^2(\alpha)) + \cos(\alpha) \sin(\alpha) $$

*   This can be further simplified, however, to obtain the standard double angle formula. We'll demonstrate a simplification here.

*   **Example 2:**  Prove that  sin(2θ) = 2sin(θ)cos(θ).

    1.  Start with:  sin(2θ) = sin(θ + θ)
    2.  Use the sine addition formula: sin(θ + θ) = sin(θ)cos(θ) + cos(θ)sin(θ)
    3.  Rearrange: sin(2θ) = 2cos(θ)sin(θ)

### Subtopic 3: Deriving the Double Angle Formulas for Cosine

*   Similarly, we can derive the **cosine of a double angle** using trigonometric identities and the sine rule.  The key is recognizing the relationships between sine and cosine.

*   Start with: cos(2θ) = cos(θ + θ)

*   Using the cosine addition formula: cos(θ + θ) = cos(θ)cos(θ) - sin(θ)sin(θ)

*   Therefore:

    $$ \cos(2\theta) = \cos^2(\theta) - \sin^2(\theta) $$

*   Using the Pythagorean identity: sin^{2}(θ) + cos^{2}(θ) = 1

    $$ \cos(2\theta) = 1 - 2\sin^2(\theta) $$

*   **Example 3:** Prove that cos(2θ) = cos^{2}(θ) - sin^{2}(θ).  (This is a direct application of the cosine addition formula.)

*   **Note:**  These formulas are frequently used in applications such as calculating areas of sectors of circles and angles in triangles.

### Subtopic 4:  HL Extension:  Deriving the Double Angle Formula for Tangent

*   For HL students, we can derive the **tangent of a double angle** using the following:

    $$ \tan(2\theta) = \frac{\tan(\theta) + \tan(\theta)}{1 - \tan^2(\theta)} = \frac{2 \tan(\theta)}{1 - \tan^2(\theta)} $$

*   This derivation involves the tangent addition formula and recognizing the relationships between tangent and sine/cosine.

*   **Example 4:**  Calculate tan(2θ) if tan(θ) = 1/3.  Substitute into the HL derived formula:

    $$ \tan(2θ) = \frac{2(1/3)}{1 - (1/3)^2} = \frac{2/3}{1 - 1/9} = \frac{2/3}{8/9} = \frac{2}{3} \cdot \frac{9}{8} = \frac{3}{4} $$

### Subtopic 5:  Applications of Double Angle Formulas

*   These formulas are invaluable in various applications:

    *   **Calculus:**  Used to differentiate and integrate trigonometric functions.
    *   **Physics:**  Used in calculations involving wave phenomena (e.g., diffraction, interference).
    *   **Navigation:**  Used to calculate angles and distances.

### Subtopic 6:  Exam Practice - Problem Solving

*   **Question:**  In triangle ABC, angle A = 60°, angle B = 45°, and side BC = 6 cm.  Calculate the length of side AB. (SL)

    *   **Solution:**
        1.  Find angle C:  C = 180° - 60° - 45° = 75°
        2.  Use the sine rule:  $$\frac{AB}{\sin C} = \frac{BC}{\sin A}$$
        3.  $$AB = \frac{BC \sin C}{\sin A} = \frac{6 \sin 75^\circ}{\sin 60^\circ}$$
        4.  Calculate:  $AB = \frac{6 \times 0.9659}{0.8660} \approx 7.21 \text{ cm}$

*   **Question:** Prove that 2cos^{2}(θ) – 1 = cos(2θ). (HL)

    *   **Solution:**
        1. Start with: 2cos^{2}(θ) – 1
        2. Use the Pythagorean identity: cos^{2}(θ) + sin^{2}(θ) = 1, so sin^{2}(θ) = 1 - cos^{2}(θ)
        3. Substitute: 2cos^{2}(θ) – 1 = 2cos^{2}(θ) - (1 - cos^{2}(θ)) = 3cos^{2}(θ) - 1
        4. Use the double angle identity for cosine: cos(2θ) = 2cos^{2}(θ) - 1
        5. Therefore, 2cos^{2}(θ) – 1 = cos(2θ)
