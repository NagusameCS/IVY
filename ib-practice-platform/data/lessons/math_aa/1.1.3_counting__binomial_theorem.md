## Counting & Binomial Theorem

This lesson will explore methods for counting, focusing on combinations and permutations, alongside an introduction to the Binomial Theorem and its application in expanding binomial expressions. Students will develop skills in calculating probabilities and understanding the relationships between different counting techniques.

## Key Concepts

*   Understanding the difference between combinations and permutations.
*   Applying the formula for calculating combinations and permutations.
*   Utilizing the Binomial Theorem to expand binomial expressions.
*   Calculating probabilities involving combinations and permutations.
*   Recognizing the significance of these concepts in IB Math AA assessments.

### 1. Combinations vs. Permutations

> **Important:** *The key difference lies in whether the order of selection matters.* Combinations involve selecting items where order doesn’t matter, while permutations do.

Combinations are used when we’re interested in the *number of ways* to choose a group of items from a set, *without considering the order in which they are chosen*.  Permutations, on the other hand, deal with the *number of ways* to arrange items in a specific order.

**Example:**

Imagine selecting a committee of 2 people from a group of 5.

*   **Combination:** We want to know how many different committees of 2 can be formed. The order doesn't matter - choosing Alice and Bob is the same as choosing Bob and Alice.
*   **Permutation:** We want to know how many different ways we can *arrange* 2 people out of 5 to form a committee, considering the order in which they are selected.

### 2. Calculating Combinations

The number of ways to choose *r* items from a set of *n* items (where order doesn't matter) is given by the combination formula:

$$C(n, r) = \binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$$

Where:
*   `n` is the total number of items in the set.
*   `r` is the number of items to be chosen.
*   `!` denotes the factorial function (e.g., 5! = 5 * 4 * 3 * 2 * 1).

**Worked Example 1:**

A committee of 3 students needs to be chosen from a group of 8 students. How many different committees can be formed?

*   n = 8 (total number of students)
*   r = 3 (number of students to be chosen)

Using the combination formula:

$$C(8, 3) = \binom{8}{3} = \frac{8!}{3!(8-3)!} = \frac{8!}{3!5!} = \frac{8 \times 7 \times 6}{3 \times 2 \times 1} = 56$$

Therefore, there are 56 different committees that can be formed.

**Worked Example 2:**

A teacher wants to select 2 teaching assistants from a group of 5 assistants.  In how many ways can she do this?

*   n = 5
*   r = 2

$$C(5, 2) = \binom{5}{2} = \frac{5!}{2!(5-2)!} = \frac{5!}{2!3!} = \frac{5 \times 4}{2 \times 1} = 10$$

There are 10 ways to select 2 teaching assistants.

### 3. Calculating Permutations

The number of ways to arrange *r* items from a set of *n* items (where order matters) is given by the permutation formula:

$$P(n, r) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}$$

**Example:**

How many different ways can you arrange 3 books on a shelf?

*   n = 3 (total number of books)
*   r = 3 (number of books to be arranged)

$$P(3, 3) = \frac{3!}{(3-3)!} = \frac{3!}{0!} = 3 \times 2 \times 1 = 6$$

There are 6 different ways to arrange the 3 books.

### 4. The Binomial Theorem

The **Binomial Theorem** provides a way to expand binomial expressions raised to any non-negative integer power. It’s defined as:

$$(a + b)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k$$

Where:
*   `n` is the power to which the binomial is raised.
*   `a` and `b` are the terms in the binomial.
*   `k` is the index of summation, ranging from 0 to *n*.
*   $\binom{n}{k}$ is the binomial coefficient, calculated as  $\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$

> **Exam Tip:**  Understand the summation notation – it’s frequently tested in IB exams.

### 5. Expanding Binomials Using the Binomial Theorem

**Worked Example 3:**

Expand the expression $(x + 2)^3$ using the Binomial Theorem.

Using the formula:

$(x + 2)^3 = \binom{3}{0} x^{3-0} (2)^0 + \binom{3}{1} x^{3-1} (2)^1 + \binom{3}{2} x^{3-2} (2)^2 + \binom{3}{3} x^{3-3} (2)^3$

$= 1 \cdot x^3 \cdot 1 + 3 \cdot x^2 \cdot 2 + 3 \cdot x \cdot 4 + 1 \cdot x^0 \cdot 8$

$= x^3 + 6x^2 + 12x + 8$

### 6. Probability Calculations

Probability can be calculated using combinations and permutations when the number of ways to select items matters in determining the likelihood of an event.

**Worked Example 4:**

A bag contains 5 red balls and 3 blue balls.  If you randomly select 2 balls from the bag, what is the probability of selecting 2 red balls?

*   Total number of ways to choose 2 balls from 8:  $C(8, 2) = \binom{8}{2} = \frac{8!}{2!6!} = 28$
*   Number of ways to choose 2 red balls from 5: $C(5, 2) = \binom{5}{2} = \frac{5!}{2!3!} = 10$

Probability (2 red balls) = (Number of ways to choose 2 red balls) / (Total number of ways to choose 2 balls)

Probability = $\frac{10}{28} = \frac{5}{14}$
