## Vector Basics

## Overview

This lesson introduces students to the fundamental concepts of vectors within the IB Mathematics Analysis and Approaches (Mathematics AA) curriculum. We’ll explore vector representation, operations, and their applications, laying a crucial foundation for more advanced topics.

## Key Concepts

*   Understanding **vector** representation and its geometric interpretation.
*   Performing **vector addition** and **scalar multiplication** accurately.
*   Applying vectors to solve problems related to displacement, velocity, and force.
*   Analyzing vectors in 2D and 3D space.

### 1. Introduction to Vectors

> **Important:** A vector is a mathematical object that has both magnitude (size) and direction. Unlike a scalar, which is a single number representing quantity (e.g., temperature, mass), a vector describes something that has a specific direction.

Vectors are commonly represented graphically as arrows. The length of the arrow represents the **magnitude** of the vector, and the arrow’s orientation indicates the **direction**.

*   **Representing Vectors:** Vectors are often written in component form, using coordinates like <x, y> in 2D or <x, y, z> in 3D space.  This allows us to precisely define their magnitude and direction.
*   **Unit Vectors:** A **unit vector** is a vector with a magnitude of 1. They are often denoted with a hat symbol (e.g.,  $\hat{i}$ , $\hat{j}$ , $\hat{k}$) and are used to express vectors in terms of their direction only.
*   **Example:** Consider a vector $\vec{v} = <3, 4>$. This represents a vector starting at the origin (0, 0) and ending at the point (3, 4) in the xy-plane. The magnitude of $\vec{v}$ is $\sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = 5$, and the direction is given by the angle it makes with the x-axis.

**Worked Example 1:**

Given a vector $\vec{a} = <2, -1>$, draw a diagram representing this vector in the xy-plane. Label the components clearly.

*   **Solution:** Draw a coordinate plane (x-y plane). Plot the point (2, -1). Draw an arrow from the origin (0, 0) to this point. This arrow represents the vector $\vec{a}$.

### 2. Vector Addition

> **Note:** Vector addition can be performed geometrically or using component-wise addition. The component-wise method is the most common and efficient in the IB context.

*   **Geometric Addition:** Adding vectors geometrically involves placing the vectors head-to-tail and drawing an arrow from the tail of the first vector to the head of the second vector. The resultant vector is the arrow drawn.
*   **Component-wise Addition:** For two vectors $\vec{a} = <a_1, a_2>$ and $\vec{b} = <b_1, b_2>$, their sum, $\vec{a} + \vec{b}$, is calculated as:
    $\vec{a} + \vec{b} = <a_1 + b_1, a_2 + b_2>$

**Worked Example 2:**

Given $\vec{u} = <1, 2>$ and $\vec{v} = <3, -1>$, find $\vec{u} + \vec{v}$ using component-wise addition.

*   **Solution:**
    $\vec{u} + \vec{v} = <1 + 3, 2 + (-1)> = <4, 1>$

### 3. Scalar Multiplication

> **Exam Tip:** Scalar multiplication changes the magnitude of a vector but not its direction (assuming the scalar is positive). If the scalar is negative, it reverses the direction of the vector.

*   **Scalar Multiplication:** Multiplying a vector by a scalar ‘k’ results in a new vector with a magnitude scaled by ‘k’ and the same direction as the original vector (if *k* > 0) or the opposite direction (if *k* < 0).

    *   If *k* > 0:  *k*$\vec{v} = <k \cdot a_1, k \cdot a_2>$
    *   If *k* < 0:  *k*$\vec{v} = < -k \cdot a_1, -k \cdot a_2>$

**Worked Example 3:**

Given $\vec{w} = <5, 3>$ and *k* = -2, find -2$\vec{w}$.

*   **Solution:**
    -2$\vec{w} = -2 <5, 3> = <-10, -6>$

### 4. Vector Magnitude and Direction

*   **Vector Magnitude (||$\vec{v}$||):**  The magnitude of a vector is its length, calculated using the Pythagorean theorem.
    *   2D:  ||$\vec{v}$|| = $\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$
    *   3D:  ||$\vec{v}$|| = $\sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}$
*   **Direction Angle (θ):** The direction angle is the angle between the vector and the positive x-axis. It can be calculated using the arctangent function:
    *   $\theta = \arctan(\frac{y}{x})$  (for 2D)
    *   $\theta = \arctan(\frac{z}{x})$  (for 3D)

**Worked Example 4:**

Given $\vec{r} = <2, 1>$, calculate its magnitude and direction angle.

*   **Solution:**
    *   Magnitude: ||$\vec{r}$|| = $\sqrt{2^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{5}$
    *   Direction Angle: $\theta = \arctan(\frac{1}{2}) \approx 26.57^\circ$

### 5. Vector Operations and Commutativity

*   **Commutativity:** Vector addition is commutative, meaning $\vec{a} + \vec{b} = \vec{b} + \vec{a}$.  This is crucial to remember for simplifying calculations.
*   **Scalar Product (Dot Product):**  The dot product of two vectors $\vec{a} = <a_1, a_2>$ and $\vec{b} = <b_1, b_2>$ is given by:  $\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = a_1b_1 + a_2b_2$. The dot product results in a scalar value.
*   **Vector Product (Cross Product):**  (Typically introduced in later IB courses, but understanding the concept is beneficial). The cross product is only defined in 3D space and results in a new vector perpendicular to both original vectors.

### 6. Applications of Vectors

> **Example:** Consider a boat traveling at a speed of 10 m/s at an angle of 30° with the shoreline.  The **velocity vector** represents the boat's motion.

*   Vectors are used extensively in physics to describe motion, forces, and equilibrium.
*   They are also used in computer graphics and game development to represent and manipulate objects in 3D space.
