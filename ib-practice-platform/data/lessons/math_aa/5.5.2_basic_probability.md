## Overview

Probability is the mathematical study of randomness and uncertainty. It provides a framework for quantifying the likelihood of events occurring in experiments and real-world situations. In IB Mathematics: Analysis and Approaches, probability forms the foundation for understanding statistics, distributions, and inferential methods. This lesson introduces the fundamental concepts of probability, from basic definitions through the rules governing how probabilities combine.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you should be able to:
- Define sample spaces and events
- Calculate theoretical and empirical probabilities
- Apply the addition and multiplication rules for probability
- Use conditional probability and independence concepts
- Solve problems involving mutually exclusive and independent events
- Apply Bayes''' theorem in probability calculations

## Key Concepts

- **Sample Space (Ω)**: The set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment
- **Event (E)**: Any subset of the sample space; a collection of one or more outcomes
- **Probability**: A numerical measure between 0 and 1 representing the likelihood of an event
- **Mutually Exclusive Events**: Events that cannot occur simultaneously (P(A ∩ B) = 0)
- **Independent Events**: Events where the occurrence of one does not affect the probability of the other
- **Conditional Probability**: The probability of an event occurring given that another event has already occurred

## Detailed Explanations

### 1. Sample Spaces and Events

A **sample space** is the complete set of all possible outcomes when conducting a random experiment. For example:
- Rolling a die: Ω = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}
- Tossing a coin: Ω = {Heads, Tails}
- Selecting a card from a standard deck: Ω has 52 elements

An **event** is any subset of the sample space.

**Example 1.1**: For rolling a fair six-sided die, if event E = "rolling an even number," then E = {2, 4, 6}.

### 2. Classical (Theoretical) Probability

For equally likely outcomes, the probability of an event E is:

$$P(E) = \frac{\text{Number of favorable outcomes}}{\text{Total number of possible outcomes}} = \frac{|E|}{|Ω|}$$

**Example 2.1**: When rolling a fair die, the probability of rolling a 4 is:
$$P(\text{rolling a 4}) = \frac{1}{6}$$

**Example 2.2**: The probability of rolling an even number (2, 4, or 6) is:
$$P(\text{even}) = \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2}$$

### 3. Addition Rule for Probability

For any two events A and B:

$$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$$

**For mutually exclusive events** (A ∩ B = ∅):
$$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)$$

**Example 3.1**: Drawing a card from a standard deck. What is P(drawing a King OR a Red card)?
- P(King) = 4/52
- P(Red) = 26/52  
- P(King AND Red) = 2/52 (2 red kings)
- P(King OR Red) = 4/52 + 26/52 - 2/52 = 28/52 = 7/13

**Example 3.2**: Rolling a die. What is P(rolling a 2 OR rolling a 5)?
These are mutually exclusive events, so:
$$P(2 \text{ or } 5) = P(2) + P(5) = \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{3}$$

### 4. Multiplication Rule for Probability

For two events A and B:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) × P(B|A)$$

**For independent events** (P(B|A) = P(B)):
$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) × P(B)$$

**Example 4.1**: Rolling a die twice. What is P(first roll is 4 AND second roll is 5)?
These are independent events, so:
$$P(4 \text{ then } 5) = P(4) × P(5) = \frac{1}{6} × \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{36}$$

### 5. Conditional Probability

The conditional probability of event B given that event A has occurred is:

$$P(B|A) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A)}$$ (provided P(A) > 0)

**Example 5.1**: In a group of 100 students, 45 study Math, 30 study Physics, and 15 study both. If we randomly select a student who studies Physics, what is the probability they also study Math?

- P(Math AND Physics) = 15/100
- P(Physics) = 30/100
- P(Math | Physics) = (15/100)/(30/100) = 15/30 = 1/2

### 6. Independence

Two events A and B are **independent** if and only if:
$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) × P(B)$$

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Compound Events

**Problem**: From a standard deck, two cards are drawn without replacement. What is P(both are hearts)?

**Solution**:
- P(1st is heart) = 13/52
- P(2nd is heart | 1st was heart) = 12/51
- P(both hearts) = (13/52) × (12/51) = 156/2652 = 12/204 = 1/17

### Example 2: Addition Rule Application

**Problem**: In a class of 30 students, 18 like pizza, 12 like pasta, and 5 like both. What is P(a randomly selected student likes pizza OR pasta)?

**Solution**:
- |Pizza| = 18, |Pasta| = 12, |Both| = 5
- |Pizza OR Pasta| = 18 + 12 - 5 = 25
- P(Pizza OR Pasta) = 25/30 = 5/6

### Example 3: Conditional Probability

**Problem**: In a survey, 60% like sport, 40% like music, and 30% like both. If someone likes music, what is the probability they also like sport?

**Solution**:
$$P(\text{Sport|Music}) = \frac{P(\text{Sport AND Music})}{P(\text{Music})} = \frac{0.30}{0.40} = 0.75 = 75\%$$

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1**: P(A|B) = P(B|A) - These are different unless special conditions exist.

**Misconception 2**: Independent events must be mutually exclusive - False; they are opposite concepts.

**Misconception 3**: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) always - Only true when A and B are mutually exclusive.

**Misconception 4**: Past independent events affect future outcomes - The "gambler''' fallacy"; each trial has the same probability.

## Real-World Applications

**Medical Testing**: Doctors use probability to interpret test results. A positive test doesn't guarantee disease - probability depends on prevalence and test accuracy.

**Insurance**: Premiums are calculated using probability of claims based on historical data.

**Quality Control**: Manufacturers use probability sampling to determine if batches meet standards.

**Weather Forecasting**: "40% chance of rain" represents predicted probability of measurable precipitation.

**Sports Analytics**: Teams use probability analysis to predict outcomes and optimize strategy.

## Summary

**Key Formulas**:
- $$P(E) = \frac{\text{favorable}}{\text{total}}$$
- $$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$$
- $$P(A \cap B) = P(A) × P(B)$$ (for independent events)
- $$P(B|A) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A)}$$

**Key Concepts**:
- All probabilities are between 0 and 1
- P(A') = 1 - P(A) where A''' is the complement
- Distinguish independent from mutually exclusive
- Conditional probability accounts for information about related events

## Exam Tips

**1. Identify the Sample Space First**: Clearly define all possible outcomes.

**2. Distinguish "And" vs "Or"**: And requires multiplication for independent events; Or requires addition with overlap adjustment.

**3. Watch "Without Replacement"**: Subsequent probabilities change when items aren''' replaced.

**4. Use Tree Diagrams**: Clearly show all paths and multiply probabilities along branches.

**5. Check Answers for Reasonableness**: Probabilities must be between 0 and 1.

**6. Learn the Language**: "At least one" = 1 - P(none); "Either...or" typically means inclusive OR.
