## Lesson: Conjugate Roots of Polynomials

## Overview

This lesson explores the concept of **conjugate roots** within the context of polynomial equations, focusing on understanding why they arise and how to find them. We'll delve into complex numbers and their role in solving polynomial equations that don’t have real roots.

## Key Concepts

*   Understanding the relationship between the roots of a polynomial and its factors.
*   The role of complex numbers in solving polynomial equations.
*   The theorem that if a polynomial with *real* coefficients has a complex root, then its conjugate must also be a root.
*   Methods for finding conjugate roots.

### Subtopic 1: Roots and Factors

> **Important:** The **Zero Product Property** states that if the product of two or more factors is zero, then at least one of the factors must be zero. This is a fundamental concept for solving polynomial equations.

Let's consider the polynomial equation:
$x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0$

We can factor this equation as:
$(x - 2)(x - 3) = 0$

Therefore, the **roots** of this equation are $x = 2$ and $x = 3$.  These roots are directly linked to the **factors** $(x - 2)$ and $(x - 3)$.  We can represent the polynomial as:
$P(x) = (x - 2)(x - 3)$

The roots are the values of *x* that make the polynomial equal to zero.

**Example 1:**

Solve the equation $2x^2 + x - 1 = 0$.

We can factor this equation as:
$(2x - 1)(x + 1) = 0$

Therefore, the roots are $x = \frac{1}{2}$ and $x = -1$.  The factors are $(2x - 1)$ and $(x + 1)$.

### Subtopic 2: Introduction to Complex Numbers

> **Note:** Complex numbers are numbers of the form *a + bi*, where *a* and *b* are real numbers, and *i* is the **imaginary unit**, defined as $i = \sqrt{-1}$.

For now, we'll primarily work with complex numbers that have a purely imaginary part (i.e., *b* ≠ 0).  Complex numbers are essential for solving polynomial equations where the roots are not real.

### Subtopic 3: The Conjugate Root Theorem

> **Exam Tip:** The Conjugate Root Theorem is a cornerstone of this topic and frequently appears in IB exams.

The **Conjugate Root Theorem** states: *If a polynomial with **real** coefficients has a complex root *a + bi*, then its conjugate, *a - bi*, must also be a root.*

> **Example:** If $x - 2i$ is a root of a polynomial with real coefficients, then its conjugate, $2i$, must also be a root.

Let's look at an example of a polynomial equation.
$x^2 + 1 = 0$

This can be rewritten as:
$x^2 + 0x + 1 = 0$

The roots are $x = i$ and $x = -i$. Notice that *i* and *-i* are complex conjugates.

### Subtopic 4: Finding Conjugate Roots – Worked Example 1

**Example 2:**

Find the conjugate roots of the polynomial $x^2 - 4x + 5 = 0$.

We can use the **Quadratic Formula** to find the roots:
$x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$
In this case, $a = 1$, $b = -4$, and $c = 5$.
$x = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{(-4)^2 - 4(1)(5)}}{2(1)} = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{16 - 20}}{2} = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{-4}}{2} = \frac{4 \pm 2i}{2} = 2 \pm i$

Therefore, the roots are $2 + i$ and $2 - i$. The conjugate roots are $2 + i$ and $2 - i$.

### Subtopic 5: Finding Conjugate Roots – Worked Example 2

**Example 3:**

Given that $x = 1 + 2i$ is a root of a polynomial with real coefficients, find the other root.

According to the Conjugate Root Theorem, if $1 + 2i$ is a root, then its conjugate, $1 - 2i$, must also be a root.

### Subtopic 6: Applications and Further Considerations (HL Extension)

> **HL Note:** At the Higher Level, students also investigate the relationship between the roots and the coefficients of a polynomial. This often involves using Vieta's formulas.

The polynomial with roots $1 + 2i$ and $1 - 2i$ is:
$(x - (1 + 2i))(x - (1 - 2i)) = ((x - 1) - 2i)((x - 1) + 2i) = (x - 1)^2 - (2i)^2 = x^2 - 2x + 1 - (-4) = x^2 - 2x + 5$

This polynomial has real coefficients and has the specified complex roots.
