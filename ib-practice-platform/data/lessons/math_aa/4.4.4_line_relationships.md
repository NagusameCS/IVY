## Lesson: Line Relationships – Vectors

## Overview

This lesson focuses on understanding and applying the concept of **displacement** and **velocity** within the context of vectors, a core element of 4.4.4. Students will learn to calculate these quantities, interpret them graphically, and solve problems involving motion.

## Key Concepts

*   Understanding vectors as representing magnitude and direction.
*   Calculating displacement and velocity, recognizing their relationship to distance and time.
*   Graphical representation of vectors and their components.
*   Applying vector concepts to solve problems involving linear motion.

### 1. Introduction to Vectors

> **Important:** Vectors are mathematical representations of quantities that have both magnitude (size) and direction. Unlike scalars, which only have magnitude, vectors are defined by their components along different axes.

Vectors are often represented graphically as arrows. The length of the arrow represents the **magnitude** of the vector, and the direction of the arrow represents the **direction** of the vector. We'll be working primarily with two-dimensional vectors in this lesson.

Let's consider a simple example. Imagine a person walking 5 meters East and then 3 meters North. We can represent this movement using a vector.

**Example 1:**

Let’s represent the 5 meters East movement with a vector  **a** = <5, 0> and the 3 meters North movement with a vector **b** = <0, 3>. The resultant displacement is then the vector sum: **a + b** = <5, 0> + <0, 3> = <5, 3>. This vector represents the overall displacement.

### 2. Displacement

> **Note:** Displacement is the change in position of an object. It is a vector quantity and is independent of the path taken.

**Definition:** Displacement is the shortest distance between an object's initial and final positions, along with its direction.

**Formula:** Displacement (Δ**r**) = Final Position - Initial Position

*   We often express displacement in terms of components along coordinate axes.

**Example 2:**

A particle moves from point A (2, 1) to point B (5, 4). Calculate the displacement vector.

*   Δ**r** = **r**~AB~ = (5, 4) - (2, 1) = (5-2, 4-1) = <3, 3>

This means the particle moved 3 units in the x-direction and 3 units in the y-direction.

### 3. Velocity

> **Exam Tip:**  Velocity is a vector quantity, and its magnitude represents speed, while its direction is crucial.

**Definition:** Velocity is the rate of change of an object's position with respect to time.  It’s the displacement over time.

**Formula:** Velocity (v) = Displacement (Δ**r**) / Time (Δt)

*   Velocity can be expressed as a scalar (speed) or a vector (including direction).

**Example 3:**

A car travels 100 meters East in 10 seconds. Calculate the car’s velocity.

*   First, find the average speed: speed = distance / time = 100m / 10s = 10 m/s
*   Then, find the velocity vector: **v** = <10, 0> m/s (East)

### 4. Graphical Representation of Vectors

> **Example:** Consider a vector **v** = <3, 4>. This indicates that the vector's x-component is 3 and its y-component is 4.  The magnitude of the vector is √(3² + 4²) = 5.

Vectors can be represented graphically using components.  The x-component represents the horizontal component, and the y-component represents the vertical component.

**Calculating Magnitude and Direction:**

*   **Magnitude:** Using the Pythagorean theorem:  |**v**| = √(v~x~² + v~y~²)
*   **Direction:** Using the arctangent function (tan^{-1}): θ = tan^{-1}(v~y~ / v~x~)  (Note:  Make sure your calculator is in degree mode!)

**Example 4:**

Given the vector **v** = <4, 3>, calculate its magnitude and direction.

*   Magnitude: |**v**| = √(4² + 3²) = √25 = 5
*   Direction: θ = tan^{-1}(3 / 4) ≈ 36.87°

This means the vector is 5 units long and points approximately 36.87° above the x-axis.

### 5. Resolving Vectors into Components

> **Note:**  This technique is vital for breaking down a vector into its horizontal (x) and vertical (y) components, which simplifies calculations.

To resolve a vector into components, you use trigonometry:

*   v~x~ = |**v**| * cos(θ)
*   v~y~ = |**v**| * sin(θ)

Where |**v**| is the magnitude of the vector and θ is its angle with respect to the x-axis.

**Example 5:**

A boat is traveling at a speed of 15 m/s at an angle of 60° with respect to the east. Resolve the velocity vector into its x and y components.

*   v~x~ = 15 * cos(60°) = 15 * 0.5 = 7.5 m/s
*   v~y~ = 15 * sin(60°) = 15 * 0.866 ≈ 12.99 m/s

Therefore, the boat's velocity has components of 7.5 m/s East and 12.99 m/s North.

### 6. Application: Combining Vectors

> **Exam Tip:**  When combining vectors, use the parallelogram law or vector addition rules.

*   **Head-to-Tail Method:**  Draw the vectors with their tails connected. The resultant vector is the vector from the tail of the first vector to the head of the second vector.
*   **Component Method:** Add the x-components and y-components separately.

**Example 6:**

A runner takes 3 seconds to run 5 meters North and then 2 seconds to run 4 meters East. What is the runner's resultant displacement?

*   North: **a** = <0, 5> m/s * 3s = <0, 15> m
*   East: **b** = <4, 0> m/s * 2s = <8, 0> m
*   Resultant: **a + b** = <0, 15> m + <8, 0> m = <8, 15> m
