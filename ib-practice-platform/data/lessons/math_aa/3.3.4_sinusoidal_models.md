## Sinusoidal Models

## Overview

This lesson focuses on understanding and applying sinusoidal functions to model periodic phenomena. Students will learn to identify key features of sinusoidal graphs, solve related equations, and analyze real-world applications.

### 3.3.4.1 Introduction to Sinusoidal Functions (A.1.1)

> **Important:** Sinusoidal functions are characterized by repeating patterns, which are essential for modeling cyclical phenomena.

Sinusoidal functions are mathematical representations of periodic phenomena like waves, oscillations, and tides. They are defined by the general form:
$$y = A \sin(Bx + C) + D$$
where:
*   **A** is the **amplitude**: the maximum displacement from the midline.
*   **B** determines the **period**: the length of one complete cycle.  The period (T) is given by: $T = \frac{2\pi}{|B|}$.
*   **C** determines the **phase shift**: a horizontal translation of the graph.
*   **D** is the **vertical shift**: a vertical translation of the graph.

**Example 1:**
Consider the function $y = 3 \sin(2x - \frac{\pi}{2}) + 1$.
*   A = 3
*   B = 2
*   C = -$\frac{\pi}{2}$
*   D = 1

Let's analyze this function. The amplitude is 3, the period is $\frac{2\pi}{2} = \pi$, the phase shift is $-\frac{\pi}{2}$, and the vertical shift is 1.  The graph will be shifted to the right by $\frac{\pi}{2}$ and have a midline at $y = 1$.

### 3.3.4.2 Identifying Key Features of Sinusoidal Graphs

> **Exam Tip:**  Carefully sketching graphs based on given equations is a crucial skill for the IB exam.

To identify the features of a sinusoidal graph, students can use the following steps:
1.  **Determine the Amplitude (A):**  Find the distance from the midline to the maximum or minimum point.
2.  **Determine the Period (T):** Calculate $T = \frac{2\pi}{|B|}$.
3.  **Determine the Phase Shift (C/B):**  Determine the horizontal shift of the graph.
4.  **Determine the Vertical Shift (D):** Determine the vertical translation of the graph.

**Example 2:**
Given the equation $y = -2\cos(3x) + 2$, identify the key features.
*   A = 2
*   B = 3
*   C = 0
*   D = 2

The amplitude is 2, the period is $\frac{2\pi}{3}$, the phase shift is 0, and the vertical shift is 2. The graph will have a midline at $y = 2$.

### 3.3.4.3 Modeling Periodic Phenomena (A.1.1)

> **Note:** Many real-world phenomena exhibit sinusoidal behavior.

Sinusoidal functions are often used to model:
*   **Tides:**  The height of the tide throughout the day.
*   **Temperature:** The daily temperature fluctuations.
*   **Sound Waves:** The pressure variations in sound.
*   **Electrical Signals:** The voltage variations in AC circuits.

To model a phenomenon, we need to understand its cycle length (period) and the maximum and minimum values it reaches.

**Example 3:**
A buoy oscillates with a period of 6 seconds. Its height above sea level, *h* (in meters), is given by the equation:
$h = 12\sin(\frac{2\pi}{6}t)$
where *t* is the time in seconds.

Here,  $T = 6$, $A = 12$, $B = \frac{2\pi}{6} = \frac{\pi}{3}$, $C = 0$, and $D = 0$.  The buoy oscillates between a maximum height of 12 meters and a minimum height of 0 meters.

### 3.3.4.4 Solving Sinusoidal Equations

> **Exam Tip:** Practice solving equations with different forms of sinusoidal functions.  Be mindful of using radians or degrees depending on the given values.

Solving sinusoidal equations involves isolating the variable. We can use algebraic manipulation and trigonometric identities to solve for the variable.

**Example 4:**
Solve for *t* in the equation $h = 12\sin(\frac{2\pi}{6}t)$:
$h = 12\sin(\frac{\pi}{3}t)$
$\frac{h}{12} = \sin(\frac{\pi}{3}t)$
$\arcsin(\frac{h}{12}) = \frac{\pi}{3}t$
$t = \frac{3}{\pi}\arcsin(\frac{h}{12})$

This equation gives the time *t* in seconds it takes for the buoy to reach a specific height *h*. Note the units!

### 3.3.4.5 Phase Shift & Amplitude Modifications (HL Extension)

> **HL Extension:** Students should be able to manipulate phase shifts and amplitude to analyze and predict changes in sinusoidal patterns.

The general form  $y = A \sin(Bx + C) + D$  allows for alterations in the phase shift and amplitude. Understanding how these changes affect the graph is crucial. Shifting the graph to the left changes the sign of C, while scaling the amplitude changes the value of A.

### 3.3.4.6  Applications in Physics (A.1.1)

> **Example:**  Modeling Simple Harmonic Motion.

Consider a mass attached to a spring, oscillating with a period *T*. The displacement *x* of the mass from its equilibrium position is given by:

$x(t) = A \cos(\omega t) + B \sin(\omega t)$

where $\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T}$.  This equation represents the motion of a particle undergoing simple harmonic motion.  *A* and *B* are constants determined by the initial conditions.
