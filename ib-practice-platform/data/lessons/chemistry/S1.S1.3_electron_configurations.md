## Overview

This lesson delves into the fundamental concept of **electron configurations**, exploring how the arrangement of electrons within atoms dictates their chemical properties. We will examine the rules governing these configurations and how they relate to the periodic table.

## Key Concepts

*   Understanding the Aufbau principle and Hund’s rule.
*   Determining electron configurations for atoms, ions, and noble gases.
*   Relating electron configurations to the periodic table and trends in reactivity.
*   Applying the concept of electron configurations to predict chemical reactions.

### 1. The Aufbau Principle

> **Important:** The **Aufbau principle** states that electrons first fill up the lowest energy orbitals before filling higher energy orbitals. It’s a fundamental rule guiding the formation of electron configurations.

The **energy levels** or **orbitals** around an atom are arranged in a specific order, starting with the lowest energy (closest to the nucleus) and increasing as you move outwards. These energy levels are labeled with principal quantum numbers (n), where *n* = 1, 2, 3, and so on. Each principal energy level contains subshells designated by letters: s, p, d, and f.

*   s orbitals hold up to 2 electrons.
*   p orbitals hold up to 6 electrons.
*   d orbitals hold up to 10 electrons.
*   f orbitals hold up to 14 electrons.

We fill the orbitals in order of increasing energy, starting with the 1s orbital.  This process dictates the electron configuration.

**Example:** Consider hydrogen (atomic number 1). It needs one electron to complete its configuration. Following the Aufbau principle, this electron occupies the 1s orbital.  Its electron configuration is 1s^{1}.

### 2. Hund’s Rule

> **Note:**  **Hund’s rule** states that within a subshell, electrons will individually occupy each orbital before doubling up in any one orbital. This minimizes electron-electron repulsion.

Within a given subshell (e.g., a p subshell), electrons will first fill each orbital singly before any orbital receives a second electron. This rule explains why we often see configurations like 2p^{3} rather than 2p^{4}.

**Example:** Consider nitrogen (atomic number 7).  It needs 5 electrons. Its electron configuration is 1s^{2} 2s^{2} 2p^{3}.  This adheres to Hund’s rule because the three 2p electrons are each placed in separate p orbitals before any single p orbital becomes doubly occupied.

### 3. Determining Electron Configurations

Now, let’s look at how we determine electron configurations for different types of atoms.

#### 3.1. Neutral Atoms

To determine the electron configuration of a neutral atom, you simply add up the atomic number of the element to the number of electrons.

**Example 1:**  Magnesium (Mg) has an atomic number of 12, meaning it has 12 electrons. Its electron configuration is 1s^{2} 2s^{2} 2p^{6}.

**Example 2:** Oxygen (O) has an atomic number of 8. Its electron configuration is 1s^{2} 2s^{2} 2p^{4}.

#### 3.2. Ions

For ions, you need to consider the charge of the ion.

**Example:** Sodium (Na) forms the Na^{+} ion. It has an atomic number of 11, so it has 11 electrons.  The +1 charge indicates that one electron has been removed. Therefore, the electron configuration of Na^{+} is 1s^{2} 2s^{2} 2p^{6}.

**Example:** Chlorine (Cl) forms the Cl^{-} ion.  It has an atomic number of 17, so it has 17 electrons. The -1 charge indicates that one electron has been added. Therefore, the electron configuration of Cl^{-} is 1s^{2} 2s^{2} 2p^{6} 3s^{2}.

#### 3.3 Noble Gases

Noble gases have completely filled electron shells, giving them very stable configurations. This is why they are unreactive.

**Example:** Neon (Ne) has an atomic number of 10. Its electron configuration is 1s^{2} 2s^{2} 2p^{6}. This is a completely filled shell, making it a noble gas.

### 4. Electron Configurations and the Periodic Table

The arrangement of elements on the periodic table is directly related to their electron configurations. Elements in the same group have similar valence electron configurations, which leads to similar chemical properties.

> **Exam Tip:**  Understanding the periodicity of electron configurations is crucial for predicting chemical reactions and understanding trends in reactivity.

### 5. Filling Orbitals: A Step-by-Step Guide

Let's work through a more complex example: Potassium (K) – Atomic Number 19

1.  **Start with the 1s orbital:** 1s^{2}
2.  **Move to the 2s orbital:** 2s^{2}
3.  **Move to the 2p orbital:** 2p^{6} – This fills the 2p subshell completely.
4.  **Move to the 3s orbital:** 3s^{1} – This fills the 3s orbital.
5.  **Final Configuration:** 1s^{2} 2s^{2} 2p^{6} 3s^{1}.  The electron configuration of Potassium is 1s^{2} 2s^{2} 2p^{6} 3s^{1}.

### 6. Shortcuts and Mnemonics

Several shortcuts and mnemonics can assist in remembering electron configurations:

*   **CAS (Aufbau Series):**  1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, 5s, 4d, 5p, etc.
*   **Octet Rule:**  Many atoms strive to achieve an octet (8 electrons) of valence electrons in their outermost shell.
