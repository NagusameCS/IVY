## Rate Expressions and Mechanisms

## Overview

This lesson delves into the quantitative relationships governing reaction rates – rate expressions – and explores the concept of reaction mechanisms, providing a framework for understanding *why* reactions proceed at specific speeds. We'll investigate how factors like concentration, temperature, and catalysts influence reaction rates and how mechanisms explain the step-by-step process of chemical change.

## Key Concepts

*   Understanding **rate expressions** (rate = k[A]^m[B]^n) and applying them to different reactions.
*   Exploring the concept of **reaction mechanisms** and their role in determining reaction rates.
*   Analyzing the effect of **catalysts** on reaction rates.
*   Relating **activation energy** to reaction rates.
*   Applying **collision theory** to explain rate relationships.

### 1. Rate Expressions – Quantifying Reaction Rates

> **Important:** The rate of a reaction is defined as the change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit time. It’s crucial to understand that reaction rates aren't just about how fast something happens, but *how much* it happens.

The general rate expression for a reaction is:

```
rate = k[A]^m[B]^n
```

Where:

*   **rate** is the reaction rate (typically in mol/dm³/s)
*   **k** is the **rate constant**, which is temperature-dependent (higher T, higher k).
*   **[A]** and **[B]** are the concentrations of reactants A and B (typically in mol/dm²).
*   **m** and **n** are the **orders of reaction** with respect to A and B, respectively. These are determined experimentally.

**Worked Example 1:**

Consider the reaction: 2N₂O₅(g) → 4NO₂(g) + O₂(g)

The initial rate is determined to be 2.5 x 10⁻³ mol/dm³/s.  After increasing the concentration of N₂O₅ by a factor of 2, the rate increases to 1.25 x 10⁻² mol/dm³/s.

*   **Step 1:** Determine the order with respect to N₂O₅ (m). If the rate is directly proportional to [N₂O₅], then m = 1.  If the rate is proportional to [N₂O₅]², then m = 2, and so on.
*   **Step 2:** Calculate k.
    *   Initial Rate = k[N₂O₅]ᵐ
    *   2.5 x 10⁻³ = k[N₂O₅]¹
    *   k = 2.5 x 10⁻³ mol/dm³/s
    *   New Rate = k[N₂O₅]ᵐ
    *   1.25 x 10⁻² = k[N₂O₅]¹
    *   k = 1.25 x 10⁻² mol/dm³/s
    *   Since k is constant, we can compare these two values: 2.5 x 10⁻³ = 1.25 x 10⁻²  -> **ERROR!** The rate isn’t directly proportional to N₂O₅.

    *   Let’s assume the rate is proportional to [N₂O₅]².  Then:  2.5 x 10⁻³ = k[N₂O₅]²  and 1.25 x 10⁻² = k[N₂O₅]²  Solving for k: k = (2.5 x 10⁻³)/( [N₂O₅]²)  and k = (1.25 x 10⁻²) / ([N₂O₅]²)  ->  (2.5 x 10⁻³)/( [N₂O₅]²)  = (1.25 x 10⁻²)/( [N₂O₅]²)  ->  2.5 x 10⁻³ = 1.25 x 10⁻² which is true. Thus, the reaction is second order with respect to N₂O₅ (m = 2).

**Worked Example 2:**

The reaction of H₂ and I₂ is 5H₂(g) + I₂(g) → 5HI(g).  At 400 K, the rate constant k = 3.0 x 10⁵ s⁻¹.  Determine the rate expression.

*   The stoichiometry suggests that the rate expression is: rate = k[H₂]⁵[I₂]
*   Substituting the value of k: rate = (3.0 x 10⁵ s⁻¹)[H₂]⁵[I₂]
*   Therefore, the rate expression is: rate = (3.0 x 10⁵ s⁻¹)[H₂]⁵[I₂]

### 2. Reaction Mechanisms – The Step-by-Step Process

> **Note:** Reaction mechanisms are proposed explanations for how a reaction occurs. They consist of a series of elementary steps, each with its own rate constant.

A **mechanism** describes the actual sequence of elementary steps that a reaction proceeds through.  It's essential to understand that the overall rate expression is determined by the *slowest* step in the mechanism, known as the **rate-determining step**.

For example, a reaction might proceed through a two-step mechanism:

Step 1: A + B → C  (Rate = k₁[A][B])
Step 2: C → D  (Rate = k₂[C])

The overall rate of the reaction is determined by Step 2 because it has the smallest rate constant (k₂).

### 3. Catalysts – Speeding Up Reactions

> **Example:** Enzymes are biological catalysts that significantly accelerate biochemical reactions.

A **catalyst** is a substance that increases the rate of a reaction without being consumed in the overall process. Catalysts often provide an alternative reaction pathway with a lower **activation energy**.

### 4. Activation Energy and Reaction Rates

> **Important:** Activation energy (Ea) is the minimum energy required for a reaction to occur. Catalysts lower Ea, leading to faster reaction rates.

The relationship between activation energy and reaction rate is inverse: lower Ea = faster rate; higher Ea = slower rate.

### 5. Collision Theory – Explaining Reaction Rates

> **Note:** Collision theory states that for a reaction to occur, reactant molecules must collide with sufficient energy and in the correct orientation.

*   **Frequency of collisions:**  The more collisions, the faster the reaction rate.
*   **Energy of collisions:** Reactions require a minimum energy (activation energy) for effective collisions.
*   **Orientation:** Molecules must collide with the correct orientation for bonds to break and form.
