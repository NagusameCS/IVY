## Entropy and Spontaneity

## Overview

This lesson explores the thermodynamic concept of **entropy** and its crucial role in determining the spontaneity of chemical reactions. We’ll investigate how entropy relates to disorder and randomness, and how this, alongside enthalpy, dictates whether a reaction will proceed forward or reverse.

## Key Concepts

*   **Entropy (S):** A measure of disorder or randomness within a system.
*   **Spontaneity:** Whether a reaction will occur without external input.
*   **Gibbs Free Energy (G):** A thermodynamic potential that combines enthalpy and entropy to predict spontaneity.
*   **ΔG = ΔH - TΔS:** The fundamental equation governing spontaneity.

### Subtopic 1: Introduction to Entropy

> **Important:** Entropy is fundamentally about the number of possible arrangements (microstates) a system can occupy.  A system with more possible arrangements has higher entropy.

Entropy is a core concept in thermodynamics, often described as a measure of disorder or randomness. It’s *not* simply about the amount of “stuff” in a system; it’s about how that ‘stuff’ is arranged.  Consider these examples:

1.  **Ice melting:** Solid ice has a highly ordered structure. When it melts into liquid water, the molecules become more disordered, leading to an increase in entropy.
2.  **Gas expansion:** When a gas expands into a vacuum, the gas molecules spread out to fill the available space, increasing randomness and thus entropy.
3.  **Mixing:**  Mixing two or more substances always increases entropy because the resulting mixture is more disordered than the separated components.

The **International System of Units (SI)** for entropy is Joules per Kelvin (J/K).

### Subtopic 2: Entropy Change (ΔS)

The **change in entropy (ΔS)** quantifies the *amount* of disorder produced or consumed during a reaction.  

*   **ΔS > 0:**  The reaction increases entropy (more disorder).
*   **ΔS < 0:** The reaction decreases entropy (less disorder).
*   **ΔS = 0:** No change in entropy.

Many reactions increase entropy due to the formation of gases (gases have higher entropy than liquids or solids) or the breaking of strong intermolecular forces.  Solid → Liquid → Gas reactions almost always result in a positive ΔS.

> **Example:** Consider the decomposition of calcium carbonate (CaCO₃):

```
CaCO₃(s) → CaO(s) + CO₂(g)
```

The formation of gaseous CO₂ dramatically increases entropy (ΔS > 0).

### Subtopic 3: The Second Law of Thermodynamics

> **Important:** The Second Law of Thermodynamics states that the total entropy of an *isolated* system can only increase over time. This implies that spontaneous processes tend to proceed in a direction that increases the overall disorder of the universe.

This doesn’t mean that *all* reactions are spontaneous. It means that for a reaction to be spontaneous, the *net* change in entropy must be positive. It’s important to consider the *system* and its surroundings – the surroundings will invariably experience some degree of entropy increase.

### Subtopic 4: Gibbs Free Energy (G)

Gibbs Free Energy (G) combines enthalpy (H) and entropy (S) to determine the spontaneity of a reaction at a constant temperature and pressure. It’s defined as:

$$G = H - TΔS$$

Where:

*   G = Gibbs Free Energy (J/mol)
*   H = Enthalpy (J/mol)
*   T = Temperature in Kelvin (K)
*   ΔS = Change in entropy (J/K)

### Subtopic 5: Predicting Spontaneity with ΔG

The **change in Gibbs Free Energy (ΔG)** is the most useful indicator of spontaneity at constant temperature and pressure.

*   **ΔG < 0:** The reaction is spontaneous (favored).
*   **ΔG > 0:** The reaction is non-spontaneous (not favored).
*   **ΔG = 0:** The reaction is at equilibrium.

> **Exam Tip:** Remember that spontaneity is determined by the *change* in Gibbs Free Energy, not the absolute value.

### Subtopic 6: Calculating ΔG using ΔH and ΔS

We can calculate ΔG using the following equation:

$$ΔG = ΔH - TΔS$$

This equation is particularly useful when you know the enthalpy change (ΔH) and entropy change (ΔS) of a reaction and the temperature (T).  Typically, ΔH will be positive (endothermic) and ΔS will be positive (increase in disorder).

## Worked Examples

**Example 1:**

Consider the reaction:  N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) → 2NH₃(g)

At 298 K, ΔH = -92.2 kJ/mol and ΔS = -198.8 J/K.  Calculate ΔG and predict the spontaneity of the reaction.

1.  **Calculate ΔG:**
    $$ΔG = ΔH - TΔS$$
    $$ΔG = -92.2 kJ/mol - (298 K)(-198.8 J/K)  = -92.2 kJ/mol + 59160 J/mol$$
    $$ΔG = -92.2 kJ/mol + 59.16 kJ/mol = -33.04 kJ/mol$$
    Since ΔG is negative, the reaction is spontaneous at 298 K.

**Example 2:**

Consider the decomposition of dinitrogen tetroxide: 2NO₂(g) → 2NO(g) + O₂(g)

At 25°C (298 K), ΔH = 0.84 kJ/mol and ΔS = 0.33 J/K. Calculate ΔG and predict spontaneity.

1.  **Calculate ΔG:**
    $$ΔG = ΔH - TΔS$$
    $$ΔG = 0.84 kJ/mol - (298 K)(0.33 J/K) = 0.84 kJ/mol - 98.34 J/K$$
    $$ΔG = 0.84 kJ/mol - 0.098 kJ/mol = 0.742 kJ/mol$$
    Since ΔG is positive, the reaction is non-spontaneous at 25°C.  It would require energy input to proceed.

**Example 3 (HL Extension):**

Calculate the entropy change (ΔS) for the following reaction at 298K, given that the standard molar entropy of water (liquid) is 70.7 J/mol·K, the standard molar entropy of ice is 56.6 J/mol·K, and the standard molar entropy of steam is 43.9 J/mol·K.

```
H₂O(s) → H₂O(l)
```

1.  **Use the definition of ΔS:**  ΔS = H~f~ - H~p~, where H~f~ is the enthalpy of formation of the product (liquid water) and H~p~ is the enthalpy of formation of the reactant (ice). The entropy change is associated with the change in state.
2.  **Calculate:** ΔS = 70.7 J/mol·K - 56.6 J/mol·K = 14.1 J/mol·K

## Summary

Entropy, a measure of disorder, plays a central role in determining the spontaneity of chemical reactions. The second law of thermodynamics dictates that the entropy of an isolated system increases over time.  Gibbs Free Energy (G), calculated using the equation ΔG = ΔH - TΔS, provides a more nuanced approach to predicting spontaneity by combining enthalpy and entropy.  A negative ΔG indicates a spontaneous reaction at a given temperature, signifying a net increase in the system’s disorder. Understanding these concepts is crucial for interpreting reaction pathways and predicting their behavior.
