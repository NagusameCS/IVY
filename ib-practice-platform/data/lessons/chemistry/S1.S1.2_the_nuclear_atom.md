## Lesson: The Nuclear Atom

## Overview

This lesson explores the fundamental structure of the atom, focusing on the nucleus and its constituents – protons, neutrons, and electrons. Students will learn about the concept of atomic number, mass number, isotopes, and how these relate to the periodic table.

## Key Concepts

*   Students will understand the composition of the atom, including the nucleus and orbiting electrons.
*   Students will be able to define and calculate atomic number, mass number, and isotopic mass.
*   Students will recognize the significance of isotopes in various applications, including radioactive dating.
*   Students will connect atomic structure to the organization of the periodic table.

### Subtopic 1: Introduction to Atomic Structure

> **Important:** The atom was once considered the smallest indivisible particle of matter. However, through advancements in physics and chemistry, we now know that atoms are themselves composed of smaller subatomic particles.

The modern model of the atom, developed primarily through the work of Ernest Rutherford, Niels Bohr, and others, describes the atom as consisting of a central **nucleus** surrounded by orbiting **electrons**. The nucleus contains positively charged **protons** and neutral **neutrons**. Electrons are negatively charged particles that occupy specific energy levels or **orbitals** around the nucleus.

```
Atom: Nucleus (Protons + Neutrons) + Electrons
```

**Example 1:** *Describe the key components of the atomic model proposed by Rutherford.*

**Solution:** Rutherford’s model proposed that atoms are composed of a central, dense nucleus containing positively charged protons and neutral neutrons. Electrons orbit this nucleus at specific distances, similar to planets orbiting the sun. This model was based on the results of the gold foil experiment.

### Subtopic 2: Atomic Number and Mass Number

*   The **atomic number** (Z) is defined as the number of protons in the nucleus of an atom. It uniquely identifies an element. For example, all atoms with 6 protons are carbon atoms.
*   The **mass number** (A) is the total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus of an atom.  It's represented by the symbol *A*.
*   The **atomic mass** is the weighted average of the masses of all naturally occurring isotopes of an element, expressed as a decimal.

**Example 2:** *Calculate the atomic number and mass number of an atom with 17 protons and 20 neutrons.*

**Solution:**
*   Atomic Number (Z) = Number of Protons = 17
*   Mass Number (A) = Number of Protons + Number of Neutrons = 17 + 20 = 37
*   Atomic Mass = Average of the isotopic masses (which we don't need to calculate here, but would involve the natural abundances of each isotope).

### Subtopic 3: Isotopes

> **Note:** Isotopes of an element have the same number of protons (and therefore the same atomic number) but different numbers of neutrons. This results in different mass numbers.

Isotopes are variations of an element that have the same chemical properties but differ in their nuclear composition.  They are denoted with a superscript indicating the neutron number and a subscript indicating the proton number (e.g., ^{12}C, ^{14}C).

**Example 3:** *An atom has 8 protons and 8 neutrons. Is it a stable isotope? What is its mass number and atomic number?*

**Solution:** Yes, it is a stable isotope.
*   Atomic Number (Z) = Number of Protons = 8
*   Mass Number (A) = Number of Protons + Number of Neutrons = 8 + 8 = 16
*   The isotope is denoted as ^{16}O (Oxygen-16).

### Subtopic 4: Isotopic Abundance and Average Atomic Mass

*   Most elements exist as a mixture of isotopes. The **isotopic abundance** represents the percentage of each isotope present in a sample.
*   The **average atomic mass** of an element is calculated by weighting the atomic masses of its isotopes by their isotopic abundances.

```
Average Atomic Mass = (Isotope 1 Abundance × Isotope 1 Mass) + (Isotope 2 Abundance × Isotope 2 Mass) + ...
```

**Example 4:** *Carbon exists as two isotopes: Carbon-12 (^{12}C) with an abundance of 98% and Carbon-14 (^{14}C) with an abundance of 2%. Calculate the average atomic mass of carbon.*

**Solution:**
*   Average Atomic Mass = (0.98 × 12.00 amu) + (0.02 × 14.01 amu) = 11.76 amu + 0.28 amu = 12.04 amu

### Subtopic 5: Isotopes and the Periodic Table

> **Exam Tip:** Understanding isotopic abundance is crucial for interpreting data presented in the periodic table.

The periodic table displays the average atomic mass of each element, calculated based on the abundance of its isotopes. The atomic mass listed for each element in the periodic table is therefore a weighted average, reflecting the natural abundance of its isotopes.

### Subtopic 6: Applications of Isotopes

*   **Radioactive Dating:** The decay of radioactive isotopes, like Carbon-14, is utilized in radiocarbon dating to determine the age of organic materials.
*   **Medical Imaging:** Radioactive isotopes are used in medical imaging techniques like PET scans (Positron Emission Tomography) to diagnose diseases.
*   **Industrial Tracers:** Radioactive isotopes are employed as tracers in various industrial processes to monitor flow rates or chemical reactions.
