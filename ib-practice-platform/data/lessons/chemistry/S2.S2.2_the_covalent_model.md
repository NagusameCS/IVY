## The Covalent Model

## Overview

This lesson explores the covalent model of bonding, focusing on the sharing of electrons between atoms to achieve a stable electron configuration. We’ll investigate the properties of covalent compounds and relate them to their structure.

## Key Concepts

*   Understanding the formation of **covalent bonds** through electron sharing.
*   Explaining the concept of **Lewis structures** and their representation of covalent bonding.
*   Relating bond properties (e.g., polarity, bond length, bond strength) to molecular geometry.
*   Applying knowledge of covalent bonding to predict the properties of covalent compounds.

### 1. Introduction to Covalent Bonding

> **Important:** Covalent bonding arises when atoms, particularly nonmetals, are short of the eight electrons in their valence shell. Sharing electrons allows them to achieve a noble gas configuration – a stable electronic arrangement.

Covalent bonds are formed when atoms share valence electrons to achieve a full outer shell. Unlike ionic bonds, where electrons are transferred, covalent bonds involve a mutual sharing arrangement. This process is driven by the lower energy state achieved when electrons are closer to the nucleus.

**Example:** Consider hydrogen (H) and chlorine (Cl). Hydrogen has one valence electron, and chlorine has seven.  They form molecules like H~2~ and HCl, respectively, through covalent bonding.

```python
# Simplified representation of electron sharing
H: 1 electron to share
Cl: 7 electrons to share
H-Cl: 1+7 = 8 shared electrons -> stable octet for both
```

### 2. Lewis Structures

> **Exam Tip:** Mastery of drawing Lewis structures is crucial for understanding covalent bonding.

Lewis structures, developed by Gilbert N. Lewis, provide a visual representation of covalent bonds. They use lines to represent shared electron pairs between atoms.

**Steps to Draw a Lewis Structure:**

1.  **Count valence electrons:** Add the number of valence electrons of all atoms in the molecule.
2.  **Draw a skeletal structure:** Connect the atoms with single lines, placing the least electronegative atom(s) at the center.
3.  **Distribute remaining electrons as lone pairs:** Add lone pairs to atoms to satisfy the octet rule (except for hydrogen, which typically follows the duet rule).
4.  **Expand the octet around the central atom:**  The central atom will usually have the most bonding electrons.

**Example 1:** Drawing the Lewis structure for water (H~2~O)

1.  Valence electrons: H (1 x 2) + O (6) = 8 electrons
2.  Skeletal structure: H-O-H
3.  Distribute lone pairs:  Place two lone pairs on the oxygen atom.
4.  Octets: Each hydrogen atom has two electrons, fulfilling the duet rule.

**Example 2:** Drawing the Lewis structure for methane (CH~4~)

1.  Valence electrons: C (4) + 4H (1 x 4) = 8 electrons
2.  Skeletal structure: C-H-H-H-H
3.  Distribute lone pairs: Place four lone pairs around the carbon atom.
4.  Octets: Each hydrogen atom has one electron, fulfilling the duet rule.

### 3. Bond Length and Bond Strength

> **Note:** Bond length refers to the distance between the nuclei of two bonded atoms, while bond strength describes the energy required to break the bond.

*   **Bond Length:** Generally, the shorter the bond length, the stronger the bond. This is because the electrons are held more tightly between the nuclei. Covalent bonds are typically shorter than ionic bonds.
*   **Bond Strength:**  The strength of a covalent bond depends on factors like bond order (the number of shared electron pairs) and the electronegativity difference between the atoms. Greater electronegativity differences lead to stronger bonds.

**Example:** Consider the bonds in H~2~ vs. HCl. H-H is longer and weaker than H-Cl because of the greater electronegativity difference between H and Cl, leading to a more polarized bond and thus greater strength.

### 4. Polarity of Covalent Bonds

> **Example:** Consider the bond in HCl. Chlorine is significantly more electronegative than hydrogen, resulting in an unequal sharing of electrons.

Covalent bonds can be **polar** or **nonpolar** depending on the electronegativity difference between the bonded atoms.

*   **Polar Covalent Bonds:** Formed when there is a significant difference in electronegativity (typically > 0.4). The electrons are unequally shared, creating partial positive (δ+) and partial negative (δ-) charges on the atoms.
*   **Nonpolar Covalent Bonds:** Formed when the electronegativity difference is small (typically < 0.4). Electrons are shared equally between the atoms.

**Measuring Electronegativity Difference:** Use the Pauling scale (approximately) to estimate the difference in electronegativity between the bonded atoms.

### 5. Molecular Geometry and Bond Angles

> **Note:**  Molecular geometry significantly influences the properties of covalent compounds.

The shapes of molecules are determined by VSEPR (Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion) theory. This theory predicts the 3D arrangement of atoms in a molecule based on minimizing repulsion between electron pairs.

**Common Geometries:**

*   **Linear:** Bond angle of 180° (e.g., CO~2~)
*   **Trigonal Planar:** Bond angle of 120° (e.g., BF~3~)
*   **Tetrahedral:** Bond angle of approximately 109.5° (e.g., CH~4~)
*   **Bent:** Bond angle less than 109.5° (e.g., H~2~O)

### 6. Properties of Covalent Compounds

> **Exam Tip:** Relate molecular geometry to physical properties like melting point and boiling point.

The properties of covalent compounds are influenced by their structure:

*   **Melting and Boiling Points:** Generally lower than ionic compounds because intermolecular forces (e.g., van der Waals forces, hydrogen bonding) are weaker.
*   **Solubility:**  Covalent compounds are often insoluble in water due to weak intermolecular forces, but may be soluble in other organic solvents.
*   **Conductivity:** Generally poor conductors of electricity because they lack free-moving ions or electrons.

**Example:**  Water (H~2~O) has a relatively high boiling point due to hydrogen bonding, while methane (CH~4~) has a low boiling point because only weak London dispersion forces are present.
