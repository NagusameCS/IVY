## Lesson: How Fast? The Rate of Chemical Change

## Overview

This lesson investigates the factors that influence the **rate of reaction** in chemical changes. Students will explore how concentration, temperature, surface area, and catalysts affect reaction speed, applying these concepts to practical scenarios and preparing them for IB exam questions.

## Key Concepts

*   Understanding **reaction rate** as the change in concentration of reactants or products per unit time.
*   Investigating how concentration, temperature, surface area, and catalysts impact reaction rate.
*   Applying knowledge of kinetics to predict and control reaction rates.
*   Recognizing the importance of rate laws and their application in IB Chemistry.

### Subtopic 1: Defining Reaction Rate

> **Note:** Reaction rate is a quantitative measure of how quickly reactants are consumed or products are formed during a chemical reaction. It’s crucial to understand this definition before exploring the factors affecting it.

Reaction rate is typically expressed as the change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit of time. This is often represented by the following equation:

$$Rate = \frac{\Delta[Product]}{Δt} \text{ or } \frac{\Delta[Reactant]}{Δt}$$

Where:

*   **Rate** is the reaction rate (typically in mol/dm³/s or mol/J/s)
*   **Δ[Product]** is the change in concentration of the product
*   **Δt** is the change in time

**Example 1:**

Consider the reaction: 2NO(g) + O₂(g) → 2NO₂(g)

If the concentration of NO changes from 0.1 mol/dm³ to 0.05 mol/dm³ in 10 seconds, the reaction rate is:

$$Rate = \frac{0.05 - 0.1}{10} = -0.01 \text{ mol/dm³ / s}$$

*The negative sign indicates a decrease in concentration.*

**Example 2:**

Suppose the concentration of O₂ changes from 0.02 mol/dm³ to 0.04 mol/dm³ in 20 seconds. The reaction rate is:

$$Rate = \frac{0.04 - 0.02}{20} = 0.001 \text{ mol/dm³ / s}$$

**Exam Tip:** IB exam questions often require students to calculate reaction rates directly from experimental data.  Ensure a thorough understanding of how to interpret concentration changes and time intervals. (A.1.1 Rate of Reaction)

### Subtopic 2: Concentration and Reaction Rate

The rate of a reaction is directly proportional to the concentrations of the reactants. This relationship is fundamental to **rate laws**. Increasing the concentration of a reactant will generally increase the reaction rate.

**Example 3:**

Consider the reaction:  aA + bB → cC + dD

The rate law can be expressed as:

$$Rate = k[A]^m[B]^n$$

Where:

*   k is the rate constant
*   [A] and [B] are the concentrations of reactants A and B
*   m and n are the *orders* of the reaction with respect to A and B, respectively.

If ‘m’ = 1, the rate is directly proportional to [A]. If ‘m’ = 2, the rate is proportional to [A]², and so on.

*   **Important:** The *order* of a reaction is experimentally determined, not necessarily based on the stoichiometric coefficients.

### Subtopic 3: Temperature and Reaction Rate

Temperature has a significant impact on reaction rates. Generally, increasing the temperature increases the rate of reaction, and decreasing the temperature decreases the rate. This is because higher temperatures provide reactants with more kinetic energy, leading to more frequent and energetic collisions.

> **Example:** The Arrhenius equation describes the relationship between temperature and the rate constant (k):

$$k = A e^{-Ea/RT}$$

Where:

*   k is the rate constant
*   A is the pre-exponential factor
*   Ea is the activation energy
*   R is the gas constant (8.314 J/mol·K)
*   T is the absolute temperature (in Kelvin)

### Subtopic 4: Surface Area and Reaction Rate

The rate of reactions involving solids is often dependent on the surface area of the solid reactant. Increasing the surface area provides more contact between the reactants, leading to a faster reaction rate.

**Example 4:**

Consider the reaction between a solid metal (e.g., magnesium) and an acid. Cutting the metal into smaller pieces significantly increases the reaction rate compared to using a single large piece, as more metal is exposed to the acid.

### Subtopic 5: Catalysts and Reaction Rate

A **catalyst** is a substance that speeds up a reaction without being consumed in the overall reaction. Catalysts work by providing an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy. This means more reactant molecules have sufficient energy to react, thus increasing the reaction rate.

**Example 5:**

The Haber-Bosch process for ammonia synthesis uses an iron catalyst. The catalyst lowers the activation energy, allowing the reaction to proceed at a commercially viable rate.

### Subtopic 6: Applying Rate Laws to IB Exam Questions

IB exam questions frequently present scenarios where students need to:

*   Calculate reaction rates from given information.
*   Determine the order of a reaction.
*   Apply rate laws to predict the effect of changes in concentration or temperature.
*   Interpret experimental data related to reaction kinetics.

**Example 6 (Exam Style):**

The reaction between sodium hydroxide and hydrochloric acid is represented by:

NaOH(aq) + HCl(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)

Experimental data shows that when the concentration of NaOH is doubled, the rate of reaction also doubles.

*   **a)** What is the order of the reaction with respect to NaOH?
*   **b)** Write down the rate law for this reaction.

*   **a)** The order is 1. (This is because the rate doubles when the concentration doubles - first order)
*   **b)** Rate Law: Rate = k[NaOH]
