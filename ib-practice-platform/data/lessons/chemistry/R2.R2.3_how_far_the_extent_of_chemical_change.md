## Overview
This lesson explores the quantitative aspects of chemical reactions, focusing on determining the **stoichiometry** of reactions, understanding **limiting reactants**, and calculating **theoretical yields**. Students will develop skills in applying the **mole concept** to solve problems involving various chemical reactions.

## Key Concepts
*   **Stoichiometry:** The quantitative relationship between reactants and products in a chemical reaction.
*   **Limiting Reactant:** The reactant that is completely consumed in a chemical reaction.
*   **Theoretical Yield:** The maximum amount of product that can be formed from a given amount of reactant, assuming complete reaction.
*   **Actual Yield:** The actual amount of product obtained in a chemical reaction.
*   **Percent Yield:** The ratio of actual yield to theoretical yield, expressed as a percentage.

### Subtopic 1: Introduction to Stoichiometry & Mole Concept (R2.R2.3.1)

> **Important:** Stoichiometry is the foundation for understanding how much of a reactant is needed to produce a certain amount of product, and vice versa. It’s rooted in the balanced chemical equation.

Stoichiometry involves using balanced chemical equations to calculate the amounts of reactants and products involved in a chemical reaction. The **mole concept** is central to this process. A **mole** is defined as 6.022 x 10^23 of a substance (Avogadro's number), representing a specific quantity of particles (atoms, molecules, ions, etc.).  The coefficients in a balanced equation represent the *mole* ratios.

**Example 1:**

Consider the reaction:  $H_2 + O_2 \rightarrow H_2O$

The balanced equation tells us that 1 mole of H~2~ reacts with 1 mole of O~2~ to produce 1 mole of H~2~O. This 1:1 ratio is fundamental to stoichiometric calculations.

**Example 2:**

If you have 3 moles of H~2~, you will need 3 moles of O~2~ to react completely according to the equation.  Similarly, you will produce 3 moles of H~2~O.

**Example 3:**

*Problem*:  What mass of sodium chloride (NaCl) is produced when 4.0 moles of hydrochloric acid (HCl) react with excess sodium hydroxide (NaOH)?  The reaction is: $HCl + NaOH \rightarrow NaCl + H_2O$

*Solution*:
1.  The balanced equation shows a 1:1 mole ratio between HCl and NaCl.
2.  Therefore, 4.0 moles of HCl will produce 4.0 moles of NaCl.
3.  The molar mass of NaCl is 58.44 g/mol.
4.  Mass of NaCl = 4.0 moles * 58.44 g/mol = 233.76 g

### Subtopic 2: Calculating Theoretical Yield (R2.R2.3.2)

> **Note:** Theoretical yield represents an *ideal* scenario.  In reality, reactions are rarely 100% efficient due to factors like incomplete mixing, side reactions, and loss of product during purification.

To calculate the **theoretical yield**, you start with the amount of a known reactant and use the stoichiometric ratio from the balanced equation to determine the maximum possible amount of product that *could* be formed.

**Example 1:**

*Problem*: Calculate the theoretical yield of carbon dioxide (CO~2~) produced when 5.0 g of methane ($CH_4$) are completely burned in excess oxygen. The balanced equation is: $CH_4 + 2O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + 2H_2O$

*Solution*:
1.  Calculate the molar mass of methane ($CH_4$): 16.04 g/mol.
2.  Calculate the number of moles of methane: 5.0 g / 16.04 g/mol = 0.311 moles.
3.  The balanced equation shows a 1:1 mole ratio between $CH_4$ and $CO_2$.
4.  Therefore, 0.311 moles of $CH_4$ will produce 0.311 moles of $CO_2$.
5.  Calculate the molar mass of carbon dioxide (CO~2~): 44.01 g/mol.
6.  Theoretical yield of CO~2~ = 0.311 moles * 44.01 g/mol = 13.67 g

### Subtopic 3: Limiting Reactants (R2.R2.3.3)

> **Exam Tip:** Identifying the limiting reactant is crucial for calculating both the theoretical yield and the amount of excess reactant present.

The **limiting reactant** is the reactant that is completely consumed in a chemical reaction. Once it's used up, the reaction stops, regardless of how much of the other reactants are still present. The amount of product formed is determined by the amount of the limiting reactant.

**Example 1:**

*Problem*:  Consider the reaction: $N_2 + 3H_2 \rightarrow 2NH_3$

If you have 36 g of N~2~ and 6 g of H~2~, which is the limiting reactant?

*Solution*:
1.  Calculate the molar mass of N~2~: 28.02 g/mol.
2.  Calculate the number of moles of N~2~: 36 g / 28.02 g/mol = 1.28 moles.
3.  Calculate the number of moles of H~2~: 6 g / 2.02 g/mol = 2.96 moles.
4.  Determine the mole ratio required for complete reaction: 1:3 (N~2~:H~2~)
5.  Calculate how many moles of H~2~ are required to react with 1.28 moles of N~2~: 1.28 moles N~2~ * (3 moles H~2~ / 1 mole N~2~) = 3.84 moles H~2~
6.  Since you only have 2.96 moles of H~2~, H~2~ is the limiting reactant.  N~2~ is in excess.

### Subtopic 4: Calculating Actual Yield (R2.R2.3.4)

> **Note:** The actual yield is the experimentally determined yield of a product. It will always be less than or equal to the theoretical yield.

The **actual yield** is the mass of the product actually obtained in a chemical reaction after performing the experiment. It’s influenced by factors like experimental error, incomplete reactions, and loss of product.

**Example 1:**

In the previous example, if you actually obtained 10.0 g of $NH_3$ after the reaction, the *actual yield* is 10.0 g.

### Subtopic 5: Percent Yield (R2.R2.3.5)

> **Exam Tip:** Percent yield is a useful measure of the efficiency of a reaction. It's expressed as a percentage and reflects how close the actual yield is to the theoretical yield.

The **percent yield** is calculated using the following formula:

Percent Yield =  ($\text{Actual Yield} / \text{Theoretical Yield}$) * 100%

**Example 2:**

*Problem*:  Calculate the percent yield of the carbon dioxide production in Example 1 (where the theoretical yield was 13.67 g and the actual yield was 10.0 g).

*Solution*:
Percent Yield = (10.0 g / 13.67 g) * 100% = 73.0%

### Subtopic 6: Applications – Reaction Optimization (R2.R2.3.6)

> **Note:** Understanding stoichiometry allows chemists to optimize reactions, maximizing product yield.

Stoichiometry can be applied to optimize reaction conditions. For instance, ensuring a specific ratio of reactants, controlling reaction temperature, and using appropriate catalysts can all impact yield.

**Example 3:**

*Problem*:  A chemist is synthesizing aspirin ($C_8H_8O_3$) from acetylsalicylic acid ($C_9H_8O_4$) and sodium hydroxide ($NaOH$). The balanced reaction is: $C_9H_8O_4 + NaOH \rightarrow C_8H_8O_3 + NaO_2H$.  The chemist wants to maximize the yield of aspirin.  How would stoichiometry influence their choices?

*Solution*: The chemist would carefully weigh out the reactants to ensure a 1:1 molar ratio of acetylsalicylic acid to sodium hydroxide.  They might also use a slight excess of NaOH to ensure complete conversion of the acetylsalicylic acid, maximizing the theoretical yield of aspirin.  Controlling the reaction temperature is also important – often reactions are conducted at lower temperatures to minimize side reactions.
