### Introduction: Chemical Change and Mechanisms

> **Important:** All chemical reactions involve a change in the *chemical composition* of substances. Understanding the *mechanism* of a reaction – the step-by-step process – is crucial for predicting and controlling the reaction. Mechanisms are often represented using **arrow diagrams** that show the formation of intermediates and the overall transformation of reactants into products.

Chemical reactions are classified based on how they proceed. We’ll examine two fundamental types based on electron behavior: ionic and covalent bonding. These bonding types directly dictate the *mechanism* of change during chemical reactions.

### Ionic Bonding: The Mechanism of Electron Transfer

Ionic bonding is characterized by the **transfer** of electrons between atoms, typically between a **metal** and a **non-metal**. This transfer results in the formation of **ions** – charged particles (cations and anions). The attraction between oppositely charged ions creates the ionic bond.

**Step-by-Step Mechanism:**

1.  **Electrostatic Attraction:** The core mechanism of ionic bond formation is the electrostatic attraction between the positively charged cation and the negatively charged anion.
2.  **Crystal Lattice Formation:** These ions arrange themselves in a highly ordered, three-dimensional structure called a **crystal lattice**.  This maximizes the attractive forces and minimizes repulsive forces.
3.  **Dissolution & Conductivity:**  When dissolved in water, the ions become surrounded by water molecules, disrupting the crystal lattice and allowing for electrical conductivity (as ions are free to move).

**Example 1:**  Sodium Chloride (NaCl) Formation

```
Na  +  Cl₂  →  NaCl
```

*   **Na** (Sodium) loses one electron to become a positively charged ion, Na⁺.
*   **Cl₂** (Chlorine) gains one electron from multiple sodium atoms to become a negatively charged ion, Cl⁻.
*   The electrostatic attraction forms the NaCl crystal lattice.

**Example 2:**  Magnesium Oxide (MgO) Formation

```
2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
```

*   **Mg** (Magnesium) loses two electrons to become Mg²⁺.
*   **O₂** (Oxygen) gains two electrons to become O²⁻.
*   The resulting MgO is held together by strong electrostatic interactions.

### Covalent Bonding: The Mechanism of Electron Sharing

Covalent bonding involves the **sharing** of electrons between two *non-metal* atoms. This sharing allows both atoms to achieve a stable *octet* configuration (8 electrons in their valence shell).

**Step-by-Step Mechanism:**

1.  **Electron Pair Formation:** Atoms share one or more pairs of electrons to form a **covalent bond**.
2.  **Sigma and Pi Bonds:** Covalent bonds are categorized as **sigma (σ) bonds** (single bonds) which have a cylindrical charge distribution, and **pi (π) bonds** (double/triple bonds) which have a planar charge distribution. The number of these bonds affects the bond strength and reactivity.
3.  **Molecular Geometry:** The shared electrons determine the *shape* or **geometry** of the molecule, influencing its properties and reactivity.

**Example 1:**  Water (H₂O) Formation

```
O + 2H → H₂O
```

*   Each **O** atom shares electrons with two **H** atoms to form two covalent bonds.
*   The geometry around the oxygen atom is bent (approximately 104.5°).

**Example 2:**  Methane (CH₄) Formation

```
C + 4H → CH₄
```

*   **C** atom shares electrons with four **H** atoms to form four sigma bonds, creating a tetrahedral geometry.

### Factors Affecting Reactivity: Ionic vs. Covalent

The mechanism of change (ionic vs. covalent) significantly influences a compound’s reactivity.

*   **Ionic Compounds:** Generally less reactive than covalent compounds.  Breaking the electrostatic forces in the crystal lattice requires significant energy.  They tend to undergo *displacement reactions*.
*   **Covalent Compounds:** Reactivity is influenced by bond strength and bond arrangement.  Reactions often involve breaking and forming covalent bonds. *Addition reactions* are common.

### Reaction Mechanisms: Arrow Diagrams

> **Example:**  A simplified mechanism for the reaction of HCl with NaOH (a classic neutralization reaction)

```
H⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l) + Cl⁻(aq)
  ↓
  (H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l))
```

*   The downward arrow represents the rate-determining step – the slowest step in the mechanism.
*   The reaction proceeds through the formation of water, which then reacts with the remaining Cl⁻ ion.

### Exam Tip:

> **Exam Tip:**  IB examiners frequently test your understanding of reaction mechanisms, particularly in the context of predicting reaction products and understanding the role of catalysts. *Always clearly show the mechanism of a reaction, and explain the key steps involved.*
