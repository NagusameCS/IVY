## Lesson: The Metallic Model

## Overview

This lesson explores the metallic bonding model, examining its key features and how it relates to the properties of metals. Students will investigate the arrangement of atoms in metallic solids and relate these arrangements to observed properties.

## Key Concepts

*   **Metallic Bonding:** A unique type of bonding formed between metal atoms, characterized by a “sea” of delocalized electrons.
*   **Delocalized Electrons:** Electrons not associated with a specific atom, freely moving throughout the metallic lattice.
*   **Metallic Properties:** The properties of metals – conductivity, malleability, ductility, and luster – are directly linked to the metallic bonding model.
*   **Lattice Structure:** The regular, repeating arrangement of atoms in a metallic solid.

### 1. Introduction to Metallic Bonding (A.1.1)

> **Note:** Metallic bonding is arguably the most crucial bonding type for understanding the behavior of metals. It differentiates metals from ionic and covalent compounds.

Metallic bonding arises from the interactions between positively charged metal ions and the freely moving **delocalized electrons**.  Unlike ionic bonds where electrons are localized between specific ions, or covalent bonds where electrons are shared between specific atoms, metallic bonds feature a ‘sea’ of electrons surrounding the metal ions. This 'sea' provides a mechanism for electron transfer and contributes to the unique properties of metals.

> **Example:** Consider copper (Cu). Copper atoms readily lose their outer electrons to form Cu^{2+} ions. These Cu^{2+} ions are then surrounded by a "sea" of electrons which are free to move throughout the crystal lattice.

Let’s represent this model:

```
Cu^{2+} +  (delocalized electrons)  →  Cu^{2+} + (delocalized electrons)
```

### 2. The Metallic Lattice Structure (A.1.1)

The atoms in a metallic solid are arranged in a highly ordered, **lattice structure**.  This lattice is typically a three-dimensional arrangement of atoms, often cubic or hexagonal, depending on the metal.

*   **Cubic Lattice:** Metals like aluminum and gold often form cubic lattices.
*   **Hexagonal Lattice:** Metals such as copper and iron frequently adopt a hexagonal lattice.

> **Exam Tip:**  Students should be able to describe and illustrate the lattice structure of common metals.  Understanding the arrangement of atoms is crucial for explaining properties.

### 3. Understanding Conductivity (A.1.1)

The free movement of **delocalized electrons** is fundamental to the high electrical conductivity of metals.  When a potential difference is applied, these electrons readily move through the lattice, carrying an electric current. This contrasts sharply with insulators, which have tightly bound electrons and cannot conduct electricity effectively.

> **Example:**  Imagine a metal wire. Applying a voltage causes the delocalized electrons to move in a directed manner, creating an electric current. The ease with which this happens is due to the non-directional nature of metallic bonding.

**Worked Example 1:**

*   **Question:** Explain how the metallic bonding model accounts for the high electrical conductivity of copper.
*   **Answer:** Copper atoms readily lose their valence electrons, forming a “sea” of delocalized electrons. These electrons are not bound to individual copper atoms and can move freely throughout the copper lattice under the influence of an applied electric field.  This movement constitutes an electric current.

### 4. Malleability and Ductility – A Result of the Model (A.1.1)

The 'sea' of electrons surrounding the metal ions enables metals to be **malleable** (able to be hammered into thin sheets) and **ductile** (able to be drawn into wires).

*   **Malleability:**  When a force is applied, the metal ions can slide past each other without breaking the bonds, because the electrons maintain contact between the ions.
*   **Ductility:**  Similar to malleability, the electrons allow the metal ions to rearrange themselves, enabling the metal to be drawn into wires.

> **Note:** These properties are absent in brittle materials like ceramics or diamond, where strong, directional bonds restrict atomic movement.

### 5. Luster and the Metallic Model (A.1.1)

The characteristic **luster** (shininess) of metals is another consequence of the metallic bonding model.  When light strikes a metal surface, the **delocalized electrons** interact with the photons, causing them to scatter in all directions. This scattered light is what we perceive as shine.

> **Example:** Polished silver reflects light due to the movement of its delocalized electrons, giving it its characteristic luster.

### 6. Applying the Model – Predicting Properties (A.1.1)

The metallic bonding model provides a framework for predicting the properties of metals based on their atomic structure and arrangement.

> **Exam Tip:**  IB examiners often ask students to link the model to observed properties. Be prepared to discuss conductivity, malleability, ductility, and luster.

**Worked Example 2:**

*   **Question:**  Explain why gold (Au) is malleable and ductile, using the metallic bonding model.
*   **Answer:**  Gold atoms lose their outer electrons to form a "sea" of delocalized electrons.  The regular cubic lattice structure of gold, combined with these freely moving electrons, allows the gold ions to slide past each other without breaking the metallic bonds, thus making gold malleable and ductile.

**Worked Example 3:**

*   **Question:** A student states that metallic bonds are responsible for the high melting point of metals. Explain this statement using the metallic bonding model.
*   **Answer:** The metallic bonding model explains the high melting point of metals by illustrating the strength of the attractive forces between the positively charged metal ions and the large number of delocalized electrons. The electrons are tightly bound and require a significant amount of energy to overcome, leading to a high melting point.  The more delocalized electrons, the stronger the attraction and the higher the melting point.

## Summary

The metallic bonding model provides a powerful explanation for the characteristic properties of metals. It describes a ‘sea’ of *delocalized electrons* surrounding positively charged metal ions arranged in a *lattice structure*. This arrangement allows for high *electrical conductivity*, *malleability*, *ductility*, and *luster*.  Understanding this model is essential for predicting and explaining the diverse behaviours observed in metals, and is frequently assessed in the IB Chemistry syllabus (A.1.1).
