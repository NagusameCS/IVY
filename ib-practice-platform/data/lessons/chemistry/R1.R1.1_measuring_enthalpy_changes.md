## Measuring Enthalpy Changes

## Overview

This lesson focuses on experimentally determining the enthalpy change of a chemical reaction, specifically using a simple calorimetry experiment. Students will learn about heat transfer, calorimetry, and how to apply these principles to calculate ΔH.

## Key Concepts

*   **Enthalpy Change (ΔH):** A measure of the heat absorbed or released during a chemical reaction at constant pressure.
*   **Calorimetry:** The science of measuring heat changes in chemical and physical processes.
*   **Heat Transfer:** The movement of thermal energy due to a temperature difference.
*   **Specific Heat Capacity (c):** The amount of heat required to raise the temperature of 1g of a substance by 1°C.

### 1. Introduction to Enthalpy and Heat

> **Important:** The concept of enthalpy is fundamental to understanding energy changes in reactions. It's not just about the heat *released*, but the *total* energy change.

Chemical reactions either absorb or release energy, and this energy is quantified as enthalpy change (ΔH). A **negative** ΔH indicates an exothermic reaction (heat is released), while a **positive** ΔH indicates an endothermic reaction (heat is absorbed).

Consider the following example:

```
H₂ (g) + O₂ (g) → 2H₂O (g)   ΔH = -483.6 kJ/mol
```

Here, the reaction releases 483.6 kJ of heat for every mole of hydrogen gas reacting with oxygen gas. This value, -483.6 kJ/mol, represents the enthalpy change.

### 2. Calorimetry – Measuring Heat Changes

Calorimetry is the systematic way of measuring heat changes. A **calorimeter** is an instrument designed to do just that. There are different types, but for our experiment, we'll use a simple coffee-cup calorimeter.

> **Note:** A coffee-cup calorimeter is an inexpensive, open-cup calorimeter. It’s useful for demonstration purposes but has significant heat loss to the surroundings, meaning our calculated ΔH will be less accurate than with a more sophisticated calorimeter.

The key components of a coffee-cup calorimeter are:

*   An insulated container (the “coffee cup”)
*   A stirrer
*   A thermometer

### 3. The Coffee-Cup Calorimeter Experiment

Let's design an experiment to determine the enthalpy change of the neutralization of a strong acid and a strong base.

**Reaction:**  HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)

**Procedure:**

1.  Prepare a solution of 0.1 mol of HCl in 100 cm³ of distilled water.
2.  Measure the initial temperature of the HCl solution (T~initial~).
3.  Add 0.1 mol of NaOH solution to the HCl solution.
4.  Stir continuously and monitor the temperature until it reaches a maximum and then stabilizes.
5.  Record the maximum temperature achieved (T~final~).
6.  Repeat the experiment at least three times.

### 4. Calculating the Heat Transfer

The change in temperature (ΔT) is calculated as:

$$ΔT = T_{final} - T_{initial}$$

The heat transferred (q) can be calculated using the following formula:

$$q = m \cdot c \cdot ΔT$$

Where:

*   *q* = heat transferred (in Joules)
*   *m* = mass of the solution (in grams) – approximate as 100 cm³ (d = 1 g/cm³)
*   *c* = specific heat capacity of the solution (assume approximately 4.18 J/g°C for water)
*   *ΔT* = change in temperature (in °C)

### 5. Calculating ΔH

Since the reaction is exothermic, the heat released by the reaction (q~reaction~) will be equal to the heat absorbed by the calorimeter (q~calorimeter~).  We'll assume all the heat lost by the reaction is absorbed by the solution.

$$q_{reaction} = -q_{calorimeter}$$

Therefore:

$$ΔH = - \frac{q_{reaction}}{n}$$

Where:

*   *ΔH* = enthalpy change of the reaction (in kJ/mol)
*   *q~reaction~* = heat released by the reaction (in Joules) – calculated using the equation above.
*   *n* = number of moles of the limiting reactant (HCl in our case)

**Worked Example 1:**

Let's say the initial temperature of the HCl solution was 22.0 °C and the final temperature was 27.5 °C.  ΔT = 27.5 - 22.0 = 5.5 °C.

Using the formula *q = m * c * ΔT* with *m* = 100g and *c* = 4.18 J/g°C:

q = 100g * 4.18 J/g°C * 5.5 °C = 2269 J

Since q~reaction~ = -q~calorimeter~, we have q~reaction~ = 2269 J

n (moles of HCl) = 0.1 mol

Therefore, ΔH = - (2269 J) / (0.1 mol) = -22690 J/mol = -22.69 kJ/mol

### 6. Sources of Error and Improvements

> **Exam Tip:** Students should be able to identify and discuss sources of error in calorimetry experiments.

Several factors can affect the accuracy of our experiment:

*   **Heat Loss:** The coffee-cup calorimeter is not perfectly insulated, leading to heat loss to the surroundings.
*   **Incomplete Mixing:**  Uneven mixing can lead to inaccurate temperature readings.
*   **Assumed Specific Heat Capacity:** The specific heat capacity of water can vary slightly depending on temperature and conditions.
*   **Volume Measurement:** Using 100cm³ as the mass is an approximation, especially if the solution density changes with temperature.

Improvements could include:

*   Using a more efficient calorimeter (e.g., a bomb calorimeter).
*   Ensuring thorough stirring.
*   Using a more precise thermometer.

Students learned to conduct a simple calorimetry experiment to determine the enthalpy change of the neutralization reaction between a strong acid and a strong base.
