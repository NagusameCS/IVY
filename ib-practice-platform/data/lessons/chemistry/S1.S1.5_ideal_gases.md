## Lesson: Ideal Gases

## Overview

This lesson explores the behavior of gases and introduces the **Ideal Gas Law**, a fundamental concept in chemistry. Students will investigate the relationship between pressure, volume, temperature, and the number of moles of a gas, focusing on the assumptions underlying the ideal gas model.

## Key Concepts

*   Understanding the **particulate nature of matter** and how it influences gas behavior.
*   Applying the **Ideal Gas Law** (PV = nRT) to solve quantitative problems.
*   Recognizing the limitations of the **Ideal Gas Law** and when deviations occur.
*   Calculating **molar volume** at standard temperature and pressure (STP).

### Subtopic 1: Kinetic Molecular Theory and Gas Pressure

> **Important:** The foundation of understanding ideal gas behavior lies in the Kinetic Molecular Theory (KMT).

The Kinetic Molecular Theory describes the behavior of gases based on the motion of their constituent particles (atoms or molecules). The postulates are:

1.  Gases are composed of tiny particles in constant, random motion.
2.  The volume of the particles is negligible compared to the total volume of the gas.
3.  The particles collide with each other and the walls of the container.
4.  Average kinetic energy is proportional to the absolute temperature of the gas.

**Gas Pressure:** *Pressure* is defined as the force exerted per unit area. In the context of gases, it's the force exerted by the continuous collisions of the gas particles against the walls of their container. The more frequently and forcefully the particles collide, the greater the pressure.

**Worked Example 1:**

A 2.0 mol sample of oxygen gas occupies a volume of 10.0 L at standard temperature and pressure (STP). Calculate the pressure exerted by the gas.

*Solution:*

1.  Identify the values:  n = 2.0 mol, V = 10.0 L, T = 273.15 K (STP)
2.  Use the Ideal Gas Law: PV = nRT
3.  Solve for P:  P = nRT / V
4.  Substitute the values: P = (2.0 mol)(8.314 J/mol·K)(273.15 K) / 10.0 L
5.  Calculate: P ≈ 46.37 kPa (or 4.64 x 10^{3} Pa)

> **Exam Tip:**  Always be mindful of the units. Conversion between Pascals (Pa) and kilopascals (kPa) is crucial.

### Subtopic 2: The Ideal Gas Law (PV = nRT)

The **Ideal Gas Law** is a mathematical expression that relates the pressure (P), volume (V), number of moles (n), and temperature (T) of an ideal gas.

*   **P:** Pressure (usually in Pascals (Pa) or atmospheres (atm))
*   **V:** Volume (usually in cubic meters (m^{3}) or liters (L))
*   **n:** Number of moles (mol)
*   **R:** Ideal gas constant (8.314 J/(mol·K) or 0.0821 L·atm/(mol·K)) – *Note the different values of R depending on the units used for P and V.*

> **Note:** The Ideal Gas Law is an approximation and works best for gases at relatively low pressures and high temperatures.

**Worked Example 2:**

Calculate the volume occupied by 5.0 mol of an ideal gas at a pressure of 2.0 atm and a temperature of 300 K.

*Solution:*

1.  Identify the values: n = 5.0 mol, P = 2.0 atm, T = 300 K
2.  Rearrange the Ideal Gas Law to solve for V: V = nRT / P
3.  Substitute the values: V = (5.0 mol)(8.314 J/(mol·K))(300 K) / 2.0 atm
4.  Calculate: V ≈ 61.1 L

### Subtopic 3: Molar Volume at STP

At **Standard Temperature and Pressure** (STP), which is defined as 0°C (273.15 K) and 1 atm, one mole of any ideal gas occupies a volume of 22.4 L. This is known as the **molar volume** at STP.

**Worked Example 3:**

What volume does 1.0 mol of a gas occupy at STP?

*Solution:*

*   The molar volume at STP is 22.4 L/mol.
*   Therefore, 1.0 mol of a gas occupies 22.4 L at STP.

### Subtopic 4: Deviations from Ideal Gas Behavior

Real gases deviate from ideal behavior, especially at high pressures and low temperatures, because the assumptions of the Ideal Gas Law are no longer valid.  At higher pressures, the volume occupied by the gas particles becomes significant compared to the total volume. At lower temperatures, the kinetic energy of the gas particles decreases, leading to stronger intermolecular forces.

**Examples of deviations:**

*   **Real gases** exhibit **liquefaction** at extremely low temperatures due to increased attractive forces.
*   **Real gases** show higher pressures than predicted by the ideal gas law at high pressures.

### Subtopic 5: Applying the Ideal Gas Law to Calculations

The Ideal Gas Law is a versatile tool for solving a variety of problems.  It can be used to determine:

*   Volume of a gas
*   Pressure of a gas
*   Number of moles of a gas
*   Temperature of a gas

> **Example:**  Calculate the temperature change of a gas if its volume is reduced from 5.0 L to 2.5 L while the pressure remains constant at 1.0 atm and the number of moles is kept at 3.0 mol.

*Solution:*

1.  Initial conditions: V~1~ = 5.0 L, P~1~ = 1.0 atm, n = 3.0 mol
2.  Final conditions: V~2~ = 2.5 L, P~2~ = 1.0 atm, n = 3.0 mol
3.  Use the combined gas law:  (P~1~V~1~) / T~1~ = (P~2~V~2~) / T~2~
4.  Solve for T~2~: T~2~ = (P~1~V~1~T~2~) / (P~2~V~2~) = (1.0 atm * 5.0 L * T~2~) / (1.0 atm * 2.5 L)
5.  Calculate: T~2~ = 2T~1~ = 2 * 298.15 K ≈ 596.3 K

### Subtopic 6:  Using Different Units of R

It is crucial to use the correct value of the gas constant (R) depending on the units you are using for pressure and volume. Remember:

*   R = 8.314 J/(mol·K) when P is in Pa and V is in m^{3}
*   R = 0.0821 L·atm/(mol·K) when P is in atm and V is in L

## Summary

The Ideal Gas Law, PV = nRT, provides a fundamental model for understanding gas behavior.  It’s based on the kinetic molecular theory and allows us to calculate relationships between pressure, volume, temperature, and the number of moles of a gas. *While it is an approximation, it’s a remarkably useful tool* for many calculations.  Understanding deviations from ideal behavior and the correct selection of the gas constant (R) are essential for applying the Ideal Gas Law effectively.  This lesson lays the groundwork for understanding more complex gas behavior and its applications in various fields like chemistry and physics.
