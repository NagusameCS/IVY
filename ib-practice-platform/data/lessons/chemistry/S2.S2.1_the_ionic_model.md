## The Ionic Model

## Overview

This lesson introduces students to the ionic model of bonding, exploring the formation of ionic compounds through electrostatic attraction and focusing on the key properties arising from this structure. Students will learn to predict the formation of ionic compounds and understand how their properties differ from those of covalent compounds.

## Key Concepts

*   **Ionic Bonding:** The electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions.
*   **Electrostatic Force:** The force of attraction between charged particles.
*   **Lattice Structure:** The regular, repeating arrangement of ions in an ionic compound.
*   **Ionic Properties:**  Properties of ionic compounds, like high melting points and conductivity when molten or dissolved.

### Subtopic 1: Formation of Ionic Compounds

> **Important:** Ionic compounds are formed through the transfer of electrons between a metal and a non-metal.

The ionic model explains this process through the concept of **electron transfer**. A **metal** (typically a Group 1 or Group 2 element) readily *loses* electrons to achieve a stable electron configuration, forming a **positive ion** (cation).  A **non-metal** (typically a Group 16 or Group 17 element) readily *gains* electrons to achieve a stable electron configuration, forming a **negative ion** (anion).

The electrostatic attraction between these oppositely charged ions results in a strong, stable bond. This attraction is the **electrostatic force**. The greater the difference in electronegativity between the two elements, the more ionic the bond is likely to be.

**Example 1:**

Let’s consider the formation of Sodium Chloride (NaCl).

*   Sodium (Na) has 1 valence electron. It loses this electron to become a Na⁺ ion:  `Na → Na⁺ + e⁻`
*   Chlorine (Cl) has 7 valence electrons. It gains this electron to become a Cl⁻ ion: `Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻`

The electrostatic attraction between Na⁺ and Cl⁻ then forms the ionic lattice structure.

**Example 2:**

Magnesium Oxide (MgO) formation:

*   Magnesium (Mg) loses 2 valence electrons to become Mg²⁺: `Mg → Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻`
*   Oxygen (O) gains 2 electrons to become O²⁻: `O + 2e⁻ → O²⁻`

The resulting attraction leads to a very stable compound.

### Subtopic 2: The Ionic Lattice Structure

> **Note:** The ionic lattice is a three-dimensional arrangement where ions are arranged in a regular, repeating pattern.

The arrangement of ions in an ionic lattice is crucial to understanding the properties of ionic compounds.  It’s characterized by:

*   **Coordination Number:** The number of nearest neighbor ions surrounding a central ion.  For example, in NaCl, each Na⁺ ion is surrounded by six Cl⁻ ions, and vice-versa.
*   **Close Packing:** The ions are arranged in the closest possible packing to maximize the attractive forces and minimize repulsive forces.
*   **Crystal Systems:** Ionic compounds can crystallize in different crystal systems (e.g., cubic, hexagonal), each with a specific lattice arrangement.  The cubic system is the most common.

### Subtopic 3: Properties of Ionic Compounds

The regular, repeating structure of ionic compounds gives rise to unique properties. These properties are significantly different from those of covalent compounds due to the nature of the electrostatic forces involved.

*   **High Melting and Boiling Points:**  Large amounts of energy are required to overcome the strong electrostatic forces holding the ions together in the lattice. $ΔH_{fusion} =  mΔs_{fus} + ΔH_{lattice}$
*   **Hardness and Brittleness:** The strong electrostatic forces make ionic solids hard, resisting deformation. However, when force is applied, ions can shift, causing ions of like charge to come into contact, leading to repulsion and fracture – hence brittleness.
*   **Electrical Conductivity (Molten and Aqueous):** In the solid state, ions are fixed in the lattice and cannot move, so ionic compounds are poor conductors of electricity. However, when molten (liquid) or dissolved in water, the ions are free to move and carry an electrical current.
*   **Solubility:**  Ionic compounds are generally soluble in polar solvents like water, which can effectively solvate and stabilize the ions.

**Example 3:**

Consider sodium chloride.  Its high melting point (801°C) and its ability to conduct electricity when molten or dissolved are direct consequences of the strong ionic bonds and the resulting lattice structure.  *Exam Tip: Always link properties to the underlying ionic structure.*

### Subtopic 4: Predicting Ionic Compound Formulas

> **Exam Tip:** Use the charges of the ions to determine the overall formula of the compound.

To predict the formula of an ionic compound, you need to consider the charges of the ions involved.

*   **Cations and Anions:** Cations (positive ions) have positive charges, and anions (negative ions) have negative charges.
*   **Balancing Charges:** The formula is determined by balancing the charges to achieve a neutral compound.

**Example 4:**

To form Magnesium Oxide, we need:

*   Mg²⁺  (charge +2)
*   O²⁻ (charge -2)

The formula is MgO, as it balances the charges perfectly:  +2 + 2(-2) = 0.

**Example 5:**

Aluminum Fluoride:

*   Al³⁺ (charge +3)
*   F⁻ (charge -1)

The formula is AlF₃, balancing the charges: +3 + 3(-1) = 0.

### Subtopic 5: Dissociation of Ionic Compounds in Solution

> **Important:** When an ionic compound dissolves in water, it doesn't simply break into individual ions. Instead, it undergoes *dissociation*.

Dissociation refers to the process where the ionic compound separates into its constituent ions when dissolved in a polar solvent like water. The water molecules surround and interact with the ions, stabilizing them in solution.

**Equation:**

`NaCl (s) → Na⁺ (aq) + Cl⁻ (aq)`

The (aq) notation indicates that the ions are dissolved in *aqueous* solution (water). This process is driven by the attraction between the ions and the surrounding water molecules.

### Subtopic 6:  Comparing Ionic and Covalent Properties

> **Note:** Comparing ionic and covalent compounds helps solidify the understanding of the underlying differences.
