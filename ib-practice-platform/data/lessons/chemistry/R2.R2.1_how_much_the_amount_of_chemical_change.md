## How Much? The Amount of Chemical Change

## Overview

This lesson investigates the quantitative relationship between reactants and products in a chemical reaction, focusing on stoichiometry and limiting reactants. Students will learn to calculate the amount of products formed and the amount of reactants required, developing skills crucial for IB Chemistry problem-solving.

## Key Concepts

*   **Stoichiometry:** The quantitative relationship between reactants and products in a chemical reaction.
*   **Limiting Reactant:** The reactant that is completely consumed in a chemical reaction, determining the maximum amount of product formed.
*   **Excess Reactant:** The reactant that remains in excess after the reaction is complete.
*   **Mole Ratio:** The ratio of moles of one substance to another in a balanced chemical equation – *a cornerstone of stoichiometric calculations*.
*   **Percentage Yield:** The percentage of the theoretical yield that is actually obtained in a reaction.

### 1. Introduction to Stoichiometry (A.1.1 & A.1.2)

> **Important:** Stoichiometry is the branch of chemistry that deals with quantitative relationships between chemical substances. It’s all about numbers – how much of each reactant you need, and how much product you’ll get.

This section introduces the fundamental concept of stoichiometry and its importance in understanding and predicting chemical reactions. We will explore how balanced chemical equations represent the exact ratios of reactants and products.

**Example 1:**

Consider the following balanced equation:

$$2H_2(g) + O_2(g) \rightarrow 2H_2O(g)$$

This equation tells us that 2 moles of hydrogen gas ($H_2$) react with 1 mole of oxygen gas ($O_2$) to produce 2 moles of water vapor ($H_2O$). This is our **mole ratio**.

**Worked Example 1:**

*   **Question:** If 4 moles of $H_2$ react with $O_2$, how many moles of $H_2O$ will be produced?
*   **Solution:**
    *   From the balanced equation: $2H_2 + O_2 \rightarrow 2H_2O$
    *   The mole ratio between $H_2$ and $H_2O$ is 2:2 or 1:1.
    *   Therefore, 4 moles of $H_2$ will produce 4 moles of $H_2O$.

### 2. Calculating the Amount of Product Formed (A.1.1 & A.1.2)

This section focuses on using the mole ratio from a balanced equation to calculate the amount of product formed when given the amount of a reactant.

**Example 2:**

Given the equation:

$$CH_4(g) + 2O_2(g) \rightarrow CO_2(g) + 2H_2O(g)$$

Calculate the amount of $CO_2$ produced when 5 moles of $CH_4$ react completely with $O_2$.

*   **Solution:**
    *   The mole ratio between $CH_4$ and $CO_2$ is 1:1.
    *   Therefore, 5 moles of $CH_4$ will produce 5 moles of $CO_2$.

### 3. Limiting Reactant – The Key to Maximum Product (A.1.1 & A.1.2)

> **Note:** The **limiting reactant** is the reactant that is completely consumed first, thereby limiting the amount of product that can be formed.

This section introduces the concept of a **limiting reactant**, which is critical for maximizing product yield.  We’ll learn to identify the limiting reactant by comparing the mole ratios of reactants in a reaction.

**Example 3:**

Consider the reaction:

$$N_2(g) + 3H_2(g) \rightarrow 2NH_3(g)$$

If 6 moles of $N_2$ react with 12 moles of $H_2$, which is the limiting reactant and why?

*   **Solution:**
    *   To react completely with 6 moles of $N_2$, we would need $3 \times 6 = 18$ moles of $H_2$.
    *   We have 12 moles of $H_2$, which is *less* than the 18 moles required.
    *   Therefore, $N_2$ is the limiting reactant.

### 4. Percentage Yield – Real-World Considerations (A.1.2)

> **Exam Tip:** Percentage yield is a key assessment point in the IB Chemistry exam. Understand how to calculate and interpret it.

The **percentage yield** indicates the efficiency of a reaction. It’s the ratio of the actual yield to the theoretical yield, expressed as a percentage.

**Formula:**

Percentage Yield =  ($\frac{\text{Actual Yield}}{\text{Theoretical Yield}} \times 100\%$)

**Example 4:**

In a reaction, the theoretical yield of $CO_2$ was 40.0 g, but the actual yield was 32.0 g.  Calculate the percentage yield.

*   **Solution:**
    *   First, we need to convert grams to moles:
        *   Moles of $CO_2$ (theoretical):  $40.0g \div 44.01g/mol = 0.909 mol$
        *   Moles of $CO_2$ (actual): $32.0g \div 44.01g/mol = 0.727 mol$
    *   Percentage Yield = ($ \frac{0.727 mol}{0.909 mol} \times 100\%$) = 80.1%

### 5. Applications of Stoichiometry – Titrations (A.1.2)

Stoichiometry is fundamental to techniques like titrations, used to determine the concentration of unknown solutions. While a full titration exploration falls beyond this focused lesson, understanding the molar relationships involved is essential.

> **Example:** In a titration, a solution of $HCl$ is reacted with a known concentration of $NaOH$. The amount of $HCl$ needed to neutralize the $NaOH$ is directly related to the moles of each reactant, allowing for quantitative analysis.

### 6.  Worked Problem – Combining Concepts (A.1.1, A.1.2)

**Question:**  When 25.0 g of magnesium ribbon is reacted with excess hydrochloric acid, 10.0 L of hydrogen gas at standard temperature and pressure (STP) is produced. Calculate the percentage yield of the reaction. (1 mole of Mg = 24.3 g, 1 mole of H2 = 22.4 L at STP)

**Solution:**

1.  **Calculate the theoretical yield of H2:**
    *   Moles of Mg = 25.0 g / 24.3 g/mol = 1.02 mol
    *   From the balanced equation (Mg + 2HCl → MgCl2 + H2), 1 mole of Mg produces 1 mole of H2.
    *   Therefore, 1.02 mol of Mg produces 1.02 mol of H2.
    *   Volume of H2 (at STP) = 1.02 mol * 22.4 L/mol = 22.9 L

2.  **Calculate the actual yield of H2:**
    *   The actual yield is given as 10.0 L.

3.  **Calculate the percentage yield:**
    *   Percentage Yield = ($ \frac{10.0L}{22.9L} \times 100\%$) = 44.2%
