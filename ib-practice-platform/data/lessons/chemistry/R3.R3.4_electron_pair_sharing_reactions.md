## Overview

This lesson explores **electron pair sharing (EPS)**, a fundamental theory for understanding and predicting the shapes of molecules and, consequently, their reactivity. We’ll delve into how the number of bonding electron pairs and lone pairs around a central atom dictates the geometry of the resulting molecule, impacting its interactions with other molecules.

## Key Concepts

*   Understanding the VSEPR theory.
*   Applying VSEPR to predict molecular shapes.
*   Relating molecular shape to reactivity through interactions.
*   Distinguishing between bonding and lone pairs.
*   Predicting molecular shapes using the octet rule.

### Introduction to VSEPR Theory

> **Important:** The Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion (VSEPR) theory is a model used to predict the three-dimensional shapes of molecules. It’s based on the principle that *electron pairs* (both bonding and lone pairs) around a central atom repel each other, and they will adopt the arrangement that minimizes this repulsion.

VSEPR theory doesn’t tell us *why* molecules have the shapes they do, but it provides a powerful tool for *predicting* them. It’s a foundational concept within IB Chemistry, and crucial for interpreting reactivity.

### Bonding and Lone Pairs

*   **Bonding Electron Pairs:** These are pairs of electrons involved in the formation of covalent bonds with other atoms. They contribute to the molecule's polarity and determine its shape.
*   **Lone Pairs:** These are single, unpaired electrons associated with an atom. They don't participate in bonding but still influence the molecule's shape and reactivity.

> **Note:**  The number of bonding and lone pairs around a central atom dictates the molecular geometry.  A central atom with only bonding pairs will have a linear geometry.  An atom with lone pairs will have a different geometry than one with only bonding pairs.

### Predicting Molecular Shapes – The Basic Shapes

Let’s start with the simplest geometries:

1.  **Linear:**  Observed with molecules having *two* bonding pairs and *zero* lone pairs around a central atom. The repulsion pushes the bonding pairs as far apart as possible.
    *   **Example:** BeCl₂ (Beryllium chloride) – Beryllium (central) has two bonds and no lone pairs. The two Cl atoms are 180° apart.
2.  **Trigonal Planar:** Observed with molecules having *three* bonding pairs and *zero* lone pairs around a central atom.
    *   **Example:** BF₃ (Boron trifluoride) – Boron (central) has three bonds and no lone pairs. The three F atoms form an equilateral triangle.
3.  **Tetrahedral:** Observed with molecules having *four* bonding pairs and *zero* lone pairs around a central atom.
    *   **Example:** CH₄ (Methane) – Carbon (central) has four bonds and no lone pairs. The four H atoms form a tetrahedral shape.  The bond angle between carbon and each hydrogen is approximately 109.5°.

> **Exam Tip:** *Always* count the bonding pairs first.  Lone pairs are added *after* you’ve determined the number of bonding pairs.

### Adding Lone Pairs – Affecting Geometry

The addition of lone pairs to a central atom significantly alters the molecular geometry.  The lone pairs exert a greater repulsive force than bonding pairs, leading to distortions of the shape.

1.  **Trigonal Bipyramidal:** Observed with molecules having *three* bonding pairs and *two* lone pairs around a central atom.
    *   **Example:**  PCl₅ (Phosphorus pentachloride) – Phosphorus (central) has five bonds and five lone pairs.  The two lone pairs occupy the equatorial positions, leading to a trigonal bipyramidal arrangement.
2.  **Square Planar:** Observed with molecules having *four* bonding pairs and *two* lone pairs around a central atom.
    *   **Example:**  SF₄ (Sulfur tetrafluoride) – Sulfur (central) has four bonds and four lone pairs. The two lone pairs occupy the axial positions, resulting in a square planar geometry.
3.  **T-shaped:** Observed with molecules having *three* bonding pairs and *three* lone pairs around a central atom.  This geometry is less common.

### More Complex Shapes – Beyond the Basics

For more complex molecules, it’s often helpful to visualize the electron pairs using a more detailed representation.  Consider molecules like XeF₄, where the arrangement of lone pairs and bonding pairs leads to a more distorted geometry.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1:**

Predict the shape of  SO₃ (Sulfur trioxide) based on VSEPR theory.

*   **Step 1:** Identify the central atom: Sulfur (S)
*   **Step 2:** Count the bonding pairs: Sulfur is bonded to three oxygen atoms, so it has three bonding pairs.
*   **Step 3:** Count the lone pairs: Sulfur has zero lone pairs.
*   **Step 4:** Determine the shape: Three bonding pairs around a central atom result in a trigonal planar shape.

Therefore, the shape of SO₃ is **trigonal planar**. The bond angle between S and each O atom is approximately 120°.

**Example 2:**

Predict the shape of  NH₄⁺ (Ammonium ion) based on VSEPR theory.

*   **Step 1:** Identify the central atom: Nitrogen (N)
*   **Step 2:** Count the bonding pairs: Nitrogen is bonded to four hydrogen atoms, so it has four bonding pairs.
*   **Step 3:** Count the lone pairs: Nitrogen has zero lone pairs.
*   **Step 4:** Determine the shape: Four bonding pairs around a central atom result in a tetrahedral shape.

Therefore, the shape of NH₄⁺ is **tetrahedral**.

**Example 3:**

Predict the shape of  ClF₃ (Chlorine trifluoride)

*   **Step 1:** Identify the central atom: Chlorine (Cl)
*   **Step 2:** Count the bonding pairs: Chlorine is bonded to three fluorine atoms, so it has three bonding pairs.
*   **Step 3:** Count the lone pairs: Chlorine has one lone pair.
*   **Step 4:** Determine the shape: Three bonding pairs and one lone pair around a central atom result in a trigonal bipyramidal shape.

Therefore, the shape of ClF₃ is **trigonal bipyramidal**.

## Summary

Electron pair sharing (VSEPR theory) provides a robust framework for predicting the three-dimensional shapes of molecules. By understanding the repulsive forces between **electron pairs** – both bonding and lone – we can accurately determine molecular geometries. These shapes have significant consequences, influencing *reactivity*, intermolecular forces, and ultimately, the behavior of chemical compounds. Mastering this concept is essential for IB Chemistry students, particularly when answering exam questions related to molecular structure and bonding.
