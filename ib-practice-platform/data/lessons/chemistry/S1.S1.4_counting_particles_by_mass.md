## Counting Particles by Mass

This lesson focuses on applying molar mass to calculate the number of particles (atoms, molecules, ions) present in a given mass, directly addressing A.1.1 Water and the overall concept of particulate nature. Students will develop proficiency in using molar mass calculations to solve quantitative problems, a crucial skill for the IB Chemistry exam.

## Key Concepts

*   **Molar Mass:** The mass of one mole of a substance expressed in grams per mole (g/mol).
*   **Mole Concept:**  A mole represents Avogadro’s number (6.022 x 10^{23}) of particles.
*   **Stoichiometry:** Using balanced chemical equations to relate quantities of reactants and products.
*   **Calculating Number of Moles:** Relating mass to the molar mass using the formula:  *moles = mass / molar mass*.
*   **Calculating Number of Particles:** Relating moles to Avogadro’s number using the formula: *number of particles = moles x Avogadro’s number*.

### Subtopic 1: Understanding Molar Mass

> **Important:** Molar mass is a fundamental property of each element and compound, directly linked to its chemical formula.

Molar mass is calculated by summing the atomic masses of all atoms in a chemical formula.  Atomic masses are found on the periodic table. *For example, the molar mass of water (H~2~O) is calculated as follows:*

*   Hydrogen (H): 1.01 g/mol
*   Oxygen (O): 16.00 g/mol

Molar mass of H~2~O = (2 x 1.01 g/mol) + 16.00 g/mol = 18.02 g/mol

**Example 1:**

Calculate the molar mass of:
a) Sodium Chloride (NaCl)
b) Carbon Dioxide (CO~2~)

**Solution:**

a) Sodium (Na): 22.99 g/mol
Chlorine (Cl): 35.45 g/mol
Molar mass of NaCl = 22.99 + 35.45 = 58.44 g/mol

b) Carbon (C): 12.01 g/mol
Oxygen (O): 16.00 g/mol
Molar mass of CO~2~ = 12.01 + (2 x 16.00) = 44.01 g/mol

**Exam Tip:** *Always include the correct units (g/mol) when stating the molar mass.*



### Subtopic 2: Calculating Moles from Mass

The relationship between mass, molar mass, and the number of moles is central to this lesson.  The basic equation is:  *moles = mass / molar mass*.  This allows us to convert a given mass into the number of moles.

**Example 2:**

Calculate the number of moles in:

a) 25.0 g of Magnesium Oxide (MgO)
b) 100 g of Iron (Fe)

**Solution:**

a) Molar mass of MgO = 16.00 + 24.31 = 40.31 g/mol
moles = 25.0 g / 40.31 g/mol = 0.620 moles

b) Molar mass of Fe = 55.85 g/mol
moles = 100 g / 55.85 g/mol = 1.78 moles

### Subtopic 3: Calculating Number of Particles from Moles

Once you have calculated the number of moles, you can use Avogadro's number (6.022 x 10^{23}) to determine the number of particles present.  The formula is: *number of particles = moles x Avogadro’s number*.

**Example 3:**

Calculate the number of molecules in 0.25 moles of Carbon Dioxide (CO~2~).

**Solution:**

number of molecules = 0.25 moles * 6.022 x 10^{23} molecules/mol = 1.5055 x 10^{23} molecules.  (Rounding to 3 significant figures)

### Subtopic 4: Applying the Concepts - Problem Solving

Let’s consolidate our understanding with a more complex problem.

**Problem:** A sample of sulfur dioxide (SO~2~) gas has a mass of 44.0 grams. Calculate:

a) The number of moles of SO~2~ in the sample.
b) The number of molecules of SO~2~ in the sample.

**Solution:**

a) Molar mass of SO~2~ = 32.07 + (2 x 16.00) = 64.07 g/mol
moles = 44.0 g / 64.07 g/mol = 0.683 moles

b) number of molecules = 0.683 moles * 6.022 x 10^{23} molecules/mol = 4.11 x 10^{23} molecules (rounding to 3 significant figures)
