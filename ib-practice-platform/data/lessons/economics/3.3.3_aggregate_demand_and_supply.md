## Aggregate Demand and Supply

## Overview

This lesson explores the fundamental concepts of aggregate demand (AD) and aggregate supply (AS), analyzing their determinants and the resulting impacts on equilibrium price levels and output. We’ll investigate shifts in these curves and their implications for macroeconomic stability.

## Key Concepts

*   Understanding the relationships between aggregate price levels, real GDP, and the costs of production.
*   Analyzing the factors that shift the AD and AS curves.
*   Determining equilibrium levels of output and price in both short-run and long-run scenarios.
*   Applying these concepts to interpret macroeconomic policies and their effects.

### 3.3.3 – Understanding Aggregate Demand

> **Important:** Aggregate demand represents the total quantity of goods and services demanded in an economy at a given price level, assuming all other factors remain constant. It's a crucial tool for analyzing economic fluctuations.

Aggregate demand is composed of four main components:

1.  **Consumption (C):** Spending by households on goods and services. This is the largest component of AD.
    *   Factors affecting consumption: Income, interest rates, consumer confidence, wealth.
2.  **Investment (I):** Spending by firms on capital goods (e.g., machinery, buildings) and inventories.
    *   Factors affecting investment: Interest rates, business expectations, technological change.
3.  **Government Spending (G):** Expenditure by the government on goods and services.
    *   *Note:* Government spending is a planned expenditure, not necessarily reflecting consumer demand directly.
4.  **Net Exports (X - M):** The difference between exports and imports.
    *   *Formula:*  X - M = (Total Exports) - (Total Imports)

The **AD curve** represents the inverse relationship between the price level and the quantity of real GDP demanded. As the price level rises, the quantity of real GDP demanded falls, all else being equal.

**Example 1:**

*   A sudden increase in consumer confidence due to positive economic news would likely shift the AD curve to the right, indicating an increased demand for goods and services at every price level.
*   Conversely, a decrease in interest rates would likely shift the AD curve to the right, encouraging investment spending and increasing demand at all price levels.

### 3.3.3 – Understanding Aggregate Supply

> **Important:** Aggregate supply represents the total quantity of goods and services that firms are willing and able to supply in an economy at a given price level.

There are two primary models for analyzing aggregate supply:

1.  **Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS):** This curve represents the relationship between the price level and the quantity of real GDP supplied in the *short run*. It’s upward sloping because firms can increase output by charging higher prices, but this doesn't cover all their increased costs.
    *   **Factors shifting the SRAS curve:**
        *   **Input Costs:** Rising wages, raw material prices, or energy costs will shift the SRAS curve to the left.
        *   **Technology:** Technological advancements can reduce production costs, shifting the SRAS curve to the right.
        *   **Expectations:** Changes in firms’ expectations about future prices or economic conditions can also affect supply.
2.  **Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS):** This curve represents the potential output of the economy when all resources are fully employed in the *long run*. It’s vertical because, in the long run, the price level is determined by supply-side factors (technology, resources, human capital) and not by aggregate demand.
    *   **LRAS is often represented as Y* (potential output)** – the level of output where AD and LRAS intersect.

**Example 2:**

*   A sudden increase in oil prices would shift the SRAS curve to the left, reducing the quantity of real GDP supplied at every price level. This leads to higher inflation and potentially lower output.
*   Investment in new machinery and technology would shift the LRAS curve to the right, increasing the economy's potential output.

### 3.3.3 – The Intersection of AD and AS

> **Exam Tip:** Be able to accurately draw and interpret both the AD and AS curves. Understanding their shapes and how they shift is fundamental.

The equilibrium in the economy is determined where AD and AS intersect. This point determines the equilibrium **price level** and the equilibrium **real GDP** (output).

*   **Equilibrium in the Short Run:** Occurs where AD = AS.  The price level is determined by the intersection of the two curves.
*   **Equilibrium in the Long Run:** Occurs where AD = LRAS. This represents the economy's potential output, and the price level is determined solely by the supply-side factors.

### 3.3.3 – Shifts in AD and AS - Scenarios

Let’s consider some common scenarios and how they affect equilibrium:

1.  **Increase in Government Spending:** Shifts AD to the right, leading to a higher equilibrium price level and output.
2.  **Technological Innovation:** Shifts LRAS to the right, leading to a higher potential output (Y*) and a possible lower equilibrium price level in the short run.
3.  **Rising Inflation Expectations:** Shifts both AD and AS to the left, leading to a lower equilibrium price level and output.
4.  **Supply Shock (e.g., sudden increase in oil prices):** Shifts SRAS to the left, leading to higher inflation and lower output in the short run.

### 3.3.3 – Equilibrium with Sticky Prices

> **Note:** "Sticky prices" refer to prices that don't adjust immediately to changes in supply or demand. This can lead to short-run imbalances.

If AD is greater than AS, there will be a shortage of goods and services, leading to upward pressure on prices. This causes firms to revise their expectations and adjust their prices, eventually shifting AS to the right and restoring equilibrium.

## Summary

Aggregate demand and supply models provide a powerful framework for analyzing macroeconomic fluctuations. Shifts in either the AD or AS curve will impact the equilibrium price level and real GDP. Understanding the determinants of these curves—including factors like consumer confidence, interest rates, technology, and input costs—is crucial for interpreting economic data and evaluating the potential effects of government policies.  Mastering the concepts of short-run and long-run equilibrium is essential for success in IB Economics.
