## Introduction to Economic Inquiry

This lesson explores the fundamental questions economists address and the diverse approaches they utilize to understand how economies function. We will delve into the core concepts of scarcity, choice, and opportunity cost, exploring how these shape economic thinking and analysis.

## Key Concepts

*   **Scarcity:** The fundamental economic problem - limited resources relative to unlimited wants.
*   **Choice:** The necessity of making decisions due to scarcity.
*   **Opportunity Cost:** The value of the next best alternative forgone.
*   **Positive vs. Normative Economics:** Understanding the difference between factual statements and value judgments.
*   **Economic Models:** Recognizing the use of simplified representations to analyze complex systems.

### 1. Understanding Scarcity and Choice

Scarcity is the bedrock of economics. It arises because resources – land, labor, capital, and entrepreneurship – are finite, while human desires are virtually infinite. This reality forces individuals, businesses, and governments to make choices about how to allocate those scarce resources.

> **Important:**  Scarcity isn't simply a lack of money; it's a lack of anything that’s valuable and limited.

**Example:** Consider a farmer with a single acre of land. They can choose to grow wheat, corn, or soybeans. The scarcity of land forces this decision.

**Worked Example 1:**

*   **Question:** A consumer has a budget of $100 and can purchase either apples or oranges. Apples cost $2 each, and oranges cost $1.50 each. Draw a budget constraint line representing all the possible combinations of apples and oranges the consumer can buy.
*   **Solution:**
    *   If the consumer buys only apples, they can buy 50 apples ($2 * 50 = $100).
    *   If the consumer buys only oranges, they can buy 66.67 oranges ($100 / $1.50 = 66.67).
    *   The budget constraint line will have apples on the x-axis and oranges on the y-axis.  The points (50, 0) and (0, 66.67) are two points on the line. The equation of the line is: $2x + 1.5y = 100$
    *   This budget constraint illustrates the consumer's choice due to scarcity.

**Worked Example 2:**

*   **Question:** A small business owner has $50,000 to invest. They can invest in a new machine or expand their marketing efforts. The machine will increase production by 10%, while marketing will increase sales by 5%. Which investment is more economically rational, considering the opportunity cost?
*   **Solution:**  The opportunity cost of investing in the machine is the potential increase in sales from marketing. The opportunity cost of investing in marketing is the increased production capacity from the machine.  The decision depends on which has the higher return. (Assuming returns are proportional to the investment, both are equally attractive, but in reality, a full cost-benefit analysis would be needed).

### 2. Opportunity Cost – The True Cost

The **opportunity cost** is a crucial concept in economics. It's *not* simply the monetary cost of a decision. Instead, it’s the value of the *next best* alternative that is foregone.

> **Note:**  Understanding opportunity cost helps individuals and businesses make rational decisions by considering the full implications of their choices.

**Example:**  If you spend an hour studying economics, the opportunity cost isn't just the price of the textbook. It’s the value you could have received by spending that hour working a part-time job or pursuing a hobby.

**Worked Example 3:**

*   **Question:** A government decides to build a new highway. The cost of the project is $100 million. The government estimates that the highway will increase GDP by $20 million per year. Calculate the opportunity cost of the highway.
*   **Solution:** The opportunity cost is the highest return alternative that could have been achieved with the $100 million. In this case, that's $20 million (the increase in GDP). The true cost isn't just $100 million, but $120 million ($100 million + $20 million).

### 3. Positive vs. Normative Economics

Economists employ two distinct types of analysis:

*   **Positive Economics:** Deals with *factual* statements about the economy. It attempts to answer “what is” and “what will be.”  These statements can be tested and potentially proven.
    *   **Example:** "An increase in the minimum wage will lead to a decrease in employment."
*   **Normative Economics:** Deals with *value judgments* and opinions about what *should* be. It attempts to answer “what ought to be.”  These statements are inherently subjective.
    *   **Example:** "The government should raise the minimum wage to reduce poverty."

> **Exam Tip:**  It's essential to distinguish between positive and normative statements when analyzing economic issues.  Simply stating an opinion doesn't constitute an economic argument.

### 4. Economic Models – Simplified Representations

Economists use **economic models** to represent complex economic phenomena in a simplified way. Models are abstractions – they don't capture all the details of reality but help us understand relationships and predict outcomes.

> **Important:**  Models are tools, not perfect representations.  They're based on assumptions and can be affected by changes in underlying conditions.

**Example:** The **Supply and Demand Model** is a simplified representation of how prices are determined in a market. It shows the interaction between buyers (demand) and sellers (supply).

### 5.  Micro vs. Macro Economics

*   **Microeconomics:** Studies individual economic agents (consumers, firms) and their behavior. It focuses on specific markets and industries.
*   **Macroeconomics:** Studies the economy as a whole. It focuses on aggregate variables such as GDP, inflation, and unemployment.
