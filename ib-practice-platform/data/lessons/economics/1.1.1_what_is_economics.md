## What Is Economics?

This lesson introduces the fundamental concepts of economics, exploring scarcity, opportunity cost, and the role of decision-making in allocating resources. We’ll delve into micro and macro perspectives, laying the groundwork for understanding economic systems and models.

## Key Concepts

*   **Scarcity:** The fundamental economic problem – limited resources relative to unlimited wants.
*   **Opportunity Cost:** The value of the next best alternative forgone when making a choice.
*   **Micro vs. Macro Economics:** Understanding the difference in scope of analysis.
*   **Positive vs. Normative Economics:** Differentiating between value-free analysis and opinion-based judgements.

### Subtopic 1: The Core Problem – Scarcity

Economics is fundamentally concerned with how societies deal with **scarcity**. This means that resources – land, labor, capital, and entrepreneurship – are limited, while human wants and needs are virtually unlimited. This creates a fundamental problem: how do we allocate these scarce resources to satisfy as many wants as possible?

> **Important:** Scarcity isn't just about physical resources; it can also refer to time, skills, or information.

**Example:** Consider a farmer with a limited plot of land. They can choose to grow wheat or corn. Scarcity dictates they can’t grow both to their full potential. This limits their output of both.

**Worked Example 1:**

*   **Question:** A student has $20 and wants to buy either a new video game or concert tickets. The video game costs $15, and the concert tickets cost $25. If the student buys the video game, what is their opportunity cost?
*   **Solution:** The student’s opportunity cost is the concert tickets ($25). This is because they forgo the opportunity to attend the concert to purchase the video game.
*   **Formula:** Opportunity Cost = Value of Next Best Alternative Forgone

**Worked Example 2:**

*   **Question:** A government has $1 billion to spend. They could invest in education or infrastructure. If they invest in education, what is their opportunity cost?
*   **Solution:** The opportunity cost is the potential benefits of investing in infrastructure (e.g., improved roads, bridges).

### Subtopic 2: Opportunity Cost – Making Choices

Closely linked to scarcity is the concept of **opportunity cost**. Every time we make a choice, we are implicitly giving up something else. The opportunity cost is the value of the *next best* alternative we forgo. It’s crucial to recognize that opportunity cost isn’t just about monetary costs; it can involve time, effort, or enjoyment.

> **Note:** Opportunity cost is subjective and can vary from person to person.

**Example:**  If you spend two hours studying economics, the opportunity cost isn’t just the time itself. It might include the chance to spend that time working a part-time job, socializing with friends, or pursuing a hobby.

**Worked Example 3:**

*   **Question:** A company has $1 million to invest. They can invest in Research & Development (R&D) or expand their existing factory. They estimate that R&D will generate a return of $1.2 million, while expanding the factory will generate a return of $900,000. What is the opportunity cost of investing in R&D?
*   **Solution:** The opportunity cost is the $900,000 return they could have earned from expanding the factory.
*   **Formula:** Opportunity Cost = Return of Next Best Alternative

### Subtopic 3: Micro vs. Macro Economics

Economics can be broadly divided into two branches:

*   **Microeconomics:** Studies individual economic units – households, firms, and markets. It focuses on how these units make decisions and how their interactions determine prices and quantities in specific markets.  A.1.1 focuses heavily on microeconomic principles.
*   **Macroeconomics:** Studies the economy as a whole – focusing on issues such as inflation, unemployment, and economic growth. It deals with aggregate variables like Gross Domestic Product (GDP).

> **Exam Tip:**  Be able to differentiate between the scope of micro and macro economic analysis.

**Example:** Microeconomics might analyze the impact of a new tax on a specific industry (e.g., the impact on a car manufacturer’s production costs). Macroeconomics might analyze the overall impact of that tax on national GDP.

### Subtopic 4: Positive vs. Normative Economics

Within economics, there are two distinct approaches to economic analysis:

*   **Positive Economics:** Deals with *facts* and *descriptions* of the economy. It aims to provide objective assessments of economic phenomena, using data and statistical analysis. Statements in positive economics are verifiable.
*   **Normative Economics:** Deals with *values* and *judgements* about what *should* be. It expresses opinions and recommendations about how the economy *ought* to be.  Statements in normative economics are subjective.

> **Note:**  It’s important to distinguish between positive and normative statements.

**Example:**
*   **Positive:** “Unemployment is currently at 5%.” (A factual statement)
*   **Normative:** “The government should reduce unemployment.” (A value-based judgment)

### Subtopic 5: Economic Systems – A Brief Overview

Understanding economics begins with recognizing that different societies utilize different **economic systems** to allocate resources. These systems vary in terms of:

*   **Ownership of Resources:** Private ownership (capitalism) vs. public ownership (socialism)
*   **Decision-Making:** Centralized planning vs. decentralized market mechanisms
*   **Role of Government:** Extensive intervention vs. minimal intervention

   The IB course focuses primarily on market-based economies.

### Subtopic 6: Modeling in Economics

Economists use **models** to simplify complex economic realities and make predictions. These models are based on assumptions and are not always perfectly accurate but help us understand the relationships between variables.

> **Important:** Recognize that economic models are simplified representations of reality and rely on certain assumptions.
