## Exchange Rates

## Overview

This lesson explores the concept of **exchange rates**, their determinants, and their impact on international trade and investment. We will investigate different exchange rate regimes and analyze how fluctuations affect economic activity.

## Key Concepts

*   **Exchange Rate:** The price of one currency expressed in terms of another.
*   **Floating Exchange Rate:** Determined by supply and demand in the foreign exchange market.
*   **Fixed Exchange Rate:** Government-managed rate pegged to another currency or a basket of currencies.
*   **Purchasing Power Parity (PPP):** A theory suggesting exchange rates should adjust to equalize the purchasing power of currencies in different countries.
*   **Balance of Payments:** A record of all economic transactions between a country and the rest of the world.

### 1. Introduction to Exchange Rates

> **Important:** Exchange rates are fundamental to international trade and finance. Understanding them is crucial for businesses, investors, and policymakers.

Exchange rates represent the value of one currency relative to another. For example, if the exchange rate between the US dollar (USD) and the Euro (EUR) is 1.10, this means you need 1.10 Euros to buy one US dollar. These rates constantly fluctuate due to various market forces. *The IB Economics syllabus (4.4.4) emphasizes the importance of exchange rates in understanding global trade patterns.*

**Example 1:**

A company in Japan needs to buy $1,000,000 worth of goods from the United States. If the exchange rate is 1 USD = 140 JPY, how many Yen will the company need to pay?

*   Calculation: $1,000,000 * 140 JPY/USD = 140,000,000 JPY

The company will need 140,000,000 Yen to pay for the goods.

### 2. Determinants of Exchange Rates

Several factors influence exchange rates, primarily driven by supply and demand.

*   **Interest Rate Differentials:** Higher interest rates in a country tend to attract foreign investment, increasing demand for that country's currency.
*   **Inflation Rates:** Countries with higher inflation rates tend to see their currency depreciate.
*   **Current Account Deficits/Surpluses:** A current account deficit (importing more than exporting) puts downward pressure on a currency.
*   **Government Policies:** Central bank intervention, monetary policy, and fiscal policy can all influence exchange rates.
*   **Market Speculation:** Expectations about future exchange rates can drive significant movements.

**Example 2:**

Assume the US Federal Reserve raises interest rates, while the European Central Bank maintains a steady rate. How might this affect the USD/EUR exchange rate?

*   **Explanation:** The higher interest rates in the US will attract capital inflows, increasing demand for the USD. This increased demand will cause the USD to appreciate against the EUR.
*   **Prediction:** The USD/EUR exchange rate is likely to increase (e.g., from 1.10 to 1.15).

### 3. Exchange Rate Regimes

There are different ways countries manage their exchange rates:

*   **Floating Exchange Rate:** The currency’s value is determined purely by market forces (supply and demand). This is the most common regime.
*   **Fixed Exchange Rate:** The currency’s value is pegged to another currency or a basket of currencies. Requires significant government intervention.
*   **Managed Float:** The exchange rate is allowed to fluctuate within a certain band, but the central bank intervenes to prevent excessive movements.
*   **Crawling Peg:** The exchange rate is adjusted periodically, usually in small increments.

> **Note:** *The choice of exchange rate regime has significant implications for a country’s monetary policy and its ability to respond to economic shocks.*

### 4. Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)

**PPP** is a theory stating that exchange rates should adjust to equalize the purchasing power of currencies in different countries. This means that the price of a good should be the same in different countries when expressed in a common currency.

*   **Absolute PPP:**  The exchange rate equals the ratio of price levels between two countries.
    $E = P_1 / P_2$
    Where:
    *   E = Exchange Rate
    *   P_1 = Price Level in Country 1
    *   P_2 = Price Level in Country 2

*   **Relative PPP:** The percentage change in the exchange rate equals the difference in inflation rates between the two countries.
    $%ΔE = π_1 - π_2$
    Where:
    *   %ΔE = Percentage Change in Exchange Rate
    *   π_1 = Inflation Rate in Country 1
    *   π_2 = Inflation Rate in Country 2

**Example 3:**

If the price of a barrel of oil is $80 in the US and €70 in the Eurozone, and the PPP theory holds, what would be the exchange rate between the USD and EUR according to Absolute PPP?

*   Calculation:  Exchange Rate = $80 / €70 ≈ 1.14 USD/EUR

### 5. Exchange Rates and the Balance of Payments

Exchange rate fluctuations directly impact a country’s balance of payments.

*   **Depreciation:** A depreciation of the currency makes exports cheaper and imports more expensive, potentially improving the current account balance (though this is not guaranteed).
*   **Appreciation:** An appreciation of the currency makes exports more expensive and imports cheaper, potentially worsening the current account balance.

**Example 4:**

A country experiences a significant depreciation of its currency.  Analyze the potential effects on its trade balance.

*   **Prediction:** The trade deficit (imports > exports) is likely to increase because imports become more expensive, and exports become relatively cheaper, potentially boosting export volume.  *This directly relates to the concept of the current account balance as detailed in A.1.1 - The Global Economy.*

### 6. Exchange Rate Risk

> **Exam Tip:** *IB Economics examiners frequently test students' understanding of exchange rate risk – the potential for losses due to exchange rate fluctuations.*

Businesses engaged in international trade or investment face **exchange rate risk**. This risk can be mitigated through:

*   **Hedging:** Using financial instruments (e.g., forward contracts) to lock in a specific exchange rate.
*   **Natural Hedging:** Matching revenues and expenses in the same currency.

## Summary

Exchange rates are vital for international economic activity, determined by complex forces of supply and demand, and influenced by macroeconomic factors. Different exchange rate regimes exist, each with implications for a country’s monetary policy. Purchasing Power Parity offers a theoretical framework for understanding exchange rate movements, while exchange rate fluctuations directly impact the balance of payments. Finally, understanding and managing exchange rate risk is crucial for businesses operating in a globalized economy.
