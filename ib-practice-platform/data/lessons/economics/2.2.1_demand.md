## Overview

This lesson will explore the concept of **demand** in microeconomics, examining its determinants, graphically representation, and the relationship to other economic variables.  We will focus on understanding how changes in various factors influence consumers’ willingness to pay for a good or service.

## Key Concepts

*   **Demand Curve:** A graphical representation of the relationship between the price of a good and the quantity demanded.
*   **Law of Demand:**  A fundamental principle stating that, *ceteris paribus*, as the price of a good increases, the quantity demanded decreases.
*   **Determinants of Demand:** Factors that shift the entire demand curve (e.g., income, tastes, prices of related goods, expectations).
*   **Elasticity of Demand:** A measure of the responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in price.
*   **Price Elasticity of Demand (PED):** Specifically, how much quantity demanded changes in response to a percentage change in price.

### Understanding the Law of Demand

The **Law of Demand** is a cornerstone of microeconomics. It’s based on the principle of diminishing marginal utility – the satisfaction gained from consuming an additional unit of a good decreases as consumption increases. > **Important:** *Ceteris paribus* means “all other things being equal.” This is crucial when analyzing the law of demand. We assume that factors other than price remain constant.

**Example 1:**

Consider the market for ice cream. As the price of ice cream increases, consumers will likely buy less ice cream. This is because they might switch to cheaper alternatives like fruit or reduce their overall consumption of ice cream.

**Example 2:**

Imagine a new study shows that eating chocolate increases the risk of heart disease.  Assuming other factors remain constant, the **demand** curve for chocolate will likely shift to the left (decrease) as consumers react to this new information.

**Example 3:**

A significant change in consumer tastes towards organic food could shift the demand curve for organic apples to the right, reflecting increased willingness to pay.

### Graphical Representation of Demand

The **demand curve** is a visual representation of the relationship between price and quantity demanded. It’s typically drawn with price on the Y-axis and quantity demanded on the X-axis.

*   **Movement along the curve:** Represents a change in **quantity demanded** due to a change in **price**, *ceteris paribus*.
*   **Shift of the curve:** Represents a change in **demand** due to a change in a determinant other than price.  Shifts to the right indicate an increase in demand, and shifts to the left indicate a decrease in demand.

```

|
|       /
|      /
|     /
|    /
|---/------
|  |       Quantity Demanded
|  |

```

### Determinants of Demand – Shifts in the Demand Curve

Several factors can cause a shift in the entire demand curve, not just a movement along it. These are the **determinants of demand**:

1.  **Income:**
    *   **Normal Goods:** As income increases, demand increases (rightward shift). Example: Demand for luxury cars.
    *   **Inferior Goods:** As income increases, demand decreases (leftward shift). Example: Demand for instant noodles.

2.  **Prices of Related Goods:**
    *   **Substitute Goods:** If the price of a substitute good increases, the demand for the original good increases (rightward shift). Example: If the price of coffee rises, demand for tea may increase.
    *   **Complementary Goods:** If the price of a complementary good increases, the demand for the original good decreases (leftward shift). Example: If the price of printers increases, demand for printer ink may decrease.

3.  **Tastes and Preferences:** Changes in consumer tastes, trends, or advertising campaigns can significantly affect demand.

4.  **Expectations:** Expectations about future prices or income can influence current demand. > **Exam Tip:**  Examine exam questions that ask you to evaluate the impact of changing consumer expectations.

5.  **Number of Consumers:** An increase in the number of potential consumers in a market will generally increase overall demand.

### Elasticity of Demand

**Elasticity of demand** measures the responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in price.  This is a crucial concept for businesses to understand.

*   **Elastic Demand:** A significant change in quantity demanded occurs in response to a price change.  PED > 1
*   **Inelastic Demand:** A small change in quantity demanded occurs in response to a price change. PED < 1
*   **Unit Elastic Demand:**  The percentage change in quantity demanded is equal to the percentage change in price. PED = 1

**Example 1:**

Consider the market for gasoline.  Gasoline is generally considered **inelastic** in the short run.  Even if the price increases significantly, people still need to drive.

**Example 2:**

The market for concert tickets is often **elastic**. If the price of a ticket increases dramatically, fewer people will be willing to attend.

**Calculating Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)**

$$PED = \frac{\% \text{ Change in Quantity Demanded}}{\% \text{ Change in Price}}$$

**Example 3:**

Suppose the price of a product increases by 10% and the quantity demanded decreases by 20%.
$$PED = \frac{-20\%}{-10\%} = 2$$
This indicates that the demand is elastic.

### Factors Affecting Elasticity of Demand

Several factors influence the elasticity of demand for a particular good:

*   **Availability of Substitutes:** The more substitutes available, the more elastic the demand.
*   **Necessity vs. Luxury:**  Demand for necessities tends to be inelastic, while demand for luxuries tends to be elastic.
*   **Time Horizon:** Demand tends to be more elastic over longer time horizons as consumers have more time to adjust their behavior.

## Summary

Demand is the quantity of a good or service consumers are willing and able to buy at various prices.  The **Law of Demand** dictates that as price rises, quantity demanded falls, and several factors – income, related goods, tastes, and expectations – can shift the entire demand curve. Understanding **elasticity of demand** is crucial, as it determines how responsive consumers are to price changes. Businesses use this information to make informed decisions about pricing strategies and market forecasting –  critical for success in microeconomic analysis.
