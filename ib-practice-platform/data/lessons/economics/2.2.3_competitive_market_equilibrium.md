## Key Concepts

This lesson will explore the concept of **competitive market equilibrium**, a fundamental model in microeconomics. We will examine how supply and demand interact to determine price and quantity in a perfectly competitive market, and how shifts in these forces lead to changes in equilibrium.

## 1. Introduction to Supply and Demand

Before we can understand competitive equilibrium, we need to solidify our understanding of supply and demand. 

### 1.1 The Law of Demand

The **law of demand** states that, *all other factors being equal*, as the price of a good or service increases, the quantity demanded decreases. This inverse relationship is driven by factors like:

*   **Substitution Effect:** Consumers switch to cheaper alternatives.
*   **Income Effect:**  Higher prices reduce purchasing power.

> **Example:** If the price of coffee suddenly doubles, consumers might switch to tea or simply drink less coffee.

### 1.2 The Law of Supply

The **law of supply** states that, *all other factors being equal*, as the price of a good or service increases, the quantity supplied increases.  This is driven by:

*   **Profit Incentive:** Producers are incentivized to produce more at higher prices.
*   **Opportunity Cost:** Lower costs of production make it more profitable to produce more.

> **Example:** If the price of wheat increases, farmers will plant more wheat to capitalize on the higher profit margin.

### 1.3 Graphical Representation

We can represent supply and demand graphically. The **demand curve** slopes downward, and the **supply curve** slopes upward. The intersection of these curves represents the **market equilibrium**.

## 2. Determining Equilibrium Price and Quantity

### 2.1 Market Equilibrium

**Market equilibrium** is the point where the quantity demanded equals the quantity supplied. This is represented graphically by the intersection of the supply and demand curves. At this point, there is no tendency for the price or quantity to change.

### 2.2 Calculating Equilibrium

Equilibrium price ($P^*$ ) and equilibrium quantity ($Q^*$ ) can be calculated algebraically:

*   $P^* = \frac{Total Revenue from Demand}{Quantity Supplied}$  (This requires understanding the demand equation and solving for P)
*   $Q^* = Quantity Supplied at Equilibrium Price$ (This requires understanding the supply equation and solving for Q)

> **Exam Tip:** The IB often asks you to *calculate* equilibrium values, not just describe them.

**Example 1:**

Suppose the demand curve is given by:  $Q = 100 - 2P$
And the supply curve is given by: $Q = 10P$

To find the equilibrium, set the two equations equal to each other:

$100 - 2P = 10P$
$100 = 12P$
$P = \frac{100}{12} = 8.33$ (approximately)

Then, $Q = 10(8.33) = 83.3$ (approximately)

Therefore, the equilibrium price is $8.33 and the equilibrium quantity is 83.3.



## 3. Shifts in Supply and Demand

### 3.1 Shifts in the Demand Curve

A **shift in the demand curve** indicates a change in the quantity demanded at *every* price level. This can be caused by:

*   Changes in consumer income
*   Changes in the price of related goods (substitutes or complements)
*   Changes in consumer tastes and preferences
*   Changes in expectations

   *Example: An increase in income might shift the demand curve for a normal good to the right.*

### 3.2 Shifts in the Supply Curve

A **shift in the supply curve** indicates a change in the quantity supplied at *every* price level. This can be caused by:

*   Changes in input prices (e.g., cost of raw materials)
*   Technological advancements
*   Changes in the number of sellers
*   Changes in expectations

   *Example: A new, more efficient production method would shift the supply curve to the right.*

### 3.3 Analyzing Shifts

*   **Increase in Demand:**  Both the demand and supply curves shift, leading to a higher equilibrium price and a higher equilibrium quantity.
*   **Decrease in Demand:**  Both the demand and supply curves shift, leading to a lower equilibrium price and a lower equilibrium quantity.
*   **Increase in Supply:**  Only the supply curve shifts to the right, leading to a lower equilibrium price and a higher equilibrium quantity.
*   **Decrease in Supply:**  Only the supply curve shifts to the left, leading to a higher equilibrium price and a lower equilibrium quantity.

## 4.  Elasticity of Demand and Supply

Understanding **elasticity** is crucial for analyzing market changes.

### 4.1 Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

**PED** measures the responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in price.  It is calculated as:

$PED = \frac{\% Change in Quantity Demanded}{\% Change in Price}$

*   **Elastic Demand (PED > 1):**  A small change in price leads to a large change in quantity demanded.
*   **Inelastic Demand (PED < 1):**  A large change in price leads to a small change in quantity demanded.
*   **Unit Elastic Demand (PED = 1):**  The percentage change in quantity demanded equals the percentage change in price.

### 4.2 Price Elasticity of Supply (PES)

**PES** measures the responsiveness of quantity supplied to a change in price.  It is calculated as:

$PES = \frac{\% Change in Quantity Supplied}{\% Change in Price}$

*   **Elastic Supply (PES > 1):** A small change in price leads to a large change in quantity supplied.
*   **Inelastic Supply (PES < 1):** A large change in price leads to a small change in quantity supplied.

## 5.  Market Equilibrium with Government Intervention (HL Extension)

(This section is primarily relevant for HL students, but provides context)

Governments can intervene in markets through:

*   **Taxes:** Shift the supply curve to the left.
*   **Subsidies:** Shift the supply curve to the right.
*   **Price Controls:**  Price ceilings and floors can lead to shortages or surpluses.

## 6. Summary

The **competitive market equilibrium** is a cornerstone of microeconomic analysis, representing the point where supply and demand forces balance. Understanding the law of supply and demand, and how shifts in these curves impact equilibrium, is critical for analyzing market behavior.  *Furthermore, calculating equilibrium price and quantity provides a quantitative framework for understanding market dynamics*. **Exam Tip:**  Be prepared to calculate equilibrium values and analyze the impact of shifts on price and quantity.  The concept of elasticity provides a more nuanced understanding of market responsiveness.
