## Measuring Economic Activity

### GDP – The Foundation of Macroeconomics

GDP is the most widely used measure of a country’s economic output. It represents the total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country’s borders during a specific period.

> **Important:** GDP focuses on *final* goods and services to avoid double-counting. Intermediate goods (those used to produce other goods) are not included.

There are three main approaches to calculating GDP:

1.  **Expenditure Approach:**  This is the most common method.
    GDP = C + I + G + NX
    Where:
    *   C = Consumption (household spending on goods and services)
    *   I = Investment (business spending on capital goods – machinery, buildings, etc.)
    *   G = Government Spending (government expenditure on goods and services)
    *   NX = Net Exports (Exports - Imports)

2.  **Income Approach:** This approach calculates GDP based on the income earned by all factors of production.
    GDP = C + I + G + R + ΔNW
    Where:
    *   R = Rent
    *   ΔNW = Change in Net Worth (change in the value of assets)

3.  **Value-Added Approach:** This method calculates GDP by summing the value added at each stage of production. **Value added** is the difference between a firm’s sales revenue and the cost of its intermediate inputs.

**Example 1:**

Consider a simple economy with a farmer, a miller, and a baker.

*   Farmer: Sells wheat for $10 (Value Added = $10)
*   Miller: Buys wheat for $8 and sells flour for $12 (Value Added = $12 - $8 = $4)
*   Baker: Buys flour for $12 and sells bread for $18 (Value Added = $18 - $12 = $6)

GDP = $10 + $4 + $6 = $20

**Example 2:**

A country's GDP is $10 trillion. Consumption accounts for 60% of GDP, Investment for 20%, and Government Spending for 15%. Calculate the value of Net Exports.

*   C = 0.60 * $10 trillion = $6 trillion
*   I = 0.20 * $10 trillion = $2 trillion
*   G = 0.15 * $10 trillion = $1.5 trillion
*   GDP = C + I + G + NX
*   $10 trillion = $6 trillion + $2 trillion + $1.5 trillion + NX
*   NX = $10 trillion - ($6 trillion + $2 trillion + $1.5 trillion) = $0.5 trillion

**Exam Tip:**  *Be prepared to calculate GDP using the expenditure approach and understand the differences between the three approaches.*  Command words used: *Calculate*, *Explain*

### Inflation – The Rising Tide

**Inflation** is a general increase in the prices of goods and services in an economy over a period of time. It’s typically measured by the **Consumer Price Index (CPI)**, which tracks the average change in prices of a basket of goods and services commonly purchased by households.

> **Note:**  Hyperinflation (rapid and extreme inflation) has significant consequences, including eroding purchasing power and destabilizing economies.

The **Inflation Rate** is calculated as:  Inflation Rate = ((CPI this year – CPI last year) / CPI last year) * 100

**Example 3:**

If the CPI in 2022 was 280 and the CPI in 2023 was 290, then the inflation rate is:

Inflation Rate = ((290 - 280) / 280) * 100 = (10 / 280) * 100 = 3.57%

*Causes of Inflation*: Demand-Pull Inflation (excess demand), Cost-Push Inflation (rising production costs).
*Effects of Inflation*: Reduced purchasing power, uncertainty in investment decisions.

### Unemployment – A Measure of Economic Distress

**Unemployment** is the percentage of the labor force that is actively seeking employment but cannot find work. The **Unemployment Rate** is calculated as: Unemployment Rate = (Number of Unemployed / Labor Force) * 100

> **Note:** The labor force includes employed and unemployed individuals. It excludes those not in the labor force (e.g., retired individuals, students).

**Types of Unemployment:** Frictional Unemployment (temporary), Structural Unemployment (long-term mismatch between skills and jobs), Cyclical Unemployment (related to economic cycles).

**Example 4:**

If the number of unemployed individuals is 5 million and the labor force is 25 million, then the unemployment rate is:

Unemployment Rate = (5 million / 25 million) * 100 = 20%

*   Factors Influencing Unemployment: Economic growth, technological advancements, government policies.
