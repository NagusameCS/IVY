## Key Concepts

This lesson focuses on the fundamental processes of cell division – mitosis and meiosis – exploring their mechanisms, significance, and evolutionary implications. Students will learn to differentiate between these processes, understand the role of chromosomes, and appreciate their importance in maintaining genetic stability and generating genetic diversity.

## Mitosis: Cell Division for Growth and Repair

### Introduction to Mitosis

> **Mitosis** is a form of cell division that results in two genetically identical daughter cells from a single parent cell. It’s crucial for growth, repair, and asexual reproduction in organisms.  This process is essential for maintaining the chromosome number in somatic cells (all cells except gametes).

Mitosis is conventionally divided into four phases: Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, and Telophase (PMAT).

> **Important:** Understanding the stages of mitosis is crucial for interpreting micrographs and answering exam questions.

#### Prophase

During **prophase**, several key events occur:
*   The **chromatin** condenses into visible **chromosomes**.  Each chromosome consists of two identical **sister chromatids** joined at the **centromere**.
*   The **nuclear envelope** breaks down.
*   The **mitotic spindle** begins to form from microtubules originating from the **centrosomes**.

> **Example:** *Imagine a tightly coiled rope (chromatin) suddenly becoming clearly defined strands (chromosomes).*

#### Metaphase

In **metaphase**, the chromosomes line up along the **equator** of the cell, attached to the **spindle fibers**. This alignment ensures that each daughter cell receives a complete and identical set of chromosomes.

> **Exam Tip:**  Micrographs of metaphase are frequently assessed in the IB Biology exam. Be able to identify the key features accurately.

#### Anaphase

During **anaphase**, the sister chromatids separate and move towards opposite poles of the cell. This is driven by the shortening of the spindle fibers.

> **Formula:**  The separation of sister chromatids is initiated by the enzymatic cleavage of cohesin proteins holding them together.

#### Telophase

In **telophase**, the chromosomes arrive at the poles and begin to decondense back into chromatin. The **nuclear envelope** reforms around each set of chromosomes, and the **mitotic spindle** disappears.

> **Note:**  Telophase is often considered the ‘end’ of mitosis, but cytokinesis (the division of the cytoplasm) typically follows.

**Worked Example 1:**

Describe the key events that occur during metaphase in mitosis.

*Solution:* During metaphase, the chromosomes, consisting of two sister chromatids, are aligned along the metaphase plate, an imaginary plane equidistant from the two poles of the cell.  Each sister chromatid is attached to spindle fibres originating from opposite poles, ensuring equitable distribution of genetic material during subsequent anaphase. The spindle fibres provide the tension needed for the chromosome alignment.*



## Meiosis: Cell Division for Sexual Reproduction

### Introduction to Meiosis

> **Meiosis** is a specialized type of cell division that reduces the chromosome number by half, producing four genetically different daughter cells called **gametes** (sperm and egg cells). It’s essential for sexual reproduction, ensuring genetic diversity.

Meiosis involves two successive divisions, Meiosis I and Meiosis II. Meiosis I is the reductional division, while Meiosis II is the equational division.

#### Meiosis I

Meiosis I is characterized by:

*   **Homologous chromosome pairing:**  Homologous chromosomes (one from each parent) pair up to form **tetrads**.
*   **Crossing over:**  Exchange of genetic material between homologous chromosomes occurs, leading to *recombination*.
*   **Separation of homologous chromosomes:**  Homologous chromosomes are separated, with each daughter cell receiving one chromosome from each pair.

#### Meiosis II

Meiosis II is similar to mitosis, with sister chromatids separating.  It results in four haploid cells (n).

> **Exam Tip:** Pay attention to the differences between mitosis and meiosis.  Mitosis maintains chromosome number, while meiosis reduces it.

**Worked Example 2:**

Explain the significance of crossing over during meiosis.

*Solution:* Crossing over, or recombination, during meiosis I increases genetic diversity. By exchanging genetic material between homologous chromosomes, new combinations of alleles are created, leading to greater variation among daughter cells. This variation is the raw material for natural selection and adaptation.*

## Comparing Mitosis and Meiosis

| Feature           | Mitosis                     | Meiosis                      |
|--------------------|-----------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Purpose**         | Growth, repair, asexual reproduction | Sexual reproduction           |
| **Chromosome Number**| Remains constant (2n → 2n)   | Reduced by half (2n → n)      |
| **Daughter Cells**  | Two genetically identical     | Four genetically different   |
| **Divisions**       | One                          | Two                          |
| **Genetic Variation**| None                        | Present (crossing over)     |



## Summary

Mitosis is a process of cell division that produces two identical daughter cells, crucial for growth and repair. Conversely, meiosis is a specialized process that creates four genetically distinct gametes, vital for sexual reproduction and maintaining genetic diversity through mechanisms like crossing over.  Understanding the differences between these two forms of cell division – their stages, purposes, and outcomes – is fundamental to grasping concepts in genetics and heredity, especially concerning A.1.1 Continuity and Change.  *Effective revision focuses on accurate stage recognition and an appreciation for the evolutionary significance of these processes.*
