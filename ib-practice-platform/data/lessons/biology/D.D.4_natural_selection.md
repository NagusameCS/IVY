## Overview

This lesson explores the fundamental mechanism of **natural selection**, detailing how variation within populations leads to adaptation and ultimately, evolution. We will investigate the roles of genetic variation, differential survival and reproduction, and the resulting changes in allele frequencies over time.



## Key Concepts

*   **Variation:** Individuals within a population exhibit differences in their traits.
*   **Heritability:** Some of this variation is genetically based, meaning it can be passed on to offspring.
*   **Differential Survival and Reproduction:** Individuals with advantageous traits are more likely to survive and reproduce successfully.
*   **Adaptation:** The process by which populations become better suited to their environment.
*   **Allele Frequency Changes:** Natural selection results in changes in the frequency of alleles within a population over time.



### 1. The Foundations of Variation

> **Important:** Variation is the raw material upon which natural selection acts. Without variation, there would be no opportunity for selection to favour certain traits.

The source of variation within a population is primarily genetic. This can arise from several mechanisms:

1.  **Mutations:** Random changes in DNA sequences that can introduce new alleles. These are the ultimate source of *novel* variation.
2.  **Sexual Reproduction:**  The combination of genetic material from two parents results in offspring with unique combinations of alleles. This is particularly crucial as it produces *recombination* – the shuffling of existing genetic material.
3.  **Gene Flow:** The movement of alleles between populations.  This can introduce new alleles or alter existing allele frequencies.

**Example:** Consider a population of beetles.  Some beetles are green, and some are brown. This variation is due to different alleles for the color gene.



### 2. Heritability and the Gene Pool

> **Note:** The **gene pool** is the total collection of alleles present in a population. Understanding the gene pool is essential for modelling how natural selection changes allele frequencies.

*   **Heritability** refers to the extent to which a trait is determined by genetic factors. Traits with high heritability are largely controlled by genes.
*   The **phenotype** (observable characteristics) is influenced by both the genotype (genetic makeup) and the environment. However, natural selection acts on the phenotype, not the genotype directly.

**Formula:**  Phenotype = Genotype + Environmental Influence

Let's say a plant's height is influenced by a gene (genotype) but also by soil nutrients (environment). A tall plant with a favorable genotype will still be stunted if the soil is poor.



### 3. Mechanisms of Natural Selection

Natural selection isn't a "goal-oriented" process. It doesn’t *want* to produce “perfect” organisms. Instead, it’s a consequence of differential survival and reproduction due to pre-existing variation. The three main mechanisms are:

1.  **Directional Selection:** Favors one extreme phenotype, shifting the population's allele frequency in that direction. *Example:*  In a population of birds, those with slightly larger beaks are better able to crack open larger seeds during a food shortage.
2.  **Stabilizing Selection:** Favors intermediate phenotypes, reducing variation around the average. *Example:* Newborn human babies – those who are born too early or too late have significantly lower survival rates.
3.  **Disruptive Selection:** Favors both extreme phenotypes, potentially leading to the formation of two distinct populations. *Example:* In a population of snails, those with pale shells and those with dark shells may be better adapted to different habitats.

> **Exam Tip:**  Be prepared to diagram or explain different types of natural selection with specific examples.



### 4. Worked Example 1: Darwin’s Finches

> **Example:**  Darwin’s finches on the Galapagos Islands provide a classic example of natural selection.  Different finch populations developed different beak shapes based on the food sources available on their respective islands.  Finches with beaks best suited to cracking seeds had a higher survival rate and therefore passed on their genes for larger beaks.

**Step 1: Identify the Variation:**  The finches exhibited variation in beak size and shape.
**Step 2: Identify the Selective Pressure:**  Availability of different food sources (seeds of varying sizes) acted as the selective pressure.
**Step 3: Describe the Outcome:**  Finches with beaks best suited for consuming available seeds survived and reproduced more successfully, leading to an increase in the frequency of alleles associated with larger, stronger beaks.

**SL/HL Extension:** *Discuss the role of genetic drift in the finch populations, particularly on smaller, isolated islands.*



### 5. Mathematical Representation of Natural Selection

Natural selection can be represented mathematically using the following equation:

$$ \frac{dP}{dt} = rP - mp $$

Where:

*   $\frac{dP}{dt}$: The rate of change in allele frequency (Δp/t)
*   $r$: The rate of **mutation** (new alleles arising)
*   $mp$: The rate of **mutation loss** (alleles becoming fixed due to chance)

This equation highlights that the rate of allele frequency change depends on the balance between new mutations and the loss of alleles due to random chance. *Note: HL students should understand the assumptions and limitations of this model.*



### 6. Evidence for Natural Selection

> **Note:** A wealth of evidence supports the theory of natural selection.

1.  **Fossil Record:** Fossils show a progression of life forms, with older fossils often displaying traits that are less well-suited to the environment than those found in modern organisms.
2.  **Homologous Structures:** Similarities in the anatomy of different species (e.g., the pentadactyl limb found in humans, bats, and whales) indicate a common ancestor and adaptation to different environments.
3.  **Vestigial Structures:** Structures that have lost their original function over time (e.g., the human appendix) are evidence of evolutionary change.
4.  **Biogeography:** The geographic distribution of species provides evidence for evolutionary relationships and adaptation to local environments.
5.  **Molecular Evidence:** DNA sequencing provides powerful evidence for evolutionary relationships, with closely related species having more similar DNA sequences.




## Summary

Natural selection is a cornerstone of evolutionary theory, explaining how populations adapt to their environments over time. It operates through variation within a population, influenced by heritable genetic differences, and driven by differential survival and reproduction based on those traits. The change in allele frequencies reflects the selection pressure exerted by the environment, leading to populations better suited to their surroundings. This process, supported by a vast range of evidence, provides a powerful framework for understanding the diversity of life on Earth.
