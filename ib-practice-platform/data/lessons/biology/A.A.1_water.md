### Subtopic 1: Water’s Molecular Structure and Intermolecular Forces

> **Important:** The properties of water are intimately linked to its molecular structure. Water (H₂O) consists of one oxygen atom bonded to two hydrogen atoms. This bent shape, along with the polarity of the O-H bond, leads to *hydrogen bonding*.

Water's polarity arises from the unequal sharing of electrons between oxygen and hydrogen. Oxygen is more electronegative, pulling the electrons closer, resulting in a partial negative charge (δ-) on the oxygen atom and partial positive charges (δ+) on the hydrogen atoms. These partial charges create *dipole-dipole interactions* – attractive forces between polar molecules.

However, the primary intermolecular force in water is the **hydrogen bond**. This occurs when the partially positive hydrogen atom of one water molecule is attracted to the partially negative oxygen atom of a neighboring water molecule. Hydrogen bonds are relatively weak individually (around 20 kJ/mol), but collectively, they contribute significantly to water’s properties.

### Subtopic 2: Cohesion and Adhesion

> **Note:** Cohesion and adhesion are crucial for the function of plant vascular tissues.

**Cohesion** refers to the attraction between *like* molecules.  In water, cohesion results from hydrogen bonding – water molecules cling together due to these bonds. This creates surface tension, allowing insects to walk on water and enabling water to be transported upwards in plants.

**Adhesion** refers to the attraction between *different* molecules. Water molecules also adhere to other polar molecules, such as those found in cell walls or xylem vessels. This helps water molecules ‘stick’ to these surfaces.

**Example 3:**
*   **Question:** Explain how cohesion contributes to the ability of water to raise capillary in plants.
*   **Solution:** Cohesion creates surface tension, allowing water molecules to form a continuous column within xylem vessels. This allows water to be ‘pulled’ upwards against gravity via capillary action. *Explain* the role of cohesion in capillary action. (SL)

### Subtopic 3: Water as a Solvent – Polarity and Dissolution

> **Exam Tip:**  Understand the concept of “like dissolves like”. Polar solvents dissolve polar solutes.

Water is an excellent solvent, particularly for *polar* and *ionic* substances. This is because water molecules can form hydrogen bonds with these charged solutes, effectively surrounding and stabilizing them.

*   **Polar Solutes:** Substances like sugars and amino acids, which are also polar, dissolve readily in water due to favorable interactions.
*   **Ionic Solutes:** Substances like salts (e.g., NaCl) are held together by strong electrostatic forces. Water molecules can effectively break apart these ionic bonds, dispersing the ions throughout the solution.

**Example 4:**
*   **Question:** Describe why salt (NaCl) dissolves in water.
*   **Solution:** Water molecules are polar and can form hydrogen bonds with the ions in salt. These hydrogen bonds disrupt the electrostatic forces holding the sodium and chloride ions together, effectively dissolving the salt. *Describe* the process of salt dissolving in water. (SL)

### Subtopic 4: Specific Heat Capacity and Thermal Stability

> **Important:** Water’s high specific heat capacity plays a vital role in regulating temperature in living organisms.

Water has a high **specific heat capacity** – the amount of energy required to raise the temperature of 1 gram of a substance by 1 degree Celsius. This is due to the energy required to break hydrogen bonds and increase molecular motion.

Water's high specific heat also means it resists temperature changes. This thermal stability is crucial for maintaining a relatively constant internal temperature in organisms and aquatic environments.

**Example 5:**

*   **Solution:** Because water requires a large amount of energy to change its temperature, it moderates temperature fluctuations in environments and within organisms. *Explain* the concept of specific heat and its role in temperature regulation. (HL)

### Subtopic 5: Density Anomalies

> **Exam Tip:**  The anomalous behavior of water’s density is critical for aquatic life.

Unlike most substances, water is *less dense* in its solid form (ice) than in its liquid form. This is due to the open, lattice structure of ice, which results from the hydrogen bonding network expanding when water freezes.

This density anomaly is essential for aquatic life – ice floats on water, insulating the water below and preventing it from freezing solid.

**Example 6:**
*   **Question:** Describe the process that explains why ice floats on water.
*   **Solution:** When water freezes, the hydrogen bonds arrange themselves into a crystalline lattice structure, creating spaces between molecules. This structure is less dense than liquid water, causing ice to float. *Describe* the process of ice formation and its effect on density. (SL)

### Subtopic 6: Water’s Role in Biological Processes

> **Note:** Water is the medium for many biochemical reactions in living organisms.

Water is a vital component in nearly all *biochemical reactions* occurring within living organisms. It acts as a reactant, a solvent, and a transport medium. For example, photosynthesis and cellular respiration both heavily rely on water.
