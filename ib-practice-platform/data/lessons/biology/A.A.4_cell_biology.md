## Lesson: Cell Biology – A.A.4 – Unity and Diversity

## Overview

This lesson explores the fundamental characteristics of cells – prokaryotic and eukaryotic – highlighting their structural differences and diverse functions. Students will investigate the organelles within eukaryotic cells and understand their roles in maintaining cell life.

## Key Concepts

*   Cell theory and its postulates.
*   Distinguishing between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells.
*   Organelle structure and function within eukaryotic cells.
*   Membrane transport mechanisms.
*   Cell specialization and its relation to tissue formation.

### Subtopic 1: Introduction to Cell Theory

> **Important:** Cell theory is the foundation of modern biology, providing a framework for understanding living organisms.

Cell theory, established primarily by Schleiden and Schwann, consists of three main postulates:

1.  All living organisms are composed of one or more **cells**.
2.  The cell is the basic structural unit of life.
3.  All cells arise from pre-existing cells.

These postulates are supported by a vast amount of experimental evidence. Understanding these concepts is crucial for analyzing biological processes at the cellular level.

**Example 1:** *Describe* how the first postulate of cell theory relates to the observation of bacteria. Bacteria are single-celled organisms, directly fulfilling the requirement that all living things are composed of cells.

### Subtopic 2: Prokaryotic vs. Eukaryotic Cells

Cells are broadly classified into two categories: prokaryotic and eukaryotic. The primary difference lies in the presence or absence of a **nucleus**.

| Feature           | Prokaryotic Cell | Eukaryotic Cell |
| ----------------- | ---------------- | --------------- |
| Nucleus           | Absent           | Present         |
| Organelles        | Absent           | Present         |
| Size              | Smaller (0.1-5 μm) | Larger (10-100 μm) |
| Examples          | Bacteria, Archaea | Animals, Plants, Fungi, Protists |

> **Note:** Prokaryotic cells are generally simpler and smaller than eukaryotic cells.  The evolution of the eukaryotic cell involved the endosymbiotic theory, where organelles like mitochondria and chloroplasts were once free-living prokaryotic organisms.

**Example 2:** *Compare and contrast* the size and complexity of prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells.  Consider how these differences might influence their metabolic capabilities and organization.

### Subtopic 3: Eukaryotic Cell Organelles – Structure & Function

Eukaryotic cells possess a variety of membrane-bound organelles, each with a specific function. Let's explore some key organelles:

*   **Nucleus:** Contains the cell’s genetic material (DNA) and controls cellular activities.
*   **Mitochondria:** Site of cellular respiration, producing ATP (energy).
*   **Ribosomes:** Involved in protein synthesis.
*   **Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER):** Network of membranes involved in protein and lipid synthesis. (Smooth ER – lipid synthesis, Rough ER – protein synthesis)
*   **Golgi Apparatus:** Modifies, sorts, and packages proteins and lipids.
*   **Lysosomes:** Contain enzymes for intracellular digestion.
*   **Chloroplasts (in plant cells):** Site of photosynthesis.

**Example 3:** *Explain* the role of the mitochondria in cellular respiration, detailing the process of ATP production. Use the following equation as a guide: $C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2 → 6CO_2 + 6H_2O + ATP$

### Subtopic 4: Membrane Transport – Facilitating Movement

Cells need to regulate the movement of substances across their membranes. This can occur through passive or active transport mechanisms.

*   **Passive Transport:**
    *   **Diffusion:** Movement of molecules from high to low concentration.
    *   **Osmosis:** Movement of water across a semi-permeable membrane from high water potential to low water potential.
    *   **Facilitated Diffusion:** Movement of molecules with the assistance of membrane proteins.
*   **Active Transport:** Movement of molecules *against* their concentration gradient, requiring energy (ATP).

**Example 4:** *Describe* the process of osmosis, illustrating with a diagram depicting water movement across a membrane.  Explain how this process is vital for maintaining cell turgor pressure in plants.

### Subtopic 5: Cell Specialization and Tissue Formation

Cells within a multicellular organism are not all identical. They specialize to perform specific functions, leading to the formation of **tissues**.

*   **Epithelial Tissue:** Covers surfaces, provides protection and secretion.
*   **Connective Tissue:** Supports and connects other tissues (e.g., bone, cartilage, blood).
*   **Muscle Tissue:** Responsible for movement.
*   **Nervous Tissue:** Transmits signals throughout the body.

> **Exam Tip:** Understand how cell specialization is driven by differential gene expression – different cells express different sets of genes, leading to the production of distinct proteins and ultimately, different functions.

### Subtopic 6:  Cell Membrane Transport – Detailed Mechanisms

Several membrane transport mechanisms exist, allowing cells to control the movement of specific molecules.

*   **Channel Proteins:** Form pores through the membrane, allowing ions to flow down their electrochemical gradients.
*   **Carrier Proteins:** Bind to specific molecules and undergo conformational changes to facilitate their transport across the membrane.
*   **Active Transport – Sodium-Potassium Pump:**  A prime example of active transport, utilizing ATP to move ions against their concentration gradients. ($3Na^+ + 2H_2O → 2H^+ + 3[Na^+]$)

**Example 5:** *Analyze* a scenario where a cell needs to maintain a specific intracellular ion concentration. Discuss the role of both channel proteins and the sodium-potassium pump in achieving this.

Finally, the concept of cell specialization and tissue formation highlighted the importance of cell diversity within multicellular organisms. This knowledge forms the basis for understanding more complex biological systems and is essential for the IB Biology curriculum.
