## Ecology: Interaction and Interdependence (C.C.4)

## Overview

This lesson explores the complex interactions within ecosystems, focusing on population dynamics, community ecology, and the flow of energy and nutrients. Students will learn to analyze data, predict outcomes, and evaluate the impact of environmental changes on biological systems.

## Key Concepts

*   Understanding population growth and limiting factors.
*   Analyzing interactions between species within a community (competition, predation, mutualism, commensalism).
*   Exploring trophic levels and the flow of energy through food chains and food webs.
*   Investigating nutrient cycles (carbon, nitrogen, and water) and their importance for ecosystem stability.
*   Evaluating the impact of human activities on ecological systems.

### 1. Population Ecology – Growth and Regulation

> **Important:** Population ecology studies the changes in population size and density over time.

Population size refers to the total number of individuals in a population, while population density is the number of individuals per unit area.  Understanding these concepts is crucial for predicting how populations will respond to changing environmental conditions.

**1.1 Exponential Growth:**

Exponential growth occurs when resources are unlimited. The equation for exponential growth is:

```
dN/dt = rN
```

Where:
*   `dN/dt` is the rate of population change.
*   `r` is the intrinsic rate of increase (birth rate – death rate).
*   `N` is the population size.

**Example 1:** A population of bacteria starts with 10 individuals and has a doubling time of 20 minutes. Calculate the population size after 1 hour.

*   Time = 60 minutes = 1 hour
*   Number of doubling periods = 1 hour / 20 minutes = 3
*   Population size = Initial population * 2^{number of doubling periods}
*   Population size = 10 * 2^{3} = 10 * 8 = 80

**Example 2:** A newly introduced population of rabbits has an intrinsic rate of increase of 0.2 per year. If the initial population size is 20 rabbits, what will the population size be after 5 years?

*   `r = 0.2`
*   `N0 = 20`
*   Using the exponential growth equation: `N(t) = N0 * e^(rt)`
*   `N(5) = 20 * e^(0.2 * 5) = 20 * e^1 = 20 * 2.718 ≈ 54.36`
*   Therefore, the population size will be approximately 54 rabbits after 5 years. *Note: Rounding to the nearest whole number is appropriate in this context.*

**1.2 Limiting Factors:**

In reality, population growth is rarely exponential due to **limiting factors**. These are factors that restrict population growth, such as:

*   **Density-dependent factors:**  These factors affect populations proportionally to their density (e.g., competition for resources, predation, disease).
*   **Density-independent factors:** These factors affect populations regardless of their density (e.g., natural disasters, extreme weather).

**Example:** A population of deer might experience reduced growth due to increased competition for food as the population density increases. *Exam Tip: Be prepared to describe and explain the impact of different limiting factors.*



### 2. Community Ecology – Interactions Between Species

> **Note:** Community ecology focuses on the interactions between different species within an ecosystem.

**2.1 Types of Species Interactions:**

*   **Competition:**  Occurs when two or more species require the same limited resource. Can be *intraspecific* (within the same species) or *interspecific* (between different species).
*   **Predation:** One species (the predator) kills and eats another species (the prey).
*   **Mutualism:** Both species benefit from the interaction.  (e.g., pollination)
*   **Commensalism:** One species benefits, and the other is neither harmed nor helped. (e.g., barnacles on a whale)

**Example:** A hawk preying on a field mouse is an example of predation.  The relationship between bees and flowers (bees pollinate flowers, flowers provide nectar for bees) is an example of mutualism.

### 3. Trophic Levels and Energy Flow

> **Exam Tip:** Understanding trophic levels is essential for analyzing food webs.

Ecosystems are organized into **trophic levels** based on their feeding relationships:

*   **Producers (Autotrophs):**  Organisms that create their own food (e.g., plants through photosynthesis).
*   **Primary Consumers (Herbivores):**  Organisms that eat producers.
*   **Secondary Consumers (Carnivores/Omnivores):**  Organisms that eat primary consumers.
*   **Tertiary Consumers (Carnivores/Omnivores):**  Organisms that eat secondary consumers.

**3.1 The 10% Rule:**

Only approximately 10% of the energy from one trophic level is transferred to the next. The rest is lost as heat due to metabolic processes.  This explains why food chains are typically short (usually 4-5 trophic levels).

**Example:** A grasshopper (primary consumer) eats plants (producer). The grasshopper converts only 10% of the plant’s energy into its own biomass. The rest is used for movement, growth, and heat.

### 4. Nutrient Cycles

> **Important:** Nutrient cycles describe the movement of essential elements (carbon, nitrogen, water) through ecosystems.

*   **Carbon Cycle:**  Involves the exchange of carbon between the atmosphere, oceans, land, and living organisms.
*   **Nitrogen Cycle:**  A complex cycle involving nitrogen fixation, nitrification, and denitrification – all crucial for plant growth.
*   **Water Cycle:**  Driven by solar energy, it involves evaporation, condensation, and precipitation.

**4.1 Significance of Nutrient Cycles:** These cycles are vital for maintaining ecosystem stability and productivity. Disruptions to these cycles, such as those caused by human activities, can have significant consequences. *Note: HL students would delve deeper into the biochemical processes involved in each cycle.*

### 5. Human Impact on Ecosystems

> **Example:** Deforestation and agricultural runoff are prime examples of human impacts on ecological systems.

Human activities have profound effects on ecosystems, including:

*   **Habitat Destruction:**  Loss of natural habitats due to urbanization, agriculture, and logging.
*   **Pollution:**  Contamination of air, water, and soil.
*   **Climate Change:**  Alteration of global climate patterns due to increased greenhouse gas emissions.
*   **Invasive Species:**  Introduction of non-native species that can disrupt local ecosystems.

### 6. Applying Ecological Principles – Case Study

**Case Study:** Analyze the impact of introducing the Brown Tree Snake to Guam.

*   **Background:** The Brown Tree Snake was accidentally introduced to Guam in the 1940s after World War II.
*   **Impacts:** The snake has decimated native bird populations, caused widespread damage to infrastructure, and altered the island's ecosystem.
*   **Analysis:** Discuss how the snake's lack of natural predators and its ability to exploit a naive ecosystem led to its population explosion and devastating consequences. *Exam Tip: This type of case study often requires students to synthesize information and evaluate the complexities of ecological interactions.*
