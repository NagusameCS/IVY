**Introduction:**

Welcome to the world of physics! Today we will explore the fascinating realm of simple harmonic motion. We'll start with some foundational concepts that help us understand and predict this natural phenomenon. 

**Section 1: What is Simple Harmonic Motion?**

Imagine you're riding a bike – it's smooth, but when you release your pedals, the bike starts to turn in circles. That rotation is just simple harmonic motion! It's a type of energy that has some degree of vibration or oscillation. The key idea here is that you can change the direction of an object’s motion by applying forces (like moving your hands around on the pedals) and then applying back those same force changes as you turn the bike.

**Section 2: Frequency and Amplitude**

Let's take a closer look at frequency and amplitude in simple harmonic motion. In simplest terms, frequency is how often an object or a system vibrates or oscillates about its mean position. It's like the number of waves in your waveboard – the more waves, the faster they travel!

In simple harmonic motion, there's a constant rate at which an object moves, and it has some degree of fluctuation due to this frequency. The amplitude is how much energy the system generates when you change direction or speed. Think about what happens if you jump up and down – that’s just one way we can show the amplitude in simple harmonic motion!

**Section 3: Types of Simple Harmonic Motion**

There are several types of simple harmonic motion, each with its own characteristics. Some common ones include: 

* Simple Harmonic (SHM) motion, where the motion is sinusoidal and has some degree of amplitude.
* Compound Harmonic (CHM), which involves a second SHM motion due to another direction that changes over time.
* Wave-like motion, like light or sound waves in air and water, but not in space – this isn't correct for our lesson as it's not applicable for the real world!

**Section 4: Frequency and Amplitude of Simple Harmonic Motion**

Let’s see how these two properties change when we apply different types of forces to a simple harmonic motion. Imagine a roller coaster, where you raise your foot off the ground (force x mass) for a short time before going over it again (mass x acceleration due to gravity). This is an example of simple harmonic motion with constant frequency and amplitude!

**Section 5: How to Measure and Understand Frequency and Amplitude**

We've discussed these in detail so far. Now, let's look at the difference between these two properties – frequency (the number of cycles per second) and amplitude (how much energy is generated when you change direction or speed).

To understand how frequency affects amplitude, think about it like this: if your motion gets more forceful, the amplitude will increase due to the greater force applied. Conversely, if your motion becomes weaker, the amplitude may decrease because there's less force being applied!
