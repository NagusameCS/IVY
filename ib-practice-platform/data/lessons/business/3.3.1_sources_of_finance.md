## Key Concepts

This lesson will explore the various **sources of finance** available to businesses, analyzing their advantages and disadvantages, and considering factors influencing a company’s financing decisions. We’ll focus on understanding how businesses choose between debt and equity and the implications of these choices for financial performance.

### Introduction to Sources of Finance (3.3.1.1)

> **Important:** Understanding the different options available to a business is crucial for effective financial management.  The choice of funding source significantly impacts a company’s risk profile and future growth potential.

Businesses require finance to fund operations, invest in assets, and undertake expansion. These funds can originate from internal sources (retained earnings) or external sources (loans, equity, etc.). This section will introduce the main categories of financing options.

**Types of Sources of Finance:**

*   **Debt Financing:**  Obtaining funds by borrowing money that must be repaid with interest.
*   **Equity Financing:** Selling ownership stakes in the company in exchange for capital.
*   **Internal Financing:** Utilizing profits generated within the business to fund operations.

**Example 1:**

Consider a small tech startup, “Innovate Solutions.” Innovate Solutions needs $500,000 to develop its new product. They could:

*   Take out a **loan** from a bank.
*   Sell **shares** to investors.
*   Use profits from initial software sales to cover the costs.

## Debt Financing (3.3.1.2)

### Bank Loans

Bank loans represent a significant source of debt financing. These loans are typically secured against assets, reducing the bank’s risk.

**Types of Bank Loans:**

*   **Term Loans:** Loans with a fixed repayment schedule and interest rate.
*   **Overdraft Facilities:** Allow businesses to overspend their current account balance up to a pre-agreed limit.
*   **Asset-Based Loans:** Secured against specific assets (e.g., property, equipment).

**Example 2:**

A retail chain, "Bright Bargains," secures a $2 million term loan from a bank to purchase new store fixtures and expand its inventory. The loan has a 5-year term with a fixed interest rate of 6%.  Bright Bargains’ monthly payments would be calculated as follows:

*   Loan Amount: $2,000,000
*   Interest Rate: 6% per annum (0.5% per month)
*   Loan Term: 5 years (60 months)

Monthly Payment = [ (Loan Amount * Interest Rate) / (1 – (1 + Interest Rate)^(-Loan Term)) ]

Monthly Payment = [ (2,000,000 * 0.005) / (1 – (1 + 0.005)^(-60)) ]
Monthly Payment ≈ $37,874

## Equity Financing (3.3.1.3)

### Share Capital

Issuing **share capital** involves selling ownership stakes in the company. This increases the shareholders' wealth, but also dilutes control.

**Types of Share Capital:**

*   **Ordinary Shares:**  The most common type of share, granting shareholders voting rights.
*   **Preference Shares:**  Holders have priority over ordinary shareholders in terms of dividends and asset distribution.

**Example 3:**

A growing e-commerce business, “Global Goods,” issues 1 million ordinary shares at $5 per share to raise $5 million in capital.  Shareholders now own a portion of the company and have the right to vote on key decisions. The company’s equity financing impacts its **financial ratios** (e.g., Debt-to-Equity ratio) positively, but introduces risk for existing owners.

## Internal Financing (3.3.1.4)

### Retained Earnings

**Retained earnings** represent profits that a company has earned and not distributed as dividends to shareholders. Reinvesting these profits can fuel future growth.

**Advantages of Internal Financing:**

*   **No interest payments:**  Unlike debt financing, retained earnings require no interest payments.
*   **Increased control:**  No new shareholders gain control of the business.

**Disadvantages of Internal Financing:**

*   **Opportunity cost:**  Retained earnings could potentially be used for other profitable investments.
*   **Limited availability:**  Retained earnings may not be sufficient to meet all funding needs.

## Factors Influencing Financing Decisions (3.3.1.5)

Several factors influence a company's choice of financing sources. These include:

*   **Risk Tolerance:**  Businesses with a high risk tolerance may be more willing to take on debt.
*   **Stage of Development:**  Start-ups typically rely on equity financing, while more established businesses can access debt financing more easily.
*   **Market Conditions:**  Interest rates and investor sentiment can significantly impact financing costs and availability.
*   **Control Considerations:**  Issuing shares dilutes control, while debt financing does not.

**Exam Tip:** Command words like "evaluate" and "compare" frequently appear in IB Business exam questions related to sources of finance. You need to be able to critically assess the advantages and disadvantages of different options.

### Funding Methods (3.3.1.6)

Beyond the primary categories, various funding methods exist:

*   **Venture Capital:** Investments from firms specializing in early-stage companies.
*   **Private Equity:** Investments in established companies, often with the goal of restructuring or improving operations.
*   **Crowdfunding:** Raising capital from a large number of individuals, typically through online platforms.
