### Subtopic 1: Defining Leadership and Management

> **Note:** Often confused, leadership and management are distinct but interconnected concepts. Leadership focuses on inspiring and motivating, while management focuses on planning, organizing, and controlling.

Leadership and management are crucial for organizational success. Leaders provide direction and vision, while managers ensure efficient operations and resource allocation. This lesson will explore these concepts within the framework of Human Resource Management (HRM), specifically A.2.1 – Human Resources Policies and Practices.

**Example 1:**
Consider a startup founder (leader) envisioning a disruptive product, versus a marketing manager (manager) overseeing campaign execution based on a pre-defined budget and strategy.  Both roles are vital.

**Example 2:**
A tech company’s CEO (leader) sets a long-term vision of innovation, while the HR Director (manager) implements a training program to develop employees’ technical skills to achieve that vision.

**Exam Tip:** The IB exams frequently require you to differentiate between leadership and management, often asking you to *evaluate* which style is most appropriate in a given scenario.



### Subtopic 2: The HRM Function & Strategic Alignment

> **Important:** HRM isn’t simply about payroll and benefits; it’s a strategic function that directly impacts an organization’s performance.

The HRM function is responsible for attracting, developing, motivating, and retaining employees. More importantly, it must be strategically aligned with the organization's overall goals. This means HRM activities should directly contribute to achieving the company’s mission and objectives.  This aligns with A.2.1.1 - "The role of HRM in strategic alignment".

**Example 1:**
If a company’s strategic goal is to expand into a new international market, HRM might implement a localization strategy – training programs tailored to the specific culture and language of the target market.

### Subtopic 5: Performance Management – Monitoring and Feedback

> **Note:** Performance management is not just about annual reviews; it's a continuous process of setting expectations, providing feedback, and rewarding performance.

Performance management systems are used to monitor employee performance, provide feedback, and identify areas for improvement. This usually involves setting SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound), conducting regular performance appraisals, and implementing reward and recognition programs. *Evaluate* the benefits and drawbacks of different performance appraisal methods.

**Example 1:**
A software development team might use a balanced scorecard approach, measuring performance against technical skills, teamwork, and customer satisfaction.

**Example 2:**
A sales team might receive commission-based incentives, motivating them to achieve sales targets. *Describe* the importance of 360-degree feedback.
