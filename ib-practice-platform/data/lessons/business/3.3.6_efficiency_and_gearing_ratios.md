### Subtopic 1: Introduction to Ratios – The Purpose of Analysis
> **Important:** Ratio analysis is a fundamental tool in financial statement analysis, allowing businesses to compare their performance over time and against competitors. It’s not just about plugging numbers into formulas; it's about understanding what those numbers *mean*.

Ratios provide a standardized way to assess a company’s financial health and operational efficiency. They allow us to move beyond simply looking at raw figures on a balance sheet or income statement. Instead, we can gain insights into areas such as:

*   How efficiently a company is using its assets.
*   How much debt a company has.
*   A company's profitability.
*   A company’s liquidity.

We categorize ratios into two main types: **Efficiency Ratios** and **Gearing Ratios**.

**Example:** A clothing retailer might analyze its inventory turnover ratio to determine how quickly it sells its stock. A large turnover suggests efficient management, while a slow one could signal overstocking or weak demand.

**Worked Example 1:**

**Scenario:** Acme Corp’s balance sheet shows the following:
*   Inventory: $500,000
*   Sales: $2,000,000

Calculate Acme Corp’s Inventory Turnover Ratio.
```
Inventory Turnover Ratio = Cost of Goods Sold / Average Inventory
```
Since we are not provided with Cost of Goods Sold, we need to estimate using sales.  Assuming a 60% gross profit margin:
*   Cost of Goods Sold = $2,000,000 * 0.4 = $800,000
*   Average Inventory = ($500,000 + $800,000) / 2 = $650,000
*   Inventory Turnover Ratio = $800,000 / $650,000 = 1.23
```
Therefore, Acme Corp’s Inventory Turnover Ratio is 1.23.
```

### Subtopic 2: Efficiency Ratios – Measuring Operational Effectiveness

Efficiency ratios measure how effectively a company uses its assets to generate revenue. Here are some key efficiency ratios:

*   **Inventory Turnover Ratio:** Measures how quickly inventory is sold.  A higher ratio generally indicates efficient inventory management.
*   **Accounts Receivable Turnover Ratio:** Measures how quickly a company collects payment from its customers.  A higher ratio suggests efficient credit and collection policies.
*   **Asset Turnover Ratio:** Measures how efficiently a company uses its assets to generate sales.

**Formulae:**

*   Inventory Turnover Ratio = Cost of Goods Sold / Average Inventory
*   Accounts Receivable Turnover Ratio = Net Credit Sales / Average Accounts Receivable
*   Asset Turnover Ratio = Net Sales / Average Total Assets

> **Note:** Average inventory and average accounts receivable are typically calculated as (Beginning Balance + Ending Balance) / 2.

**Example:** A supermarket chain with a high inventory turnover ratio is likely managing its stock effectively, minimizing storage costs and reducing the risk of obsolescence.

**Worked Example 2:**

**Scenario:** Beta Industries has the following data:
*   Net Sales: $10,000,000
*   Average Accounts Receivable: $800,000
*   Average Total Assets: $5,000,000

Calculate Beta Industries’ Accounts Receivable Turnover Ratio and Asset Turnover Ratio.

```
Accounts Receivable Turnover Ratio = $10,000,000 / $800,000 = 12.5
```

```
Asset Turnover Ratio = $10,000,000 / $5,000,000 = 2
```

### Subtopic 3: Gearing Ratios – Assessing Financial Leverage

Gearing ratios measure a company’s financial leverage, indicating the extent to which it uses debt to finance its operations. These ratios are crucial for assessing risk.

*   **Debt-to-Equity Ratio:** Measures the proportion of debt financing relative to equity financing.  A high ratio indicates a greater reliance on debt.
*   **Total Debt-to-Assets Ratio:** Measures the proportion of a company's assets that are financed by debt.
*   **Interest Coverage Ratio:** Measures a company’s ability to pay its interest expense.

**Formulae:**

*   Debt-to-Equity Ratio = Total Debt / Total Equity
*   Total Debt-to-Assets Ratio = Total Debt / Total Assets
*   Interest Coverage Ratio = Earnings Before Interest and Taxes (EBIT) / Interest Expense

> **Exam Tip:** Higher gearing ratios generally indicate greater risk but can also amplify potential returns.

**Example:** A technology company with a high debt-to-equity ratio may be taking on significant risk, but it could also be investing heavily in growth opportunities.

**Worked Example 3:**

**Scenario:** Gamma Ltd has the following data:
*   Total Debt: $1,500,000
*   Total Equity: $3,000,000
*   Earnings Before Interest and Taxes (EBIT): $600,000
*   Interest Expense: $100,000

Calculate Gamma Ltd’s Debt-to-Equity Ratio, Total Debt-to-Assets Ratio, and Interest Coverage Ratio.

```
Debt-to-Equity Ratio = $1,500,000 / $3,000,000 = 0.5
```

```
Total Debt-to-Assets Ratio = $1,500,000 / $4,500,000 = 0.333
```

```
Interest Coverage Ratio = $600,000 / $100,000 = 6
```

### Subtopic 4: Interpretation of Ratios – Context is Key

Ratios should *always* be interpreted in context.  Comparing a company’s ratios to those of its competitors and its own historical performance is vital.  A “good” ratio for one company may be “bad” for another.  Furthermore, industry norms heavily influence interpretation.

For instance, a high inventory turnover ratio is desirable in retail but less so in industries with long production cycles. Similarly, a high debt-to-equity ratio is acceptable for a rapidly growing tech firm but concerning for a mature utility company.

### Subtopic 5: Limitations of Ratio Analysis

> **Note:** Ratio analysis has limitations. It relies on historical data, which may not be indicative of future performance. It also assumes that accounts are prepared accurately, and it doesn't capture qualitative factors (e.g., management quality, brand reputation).

Key limitations include:

*   **Accounting Manipulation:** Ratios can be manipulated by accounting practices (e.g., using different accounting methods).
*   **Industry Differences:** Ratios vary significantly across industries.
*   **Static Snapshot:** Ratios provide a snapshot in time and do not reveal trends.

### Subtopic 6: Applying Ratios to Business Decisions

Ratios are used in a multitude of business decisions, including:

*   **Investment Decisions:** Investors use ratios to assess the financial risk and potential return of a company.
*   **Credit Decisions:** Lenders use ratios to assess a borrower’s ability to repay a loan.
*   **Operational Decisions:** Managers use ratios to monitor operational performance and identify areas for improvement.
*   **Mergers and Acquisitions:** Ratios are used to evaluate the value of a target company.
