## Profitability and Liquidity Ratios

## Overview

This lesson delves into the critical role of **financial ratios** in assessing a company’s performance, specifically focusing on **profitability** and **liquidity**. Students will learn to calculate, interpret, and analyze these ratios to evaluate a business’s ability to generate profits and meet its short-term obligations.

## Key Concepts

*   Understanding the distinction between profitability (how efficiently a company generates profit) and liquidity (a company’s ability to pay its short-term debts).
*   Calculating and interpreting key profitability ratios (e.g., Gross Profit Margin, Net Profit Margin, Return on Assets).
*   Calculating and interpreting key liquidity ratios (e.g., Current Ratio, Quick Ratio).
*   Analyzing how changes in these ratios reflect a company’s performance over time and relative to its competitors.
*   Applying ratio analysis to make informed business decisions – *a crucial skill for the IB Business Management exam*.

### Profitability Ratios

**1. Gross Profit Margin**

> **Definition:** The gross profit margin indicates the percentage of revenue remaining after deducting the cost of goods sold (COGS).  It’s a primary measure of operational efficiency.
>
> **Formula:**  Gross Profit Margin = (Revenue – Cost of Goods Sold) / Revenue

> **Example:** A company has revenue of $1,000,000 and COGS of $600,000.
>
>  Gross Profit Margin = ($1,000,000 – $600,000) / $1,000,000 = 40%
>
> > **Interpretation:**  This means that for every $1 of revenue, the company retains $0.40 after covering the direct costs of producing and selling its goods.
>
> **Worked Example 1:**  ABC Ltd. has revenue of $500,000 and COGS of $300,000.  Calculate the gross profit margin.
>
> Solution: Gross Profit Margin = ($500,000 - $300,000) / $500,000 = 40%
>
> **Worked Example 2:** XYZ Corp. reports a gross profit margin of 35%. If its revenue is $2,000,000, what is its COGS?
>
> Solution:  35% = ($2,000,000 - COGS) / $2,000,000  =>  COGS = $2,000,000 * 0.35 = $700,000

**2. Net Profit Margin**

> **Definition:** The net profit margin represents the percentage of revenue remaining after deducting *all* expenses (COGS, operating expenses, interest, taxes) from revenue. It is the most comprehensive profitability measure.
>
> **Formula:** Net Profit Margin = Net Profit / Revenue

> **Example:** A company has a net profit of $50,000 and revenue of $500,000.
>
> Net Profit Margin = $50,000 / $500,000 = 10%
>
> > **Interpretation:**  This indicates that the company earns $0.10 for every $1 of revenue.
>
> **Worked Example 1:**  DEF Inc. has a net profit of $200,000 and revenue of $1,500,000. Calculate the net profit margin.
>
> Solution: Net Profit Margin = $200,000 / $1,500,000 = 13.33% (approximately)
>
> **Worked Example 2:** A company’s revenue is $1,000,000 and its net profit margin is 5%. What is its net profit?
>
> Solution: Net Profit = $1,000,000 * 0.05 = $50,000

**3. Return on Assets (ROA)**

> **Definition:** ROA measures how effectively a company uses its assets to generate profits. It’s a useful measure for comparing companies of different sizes.
>
> **Formula:** ROA = Net Profit / Total Assets

> **Example:** A company has a net profit of $80,000 and total assets of $800,000.
>
> ROA = $80,000 / $800,000 = 10%
>
> > **Interpretation:**  This means the company generates $0.10 in profit for every $1 of assets.

### Liquidity Ratios

**1. Current Ratio**

> **Definition:** The current ratio assesses a company’s ability to pay its short-term liabilities (debts due within one year) with its current assets (assets that can be converted to cash within one year).
>
> **Formula:** Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities

> **Example:** A company has current assets of $200,000 and current liabilities of $100,000.
>
> Current Ratio = $200,000 / $100,000 = 2
>
> > **Interpretation:**  A current ratio of 2 suggests that the company has $2 of current assets for every $1 of current liabilities, indicating a relatively strong ability to meet its short-term obligations. Generally, a ratio above 1.5 is considered good.

**2. Quick Ratio (Acid-Test Ratio)**

> **Definition:** The quick ratio is a more conservative measure of liquidity than the current ratio, as it excludes inventory – an asset that may not be easily or quickly converted to cash.
>
> **Formula:** Quick Ratio = (Current Assets - Inventory) / Current Liabilities

> **Example:**  Using the previous example, if inventory is $50,000:
>
> Quick Ratio = ($200,000 - $50,000) / $100,000 = 1.5
>
> > **Interpretation:**  A quick ratio of 1.5 indicates the company can cover its current liabilities with *only* its most liquid assets.  A ratio of 1.0 or greater is generally desired.

### Ratio Analysis and Interpretation

> **Important:** Ratios are most useful when analyzed in isolation *and* in comparison to industry benchmarks, competitors, and a company’s historical performance.  A high ratio isn't always good; it depends on the context.
